## Summary

The film 'As Deep as the Grave' features a fully AI-generated performance by the late Val Kilmer, who was originally cast in 2020 but could not film due to his terminal throat cancer. Director Querte Vorhees used AI tools with permission from Kilmer's estate and his children to complete the role of Father Finton, a character deeply tied to Kilmer's Native American heritage and the film's Southwestern setting.

The ethical framework is highlighted as a potential model: Vorhees followed SAG guidelines, compensated the estate, and acted with the family's stated belief that Kilmer wanted to be part of this important story. The film uses Kilmer's actual damaged voice, which fittingly aligns with his character's tuberculosis. This isn't Kilmer's first AI use; he previously supported AI to recreate his voice for 'Top Gun: Maverick.'

Critics, like Raymond Aoyo, argue this 'digital necromancy' creates a hollow facsimile lacking an actor's human choices and agency. However, the video host notes the family's support complicates the ethical critique, leaving the final judgment to the audience and market.

### Microsoft's AI Reorganization

In a major corporate shift, Microsoft is restructuring its AI organization to unify its Copilot efforts. The consumer and commercial Copilot teams are being combined under a new EVP, Jacob Andrew, who will now report directly to CEO Satya Nadella. This resolves the issue of Copilot not having a single owner and aims to reduce customer confusion from multiple versions.

AI CEO Mustafa Suleyman will now focus exclusively on Microsoft's proprietary model training and superintelligence efforts, which have seen little public progress. This move reinforces AI as a critical, structured business unit. The Verge's Tom Warren sees it as an admission that separating consumer and business Copilot experiences had failed.

This reflects a broader industry trend where Google, Meta, and Alibaba have also recently restructured their AI teams to sharpen product focus, similar to OpenAI's removal of 'side quests.'

## Context

The use of AI to digitally resurrect deceased actors is a flashpoint in the ongoing debate about AI's role in creative industries, touching on ethics, consent, labor rights, and the nature of performance. It matters to filmmakers, actors, unions, and audiences navigating a new technological landscape. Simultaneously, Microsoft's restructuring highlights how major tech companies are struggling to organize effectively around generative AI, a foundational shift affecting millions of enterprise and consumer users. Both stories signal AI's move from experimentation to integrated, real-world application.
