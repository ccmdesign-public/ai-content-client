## Key Takeaways

Katie Dill discusses the evolution of design at Stripe in the AI era, focusing on cultural shifts, tooling, and quality standards.

* **Design shifts from pitching to prototyping:** AI tools eliminate the need for lengthy presentations; designers now build working prototypes first to communicate ideas.

* **AI raises the floor, allowing focus on the ceiling:** As baseline quality becomes easier to achieve, the strategic goal is to concentrate effort on creating magical, high-impact '15 out of 10' experiences.

* **Beauty and detail as signals of care:** Stripe intentionally invests in seemingly superfluous details (like interactive dividing lines) to communicate reliability and build user trust in their financial products.

* **Hiring for curiosity and agency:** The ideal designer is proactively curious about AI's potential and demonstrates agency by building solutions, not just identifying problems.

* **Balancing high standards with psychological safety:** Stripe maintains a culture of rigorous critique and last-minute quality holds, but aims to pair this with an environment where taking creative risks is encouraged.

## Summary

### Introduction and Website Design PhilosophyKatie Dill, Head of Design at Stripe, joins Dive Club to discuss the new era of design at the company. She begins by walking through the thoughtful decisions behind the all-new Stripe.com. A key challenge was creating a design that was both fresh and enduring. The team explored millions of visual iterations (like the signature 'waves') over five years to find the right balance. The site uses a 'bento grid' with clean entry points and a page overlay system to help users traverse Stripe's vast product ecosystem without feeling overwhelmed. The philosophy centers on 'show, don't tell'—using imagery to communicate concepts at a glance, paired with straightforward, non-marketing language.

Dill highlights specific details she's proud of, like a non-functional but beautiful 'data viz' animation that subconsciously communicates global scale and Stripe's technical care. Another 'moment of magic' is interactive dividing lines that twist and swirl—a detail that doesn't need to exist but adds humanity. She shares that some imagery originated from a designer's personal hiking photographs, and that AI-generated assets for customer story graphics required intense manual refinement in Photoshop to achieve perfect lighting and realism, underscoring the commitment to quality.

### The Call for a New Aesthetic and AI's Impact on DesignDill discusses Patrick Collison's public 'call for a new aesthetic,' arguing that modern design has become sterile and lacking in humanity. She references a video by The Cultural Tutor comparing ornate historical lampposts to bland modern ones, asking what the 'finely detailed lampposts' of product design look like. Dill believes AI's efficiency gains should free up time not to do more, but to add more care, beauty, and detail. She cites a 1927 Atlantic article, 'Beauty, the New Business Tool,' predicting that in an age of mass production (or AI-generated baseline quality), products demonstrating extra love and care will stand apart.

The practice of design at Stripe is changing in three key ways due to AI: evolving products to partner with AI-using businesses, using AI to make Stripe's own products better, and using AI to accelerate internal product development. A major shift is the move from presentation to prototype. Stripe built an internal tool called **ProtoDash** that allows anyone to rapidly create AI prototypes that look and feel like the Stripe dashboard. This means ideas can be shared as functional links, not decks, speeding up strategy and exploration. Designers are now more empowered to be proactive with big ideas.

### Evolving Roles, Quality Standards, and Collaboration
As tasks and roles evolve, Dill sees designers engaging more in product thinking and problem identification, while PMs and engineers can more easily mock up ideas. This blurs traditional discipline boundaries but expands creative exploration. The strategic goal is to leverage systems for consistency while empowering teams to challenge the status quo when the user experience demands it. She gives the example of the Financial Accounts product, where the initial UI used basic form fields, but the team later redesigned it with direct manipulation and fluid containers to better match the product's 'magical' capability of moving money.

Stripe maintains a intense culture of high standards and feedback. Dill and Patrick Collison have pulled the plug on projects or demanded changes to billboards already installed because the work wasn't great. She believes it's better to have the hard conversation than to ship mediocre work. The feedback culture aims to balance this rigorous critique with psychological safety, allowing designers to take risks. This is critical because Stripe moves people's money; every detail, down to a typo, impacts user trust.

### Hiring and Leadership in the AI EraWhen hiring, Dill looks for curiosity and agency, traits that have taken on new meaning. Curiosity now means a genuine interest in leaning into the AI revolution and understanding how it shapes user experiences. Agency is demonstrated by people who, empowered by new tools, proactively build solutions to problems they see. She gives examples like the designer who built ProtoDash and the content designer who prototyped a better experience for platform businesses.

For leadership, Dill's role has evolved from being intimately involved in projects to building the systems and community where great work happens. A critical part of staying effective is 'walking the store'—using the tools firsthand (like ProtoDash) and spending time with users to understand their reality. She emphasizes that leaders must get their hands dirty with new tools to stay in touch with the modern workflow and to be better equipped to guide their teams through this transformative period.
