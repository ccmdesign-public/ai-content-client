## Key Takeaways

This episode analyzes the seismic shift in global power dynamics following Canadian PM Mark Carney's Davos speech, with international relations scholar Henry Farrell explaining the concept of 'weaponized interdependence' and its consequences.

## Summary

### The Davos Rupture

Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney's speech at the World Economic Forum sent shockwaves through the international community by declaring we are experiencing a 'rupture, not a transition' in the global order. As a quintessential establishment figure—former governor of both the Bank of Canada and the Bank of England—Carney's blunt assessment carried extraordinary weight. He argued that the fiction of a mutually beneficial, rules-based international order is over, and that economic integration has become a source of subordination rather than mutual gain. This represents a fundamental break point, particularly coming from America's geographically and spiritually closest ally.

Carney's analysis builds on the concept of **weaponized interdependence** developed by Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman in their book *Underground Empire: How America Weaponized the World Economy*. The core idea is that over decades of globalization, countries became deeply integrated into American-led systems—financial markets, technology platforms, supply chains—which initially provided benefits but have now become tools of coercion. The United States has discovered it can use these dependencies to punish, extort, and control other nations, fundamentally changing the nature of the bargain.

### The Mechanics of Weaponized Interdependence

The weaponization of interdependence began quietly after 9/11 as the U.S. sought to disrupt terrorist financing networks. The key mechanism is the U.S. dollar's role as the global economy's lingua franca. Since international banks need access to dollars through correspondent relationships with American banks, they become subject to U.S. regulation and pressure. When the U.S. designates an institution as a pariah, most global banks comply to maintain their dollar access.

This power was first used against terrorists and rogue states like North Korea, then dramatically escalated against Iran during the Obama administration. Iranian banks were cut off from the international financial system, forcing the country to resort to barter trade for essentials. Even International Criminal Court officials found themselves unable to use credit cards or Google services when targeted by Trump administration sanctions. These 'boring' infrastructures of daily life—payment systems, communication networks—became instruments of political coercion.

### The Enshittification of American Power

Farrell applies Cory Doctorow's concept of **enshittification**—where platforms initially add value to attract users, then extract value once they're locked in—to American hegemony. Like tech platforms, the U.S.-led international system provided enormous benefits initially: open sea lanes, stable financial systems, collective security. But as countries became dependent, the U.S. began using that leverage more aggressively. This creates a dilemma similar to being locked into Google or Facebook: leaving is painful and costly, but staying means accepting worsening terms.

The Trump administration's approach represents this enshittification in its purest form. Where previous administrations maintained at least the pretense of rules and values, Trump openly treats alliances as tribute systems. His demand for Greenland—backed by threats of tariffs and even force—exemplifies this naked transactional approach. While he eventually backed down when faced with united European opposition, the episode revealed a fundamental shift: the U.S. now explicitly views its power as something to be monetized.

### The Consequences and Responses

Carney warns that hegemons cannot continually monetize their relationships without consequence. Allies will diversify to hedge against uncertainty, buying 'insurance' by building alternative systems and relationships. This is already happening: Carney's Canada has made deals with China on electric vehicles and with Qatar, signaling a willingness to balance between powers rather than remain exclusively dependent on the U.S.

Countries face three possible responses: **hedging** between the U.S. and rising powers like China; **going it alone** by building independent, if inferior, platforms; or developing **deterrence capacity** to make coercion more costly. The more the U.S. acts like a predatory hegemon, the higher the costs of that behavior become as other nations invest in alternatives. This dynamic is strengthening China's geopolitical position almost immeasurably, as countries like Canada calculate that predictable authoritarianism thousands of miles away may be preferable to unpredictable coercion right across the border.

### The Power of the Powerless and Coordination Problems

Carney invoked Václav Havel's essay *The Power of the Powerless*, which described how communist systems persisted through people's participation in rituals they knew to be false—like shopkeepers displaying 'Workers of the World Unite' signs. Similarly, the liberal international order relied on maintaining fictions about rules and values, even as powerful nations exempted themselves. By speaking truth to power, Carney helped create **public recognition** of the rupture, which is essential for collective action.

Power in modern societies depends on **social coordination**—shared understanding of how the world works. The Trump administration wants to recreate the system around itself and its interests, offering incentives to join its coalition while using coercion against holdouts. But Trump's fundamental weakness is his inability to commit to restraining himself in the future, making even collaborators nervous they'll eventually be betrayed. This short-term transactionalism undermines long-term trust and coordination.

### Historical Parallels and Future Trajectories

The discussion turns to Thucydides' famous line—'the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must'—often cited as evidence of inevitable power politics. But Farrell notes Thucydides actually presented this as an example of **Athenian hubris**, a symptom of democratic decay that ultimately led to Athens' defeat and occupation by Sparta. The dialogue represents not natural law but a warning about the consequences of unrestrained power.

We may be witnessing the emergence of what Farrell calls **neo-royalism**—an international system based on clan loyalties and personal relationships to powerful individuals like Trump, rather than state-to-state relations. This represents a 'dark Davos' where power shifts to authoritarian regimes and their connected elites. Meanwhile, domestic resistance in places like Minnesota—where ordinary people protest against administration policies—shows how moral consensus can form against perceived injustice, both internationally and domestically.

The episode concludes with book recommendations that illuminate these dynamics: Mary Bridges' *Dollars and Dominion* on the messy, self-interested construction of American hegemony; Francis Spufford's forthcoming *Nonsuch* on economic systems and crisis; and Cixin Liu's *The Score* on the intimate knowledge and joy that persist beneath oppressive structures.
