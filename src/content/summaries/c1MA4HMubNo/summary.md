## Summary

The speaker directly addresses the question of whether dedicating most of one's time to work constitutes wasting life, especially during perceived 'golden years.' They reject the premise that life quality is measured by novelty, travel, or new hobbies.

Instead, they emphasize that personal fulfillment stems from **living intentionally according to one's own desires**. The speaker states they structure their days to do exactly what they want with the highest possible percentage of their time, alongside people they choose. This intentional design, they argue, is the antithesis of wasting life.

A significant portion of the response tackles external criticism, often from family, about working 'too much.' The speaker frames this critique as a **projection of others' negative associations with work**. They illustrate this with a hypothetical: if their job was playing video games all day, critics would still find fault, proving the issue is the critic's preference for how the speaker *should* live, not an objective measure of a well-lived life.

Finally, the speaker challenges the idea that discovering 'something better' through new experiences is a reliable path to happiness. They reference the concept of **hedonic adaptation**—the tendency to return to a baseline level of happiness—and note that people in simple, unchanging environments can be happier than those constantly seeking novelty. The ultimate metric is whether you are living the life *you* want.
