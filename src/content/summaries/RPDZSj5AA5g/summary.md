## Key Takeaways

This video discusses strategies for self-presentation when visual design skills aren't your primary strength. Key takeaways include:

*   **Minimize surface area**: Reduce your personal website to the bare essentials—your name and a contact button—to avoid showcasing areas where you lack confidence.

*   **Showcase thinking through writing**: If your strength lies in **systems thinking** or **product thinking**, use well-written blog posts to demonstrate your analytical abilities instead of relying on visual craft work.

*   **Writing is a powerful signal**: The presence of writing, even older posts, is a major **green flag** that shows initiative and allows others to explore your thought process directly.

## Summary

The speaker addresses the challenge of selling oneself professionally when strong visual craft skills are not available as a primary asset. The core advice is to strategically focus on your unique strengths and communicate them effectively.

### Strategic Minimalism for Your Website

The first approach is to **reduce your website to the bare minimum**. The goal is to remove any "surface" you are not confident in. The recommended minimum viable portfolio site is simply your name and a clear contact button. This eliminates potential weak points and forces focus on your core message.

### Highlighting Non-Visual Strengths

The strategy shifts if your key strength is analytical rather than visual. For someone whose **archetype** is excellence in **systems thinking** or **product thinking**, the recommendation is to **get some good writing up there**. High-quality written content that explains your approach to problems can be more effective than attempting to showcase mediocre visual work.

### The Enduring Value of Writing

Writing is highlighted as a **huge** tool for self-presentation. When a hiring manager or client visits a website, the presence of blog posts is a significant positive signal.

*   It allows them to quickly **skim read** and understand your perspective on various topics.

*   **Older content is completely fine**—a blog post from 2020 or 2021 is still valuable. The mere act of writing demonstrates dedication and intellectual engagement.

*   The combination of seeing the effort (**a green flag**) and being able to explore the candidate's thinking is particularly crucial for those who **can't rely on craft**.

## Context

This advice is crucial for designers, product managers, and other professionals in tech and creative fields where portfolio presentation is key, but not everyone has standout visual design skills. It reflects a shift toward valuing strategic thinking and communication as much as, or sometimes more than, pure aesthetic execution. This is especially relevant in hiring for roles focused on product strategy, UX, and systems design, where the ability to think clearly and communicate ideas is paramount.
