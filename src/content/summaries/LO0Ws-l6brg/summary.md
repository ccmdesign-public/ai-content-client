## Summary

For years, the AI community has operated under the assumption of a 'jagged frontier'—the idea that AI models excel at some tasks while failing miserably at others. The video argues this jaggedness was never an inherent property of AI intelligence, but rather an artifact of how we've been using it: asking models to solve complex problems in a single turn with no ability to iterate, retry, or accumulate information.

This is changing rapidly with the advent of **agentic harnesses**—organizational structures that wrap around AI agents, allowing them to work like human professionals. These harnesses provide memory, task decomposition, verification loops, and the ability to restart with fresh context. The most compelling evidence comes from **Cursor**, a coding company whose agent system, designed for software engineering, recently solved an unpublished research-grade mathematics problem from Stanford/MIT/Berkeley that it had never seen before, even improving upon the human-written solution.

Cursor's architecture follows a **planner-worker-judge** pattern: a planner explores the problem and creates tasks, workers grind on individual tasks, and a judge determines when to restart with a fresh agent to avoid context window limits. This system, which also built a web browser from scratch and migrated large codebases, demonstrates that the right organizational wrapper allows AI to generalize far beyond its initial training domain.

Critically, **Anthropic, Google DeepMind, OpenAI, and Cursor** have all independently built very similar multi-agent coordination systems for long-horizon work. Their shared pattern is: decompose the work, parallelize execution, verify outputs, and iterate toward completion. This convergence suggests we've discovered a fundamental structure for solving problems at work with AI.

The implication is profound: **AI is becoming 'smooth' for practical work**. The limiting factor is no longer whether AI can do a specific task, but whether a task can be decomposed into verifiable sub-problems. This shifts the essential human skill from execution to **evaluation**—the ability to 'sniff-check' correctness. Professionals in law, marketing, product management, and customer success will need to develop meta-skills for judging agent output, as their value migrates from doing the work to ensuring it's right.

## Context

This matters because the dominant mental model for AI strategy for the past three years has been the 'jagged frontier'—the belief that AI capabilities are uneven and unpredictable. If that model is wrong, and AI performance is smoothing due to better organizational structures (harnesses), then businesses and individuals need to radically rethink how they deploy AI. Knowledge workers across all domains must prepare for a shift where their primary role becomes evaluating and directing AI agents, not performing tasks manually.
