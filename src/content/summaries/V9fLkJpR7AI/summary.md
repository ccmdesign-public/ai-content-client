## Key Takeaways

The video critiques the belief that AI-driven visibility tools provide truthful oversight, arguing they often obscure critical, informal work. Key insights include: • AI creates **fake legibility**—a cheap illusion of total visibility that misleads leaders. • The real value is **illegible work** (quick fixes, back channels) that keeps companies running. • AI should act as a **power pack for small teams**, not a surveillance tool for leadership. • The dropping cost of visibility (via LLMs) is eliminating middle-management roles focused on collation.

## Summary

The video warns that AI-powered dashboards and visibility tools are creating a dangerous trap for companies by promoting **fake legibility**—the belief that leadership can see and understand everything through AI-generated reports. This illusion obscures the **illegible work** that truly drives organizations: the quick fixes, shared intuition, back channels, and tiger teams that handle emergencies when formal processes fail.

Drawing inspiration from Shan Gadeke's essay on legible vs. illegible work, the speaker explains that **legible work** is what appears in Jira, OKRs, and roadmaps—planned, trackable, and explainable. In contrast, **illegible work** is the harsh reality underneath: the favors, intuition, and rapid problem-solving that occur outside formal systems. When a database hits a limit or a top customer is on fire, companies bypass formal processes and rely on trusted individuals to solve the problem—a dynamic that AI does not erase but amplifies.

The problem with amplification is that as AI makes real legibility (reasonable clarity about business operations) cheap, it also makes fake legibility (the illusion of total visibility) even cheaper. Vendors sell this as a **single pane of glass**, but the old cost of legibility—human effort in writing tickets, summaries, and decks—acted as a constraint. Now, AI drops that cost to near zero, leading to cases like Amazon firing middle managers whose primary role was collating information.

Large Language Models (LLMs) can pull a decent state of the world from diverse sources: code diffs, Slack threads, documentation, meeting notes, on-call logs, and tickets. This allows asking an AI, 'What changed in this service last week?' or 'What's blocking the roadmap?' However, this capability risks prioritizing visible, measurable work over the critical, often invisible, work that sustains the organization.

The speaker advocates rethinking AI's role: instead of a tool for leadership visibility, it should serve as a **power pack for small teams**, enabling them to do real work more effectively. This shift acknowledges that the most valuable organizational activities are often those that don't show up in dashboards.

## Context

This video addresses a critical pitfall in the adoption of AI within organizations. As companies increasingly deploy AI for visibility and reporting, there's a risk of mistaking easily measured activities for meaningful work. The discussion is grounded in organizational theory (legible vs. illegible work) and reflects real-world trends, such as the restructuring of middle management roles due to AI automation. This matters for leaders, managers, and teams implementing AI solutions, as it challenges the assumption that more data visibility equates to better decision-making and highlights the enduring importance of human intuition and informal networks.
