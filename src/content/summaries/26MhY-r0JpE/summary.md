## Summary

In this short talk, Ben Callahan identifies a fundamental cultural disconnect that undermines the value of design systems. He notes that leadership often holds an oversimplified view of what a design system should deliver, expecting it to be a comprehensive toolkit covering every conceivable product need.

This expectation directly conflicts with how effective design system teams actually operate. These teams understand that design systems thrive in the **overlap between products**. Their goal is to be surgical, identifying and building only the shared, reusable components that deliver maximum efficiency—covering **just the right things**, not all things.

This nuanced difference in perspective is more than semantic; it creates a **tremendous amount of tension and miscommunication**. When leadership pushes for breadth and teams advocate for strategic focus, it becomes "really hard to be surgical in your prioritization." The resulting friction makes it challenging to demonstrate the system's true value, which lies in streamlining development and ensuring consistency where it matters most.

Callahan's exploration began with this simple insight, which he placed into a Fig Jam file, sparking a deeper dive into the organizational dynamics that can make or break a design system initiative. The core message is that bridging this **value gap** requires aligning on a shared understanding of a design system's purpose: as a strategic enabler for product teams, not a universal asset library.

## Context

Design systems are critical for scaling product development, ensuring consistency, and improving team velocity in modern software companies. However, many initiatives struggle to prove their ROI or gain sustained support. This talk matters to product managers, design system leads, engineering managers, and company executives who invest in or rely on these systems. It highlights that failure is often not technical but cultural—stemming from misaligned expectations about the system's scope and purpose. Addressing this gap is key to unlocking the promised efficiency and quality benefits.
