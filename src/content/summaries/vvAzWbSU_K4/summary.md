## Key Takeaways

The core message is to focus on selling outcomes, not activities. Key insights include:

* **Reframe value from activity to outcome**: Shift client conversations from counting leads to projecting revenue volume to align with their true business goals.

* **Solve the core bottleneck first**: Identify the single biggest constraint on growth and focus all energy on solving that specific problem before expanding services.

* **Keep the main thing the main thing**: Avoid diluting your core successful service with additional offerings unless they can be charged for separately with a clear value proposition.

## Summary

The video features a consultant advising the owner of a healthcare listing service who connects physicians with personal injury attorneys for patient referrals. The business owner's primary bottleneck is that his physician clients want predictable lead counts, but accidents (and thus referrals) are inherently unpredictable by location and volume.

The consultant identifies this as a **framing problem**. Instead of promising a specific number of leads (an activity metric), the service should reframe its promise around **revenue volume** (an outcome metric). For example, promising approximately $20,000 in revenue per month, regardless of whether it comes from two large cases or five smaller ones.

This shift refocuses the conversation on the physician's ultimate goal—revenue—and makes the service's value more consistent and compelling, even when lead counts fluctuate. The consultant emphasizes that branding and full-service marketing are a different business and advises against diluting the core offering unless it's launched as a separate, paid service.

The final advice is to relentlessly focus on solving the primary bottleneck that limits growth before expanding into new states or adding overhead.

## Context

This advice is crucial for service-based businesses, especially B2B companies, where clients often fixate on intermediate metrics (like leads) instead of the final business outcomes (like revenue). It matters because how you frame your value proposition directly impacts client satisfaction, retention, and your ability to scale. Anyone selling a service where deliverables have variable outcomes but clients demand predictability can apply this reframing principle to align expectations and demonstrate clearer value.
