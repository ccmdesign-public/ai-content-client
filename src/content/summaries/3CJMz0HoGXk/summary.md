## Summary

The video debunks common myths about medial elbow pain, clarifying that the preferred diagnosis is **medial elbow tendinopathy**, not 'golfer's elbow' or 'medial epicondylitis'. This shift in terminology is important because it moves the focus away from inflammation and rest, and toward managing mechanical load on the tendons.

The condition is thought to occur when the **intensity, frequency, and volume of tendon loading** (from work, sports, or lifting) exceeds your body's capacity to recover. Therefore, the fundamental goal of rehabilitation is to rebalance this **load-capacity equation**.

### Initial Management: Load Modification

The first step is to **modify aggravating activities** to reduce the load side of the equation. For gym-goers, this may involve:

*   Using lifting straps to reduce grip demand.

*   Choosing alternative exercises for the same muscle groups.

*   Experimenting with different forearm positions (supinated, pronated, neutral).

*   For athletes, reducing playing time, session duration, or intensity while focusing on technique to ensure the elbow isn't handling excessive force.

### Pain Monitoring and Progression

Understanding pain is a fundamental component. Ask two key questions:
1.  Is my pain tolerable *during* the activity?
2.  Is my pain better, worse, or the same *the day after*?
A significant increase in pain the next day indicates you've done too much and need to scale back. This framework applies to exercise, work, or any physical activity.

### Core Rehabilitation Exercises

The primary goal of exercise is to slowly increase the tolerance and capacity of the medial elbow tendons. A foundational program includes three movements, performed 2-3 sets, 3 times per week:

*   **Resisted Wrist Flexion:** A primary action of the medial tendons. Can be done seated with a dumbbell, band, or household object. Perform 8-15 reps. Wrist extension can be added for comprehensiveness.

*   **Pronation/Supination Strengthening:** Hold a weight (dumbbell, hammer) and rotate the forearm. Aim for 8-15 reps. Supination is optional if using a band.

*   **Grip Strength & Endurance:** Options include squeezing a ball periodically, farmer's carries, or plate pinches. Aim for 2-3 sets of 15-45 seconds of total work.

### Optional Exercises for Related Issues

Two optional exercise categories address commonly associated issues:

*   **Shoulder Strengthening:** Recommended if you play overhead sports or have concurrent shoulder issues. Options include sideline external rotations, banded external rotations, and scaption. Perform 2-3 sets of 10-15 reps.

*   **Thoracic Mobility:** Recommended to break up repetitive tasks or if you have neck/upper back stiffness. Options include sideline thoracic rotations and seated rotations. Perform 1-2 sets of 6-8 reps per side.

### Adjunct Treatments and Realistic Expectations

The video notes that treatments like cortisone injections may provide short-term relief but lead to worse long-term outcomes for similar conditions. Surgery is not supported by current research. Low-risk adjuncts like elbow straps can be tried but should not replace a structured rehab plan.

**The process requires patience**, with a minimum expected timeline of 3 months. Success depends on tailoring the plan to individual symptoms, goals, and needs, and consistently applying the principles of load management and progressive strengthening.

Medial elbow pain, often mislabeled 'golfer's elbow', is a common overuse injury affecting not just athletes but also workers in repetitive manual jobs and gym enthusiasts. Traditional advice focused on rest, ice, and anti-inflammatories is outdated.
