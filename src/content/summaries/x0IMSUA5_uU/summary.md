## Summary

The March 27, 2026 episode of The Future Live features host Matt Berman, guest host Hiten Shah, and a special interview with Turing CEO Jonathan Siddharth.

### The Demise of Sora and Intellectual Property Challenges

The episode opens with the major news that OpenAI is completely shutting down its Sora text-to-video social application and pulling API access.

The hosts attribute this sudden closure to a combination of stalled consumer growth, astronomical GPU running costs, and an untenable intellectual property nightmare.

A massive underlying factor appears to be the Walt Disney Company abandoning a planned $1 billion investment in OpenAI that was struck late last year.

The hosts speculate that policing copyrighted characters within non-deterministic AI video generation was impossible, causing the specific licensing partnership to dissolve.

This leaves a major vacuum in the market for AI video generation, though the hosts believe the future lies in approved generation ecosystems controlled directly by IP holders like Disney.

### The Enterprise Pivot and Anthropic's Flywheel

OpenAI is undergoing a significant strategic shift, highlighted by executive Fidji Simo moving from applications to AGI deployment.

This signals a hard pivot away from scattered consumer bets like the Atlas browser and toward enterprise solutions, a move directly inspired by the runaway success of rival Anthropic.

Anthropic has built a highly effective flywheel by focusing intensely on coding models and enterprise sales.

By selling highly capable coding tools to enterprises, Anthropic generates massive real revenue, which it reinvests into training future models.

Crucially, these advanced coding tools also accelerate Anthropic's internal model development, allowing them to ship new features at an unprecedented pace that leaves competitors struggling to keep up.

OpenAI is now attempting to replicate this focus by paring down its product offerings, creating a unified desktop super app, and doubling down on enterprise deployment where the true revenue lies.

### The Paradox of Tech Layoffs and AI Hiring

The hosts tackle the apparent contradiction in the current job market, where legacy tech giants like Block, Oracle, and Meta are executing massive layoffs while OpenAI and Anthropic plan to double their headcounts.

They reference a quote from Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, who argued that imaginative companies use AI to do more with more, while unimaginative leadership simply uses it to trim staff.

The hosts agree that the current wave of layoffs is less about AI automation replacing workers and more about companies trimming the excessive organizational bloat accumulated during the zero-interest-rate policy era.

They argue that true end-to-end AI automation remains far off, and companies still desperately need human oversight at the edges of AI workflows to prompt, verify, and manage complex systems.

### Turing and the Quest for Elite Human Data

Jonathan Siddharth, CEO of the AI infrastructure company Turing, joins the show to discuss the critical bottleneck in AI development: high-quality data.

Siddharth explains that frontier AI labs have already consumed all easily accessible internet data and books, which he refers to as dumb data.

To achieve superintelligence, labs now require highly specific, expert-level data in three core areas: coding, enterprise workflows, and frontier STEM fields.

Siddharth emphasizes that improving a model's coding ability mysteriously improves its reasoning in unrelated scientific domains like physics and biology, making code the ultimate foundation for general intelligence.

Furthermore, Turing models the enterprise sector as a four-dimensional matrix encompassing industry, function, role, and specific human workflows.

Turing acts as a bridge, employing PhDs, financial experts, and doctors to create reinforcement learning environments and verifiers that teach models how to reason through these complex, real-world matrices.

Siddharth also reveals a fascinating initiative called Project Lazarus, where Turing purchases the codebases, Slack histories, and internal documents of failed startups to feed proprietary, real-world business data into next-generation models.

### The AI Quota War

The episode concludes with an analysis of the fierce tactical competition between Anthropic and OpenAI regarding model accessibility.

Anthropic recently announced it would lower the 5-hour session limits for its paid Claude subscribers due to severe GPU constraints, specifically impacting the 7% of power users running token-intensive background jobs.

Within hours of Anthropic's announcement, the OpenAI team aggressively countered by eliminating usage limits entirely on their Codex platform.

The hosts note that this pricing and access war highlights the vastly different cultures of the two labs, with Anthropic remaining hyper-focused on its internal roadmap while OpenAI remains fiercely reactive and competitive in acquiring developer mindshare.

## Context

The artificial intelligence industry in 2026 is undergoing a massive maturation phase, transitioning from flashy consumer experiments to rigorous enterprise deployment. The shutdown of high-profile projects like OpenAI's Sora highlights the hard economic realities of GPU costs and intellectual property disputes, proving that viral consumer technology is not always a viable business model. Simultaneously, a clear winning strategy has emerged: focusing heavily on coding models to create a self-sustaining flywheel of enterprise revenue and accelerated internal research. This shift matters to developers, founders, and corporate leaders who are trying to navigate the rapid evolution of AI tools. As tech incumbents shed jobs to correct pandemic-era bloat, the true value in the AI ecosystem is being generated by those who can provide highly specialized, verifiable human data. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for anyone building AI-integrated products, as the battle for compute resources and expert data will dictate which platforms dominate the market.
