Alex Hormozi shares his unconventional approach to working with his spouse. ## Summary

Alex Hormozi discusses his unique partnership with his wife Leila, revealing that their business collaboration began before their romantic relationship. On their first date, they talked business for four hours, and he immediately tried to recruit her to work for him. She joined his company three weeks later, creating a foundation where their professional partnership developed first.

Their approach to avoiding conflict centers on **domain-specific ownership**. Each has clear areas of responsibility where they make final decisions

- Leila handles talent and recruitment, while Alex focuses on marketing and brand strategy. For major decisions, they talk through issues until reaching mutual agreement, having had only one significant business-related argument in their entire marriage.

That single conflict occurred when Alex wanted to launch a software company while running Gym Launch and Prestige Labs. He steamrolled ahead despite Leila's objections, which proved to be a mistake due to opportunity costs and lack of infrastructure. He acknowledges she was right, but notes she never rubbed it in his face.

They don't try to artificially balance work and personal life. Instead, they communicate directly when they need more "us time" and adjust accordingly. Their relationship maintains traditional elements (she makes dinner nightly) while their business partnership is modern and collaborative. Leila's discipline is highlighted by her 11-year unbroken MyFitnessPal streak, demonstrating the commitment she brings to both partnership and business.

## Context

This topic matters for entrepreneurs, business partners, and couples considering working together. Many struggle with separating professional and personal dynamics when working with a spouse. Hormozi's experience offers an alternative model where the work relationship develops first, domain ownership prevents conflict, and direct communication replaces artificial work-life balance attempts. This challenges conventional wisdom about mixing business and personal relationships.
