## Key Takeaways

The core message is that competition is a positive signal for market viability, not a deterrent. • **Competition validates demand**: A crowded market proves people are willing to pay for solutions. • **Avoid market creation**: Leave creating new industries to VC-backed companies with resources to burn. • **Focus on execution**: Entering a validated market removes market risk, leaving only the more controllable risks of building and execution.

## Summary

Based on building over 14 apps in five years, the speaker's biggest lesson is to specifically target ideas with many existing competitors. The common trap for builders is to get excited when a quick Google search reveals no direct competitors, interpreting this as a massive opportunity. In reality, a lack of competition typically signals that the problem being solved isn't a significant enough pain point for people to pay for a solution.

For solo developers, bootstrappers, or small teams, attempting to create a brand-new market or industry carries extreme risk and has a success rate of less than 1%. This 'market creation' game is better left to well-funded, VC-backed companies that can afford to burn cash educating users and building demand from scratch.

The strategic alternative is to enter an **already validated market**. Here, the existence of paying customers for similar products eliminates **market risk**. The remaining challenges—**execution risk** and **building risk**—are far more controllable. You are competing on your ability to build a better, faster, or more focused solution for a known customer base.

The speaker provides a personal case study: they built a meeting recording app, a space with 'hundreds, if not thousands' of competitors. By executing effectively in this proven market, they were able to scale it to $1,500-$1,700 in monthly recurring revenue, demonstrating that success doesn't require an original idea, but rather effective execution in a space with proven demand.

## Context

This advice is crucial for indie hackers, solo founders, and small startup teams operating with limited resources. It counters the common myth that a great idea must be completely novel. In the current landscape of SaaS and digital products, finding a 'blue ocean' of no competition is increasingly rare and risky. This perspective aligns with lean startup principles, emphasizing validation and de-risking business ventures by focusing on execution in existing markets rather than speculative market creation.
