## Key Takeaways

Theo defends his use of Notion for his business operations, highlighting the gap between personal note-taking tools and collaborative project management platforms. Key points include: • **Notion's collaborative features** (real-time editing, team permissions, shared databases) are essential for his workflow, whereas Obsidian is designed for individual use. • **Obsidian's limitations** include being closed-source, lacking robust sync for team use, and requiring manual Git-based workflows for collaboration, which are impractical for non-developers. • The core value lies in **structured relationships between data** (like calendars, kanban boards, and linked properties), not just the documents themselves, which local file-based tools cannot easily replicate.

## Summary

Theo addresses the constant suggestions from his audience to switch from Notion to Obsidian. He details his team's reliance on Notion for their entire content production pipeline, including a content calendar, task assignments, sponsor management, and video status tracking (e.g., 'two stream', 'recorded'). The system uses relational databases, custom properties, and automated workflows that trigger actions when tasks are moved, which would require building a custom application to replicate in another tool.

He acknowledges Notion's significant drawbacks: it's expensive ($1,000/year), buggy, prone to outages, and has a frustrating user interface. However, he argues that alternatives like Obsidian are not viable replacements. Obsidian is a **local-first, markdown-based note-taking app** focused on individual knowledge management. It is **closed-source** (contrary to common perception) and its sync features are not designed for real-time, multi-user collaboration. Managing a team workflow with Obsidian would necessitate using Git for version control, which is untenable for a mixed team of developers and non-developers.

Theo clarifies that his use case is **collaborative project management**, not simple personal note-taking. The value is in the interconnected system of data, relationships, and automations that Notion provides, despite its instability. He concludes that while he dislikes Notion's problems, the only true alternative would be building a custom internal platform, and he implores viewers to stop recommending Obsidian as a solution for complex team workflows.
