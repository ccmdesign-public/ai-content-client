## Key Takeaways

In this paid media monthly discussion, experts from Exposure Ninja analyze current trends, emphasizing that a rigid approach is no longer viable in a rapidly changing landscape.

*   **ChatGPT Ads are the hot topic** but remain in limited US testing; marketers should register interest and allocate a portion of their **innovation budget (e.g., 20%)** to test new platforms like this.

*   **A 'test-and-learn' methodology is now essential** due to constant changes in tracking, AI-driven campaigns (PMAX, AIAX), and user behavior; treat campaigns like science experiments with clear hypotheses and outcomes.

*   **Meta's Manus AI is a promising integrated tool** within the Ads Manager for auditing and ideation, but like all LLMs, it should be used as an **assistant, not a replacement**, for human strategy and nuance.

*   **For small budgets, foundational setup is critical**: prioritize accurate tracking (Consent Mode, server-side), upload offline conversions to train AI, and consider tight geographic search campaigns over complex PMAX if funds are under £1,000.

*   **Creative is the biggest lever for performance**: use varied assets (10+ images, 5+ videos for PMAX), personalize ad copy with USPs and urgency, and leverage simple UGC or Google's generative video tools if resources are limited.

*   **Analyze competitor campaigns** (like Vitality's) to identify gaps in targeting, messaging, and funnel strategy, and use that insight to create more personalized and effective ads.

## Summary

### Introduction and ChatGPT Ads

The video opens with hosts Dale Davies and Rebecca Pilotton from Exposure Ninja's paid media team. Rebecca immediately identifies **ChatGPT Ads** as the dominant headline in paid media, noting constant client inquiries. She clarifies that the platform is still in a testing phase in the US with select big brands like Sephora. Exposure Ninja has registered its interest to begin testing as soon as it becomes widely available. The discussion highlights the parallel to early Google Ads, which were less prominent and evolved over time. Rebecca expresses excitement for any new PPC innovation, emphasizing the need to be a trailblazer. The conversation turns to budget allocation, with Dale referencing an 80/20 rule (80% on proven channels, 20% for innovation). Rebecca advises that the 'test' budget should be allocated based on specific campaign goals (awareness, leads, sales) and the size of the total budget, suggesting that for smaller pots, it might be best focused on a single new opportunity like ChatGPT Ads.

### The Imperative of Test-and-Learn

The core philosophy of the episode emerges: **test-and-learn is no longer optional**. Rebecca states it's her catchphrase, driven by relentless changes in tracking (Consent Mode), campaign types (PMAX, AIAX), and user behavior. She draws a comparison to the initial criticism of **Performance Max (PMAX)**, which many wrote off early without understanding best practices. The key is managing client expectations and setting up defined tests with predicted outcomes, much like a scientific experiment. The hosts agree that while budgets are tighter, the luxury of prolonged testing is a necessity to adapt and find what works in a new environment where 'the way people search is new.'

### Meta's Manus AI and the Role of LLMs

The discussion shifts to paid social and Meta's new integrated LLM, **Manus AI**. Rebecca notes it's visually identical to ChatGPT but produces different outputs. Its key advantage is direct integration with the Meta Ads Manager, allowing for account audits and pitch creation without data privacy concerns. However, she strongly cautions against using it as a replacement for human expertise. LLMs don't understand brand strategy or nuance. She uses the analogy of self-service checkouts: they need human oversight. The output is only as good as the widely available information it synthesizes, potentially leading to homogenized campaigns if over-relied upon for creative work.

### Meta Ads Surcharge and Budget Strategy for Small Businesses

A practical update notes that Meta Ads are becoming more expensive in Europe due to a new digital services tax, with costs being passed to advertisers (e.g., a 3% surcharge in the UK). The hosts then tackle a common question: what to focus on with a small paid media budget. Rebecca's foundational advice is unwavering:

*   **Tracking is paramount**: Ensure Consent Mode v2 (and soon v3) and server-side tracking are correctly implemented to avoid a 'black hole' in conversion data.

*   **Upload offline conversions**: This is critical for AI campaigns (PMAX, AIAX, Demand Gen) to teach the platform what a good and bad lead looks like, saving budget.

*   **Choose campaign type wisely**: For budgets under £1,000, a tightly targeted search campaign with exact/phrase match may be more effective than waiting for PMAX's 30-90 day learning period.

*   **Understand attribution**: Use Google Analytics 4 (GA4) to see touchpoints and decide if budget should be split across channels (e.g., Google for prospecting, Meta for retargeting).

*   **Retargeting is still possible**: Audiences can now be as small as 100 people, and website engagement data (page views, time on site) can be used to create meaningful remarketing lists even without a large CRM.

### Creative Strategy and Video Production

The conversation explores creative demands, noting that some large brands run hundreds of ad variations. Rebecca recommends at least 10 different visuals per campaign, with PMAX ideally having 20 images and 5 videos. She acknowledges the challenge for businesses without video resources. Solutions include:

*   **User-Generated Content (UGC)**: Simple founder talking-head videos filmed on a smartphone.

*   **Editorial services**: Agencies (like Exposure Ninja's) can edit raw footage into polished ads.

*   **Content creators**: For e-commerce, sending products to creators for filmed reviews.

*   **Google Ads generative tools**: The platform can now create videos from product photos and offers AI voiceovers, providing an in-house option.

### Campaign Deep Dive: Vitality Health Insurance

The hosts analyze a real campaign from **Vitality**, a UK health insurer, using Semrush's advertising research tools. Key observations:

*   **Scale**: Vitality runs over 1,200 ad variations, heavily focused on search.

*   **Strategy Gaps**: Their business insurance ads are triggered by broad/local keywords, suggesting AIAX or broad match use, which requires strong negative keyword lists to avoid wasted spend on top-funnel, low-intent clicks costing ~£6 each.

*   **Ad Copy Critique**: While some ads use good incentives ('cover from £5 per month'), many lack strong USPs and clear pain points in the headlines, burying compelling details (like high claims acceptance rates) in the descriptions.

*   **Full-Funnel Presence**: They use some video and display ads, indicating a remarketing or PMAX strategy, but the paid social creative observed is often vague ('cute' brand animations) with weak calls-to-action.

*   **Competitive Analysis Lesson**: Marketers can use this public data to find targeting gaps (e.g., specific age groups within a broad audience) where a competitor is weak and to create more personalized, high-converting ads.

### Emerging Channels: Netflix and YouTube Ads

The episode concludes with thoughts on newer ad platforms. Rebecca sees potential in **Netflix ads** (including pause ads) for B2C and e-commerce brand awareness, likening them to supporting offline activities. She hasn't seen B2B ads there yet. They briefly mention evolving **YouTube ad capabilities**, such as creators being able to dynamically swap pricing in videos for different regions, highlighting the ongoing innovation in native advertising formats.
