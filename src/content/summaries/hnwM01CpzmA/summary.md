## Key Takeaways

AI fundamentally changes team dynamics by amplifying coordination costs alongside individual productivity. Key insights include:

*   **Team size is the root problem**, not meetings: AI-native companies achieve $2-3M revenue per employee, making the coordination cost of a sixth person catastrophic.

*   **Five-person 'strike teams' are optimal** for maintaining shared context and verifying AI output for correctness, the new scarce resource.

*   **AI is a force multiplier, not just an efficiency tool**: Companies should restructure into small teams to pursue missions 5-10x larger, not just reduce headcount.

*   **Talent evaluation must shift** to scout missions that identify people who can direct AI with architectural judgment, not just follow specs.

## Summary

The proliferation of meetings is a symptom of a deeper problem: team sizes that are fundamentally incompatible with AI's impact on productivity. Research from evolutionary psychology (Dunbar's number), military organization, and software engineering (Brooks' Law) consistently shows the human brain optimally coordinates with about five people for high-context work.

AI breaks traditional team math by increasing per-person output by 5-10x (evidenced by AI-native companies like Midjourney and Anthropic achieving millions in revenue per employee). This dramatically raises the coordination cost of each additional team member. Adding a sixth person to a team where each member generates $2M in value creates a 'coordination catastrophe,' measured in millions in lost productivity.

### The Shift from Volume to Correctness

AI has made volume a cheap commodity. The new scarce resource is **correctness**—whether output is architecturally sound, strategically coherent, and right for the customer. A Harvard Business School study (2025) found AI-augmented teams were three times more likely to produce top-tier ideas, not just more output.

Small, AI-augmented teams win because they maintain a **shared mental model** necessary to verify AI output. In a five-person team, each person can review a manageable volume against coherent context. Larger teams degrade this shared context, leading to more meetings, more AI-generated slop to verify, and a vicious cycle of coordination overhead—a phenomenon called the **'agentic tarpit.'**

### Two New Organizational Archetypes

*   **Scouts**: Solo operators with full AI toolkits for exploration (e.g., Peter Steinberger building OpenClaw with coding agents). They move fast with zero coordination overhead but lack error correction.

*   **Strike Teams**: Five-person units executing where correctness matters. This size covers necessary domains (product, engineering, design) without creating silos or blind spots. Every piece of AI output passes through at least one other brain with shared context for validation.

### Strategic Recomposition, Not Layoffs

Leaders should view their workforce as having acquired a 5-10x capacity multiplier. The goal isn't to do the same with fewer people, but to **recompose teams into strike teams** and pursue vastly more ambitious missions. For example, a 400-engineer SaaS company restructured into 80 strike teams could build a multi-product platform.

This requires a new talent calculus: every team member must have exceptional judgment, as mediocre contributors now impose an **'AI slop tax'** by consuming precious shared attention for verification. Companies like Shopify run mandatory AI prototyping to identify and develop these skills at scale.

## Context

As AI adoption accelerates, companies are misdiagnosing symptoms (endless meetings) and applying wrong solutions (AI note-takers). The core issue is that AI has changed the economics of team coordination by massively increasing individual output, making traditional team structures obsolete. This matters for every leader, manager, and knowledge worker because it redefines organizational design, talent strategy, and competitive advantage in the AI era. Companies that restructure around small, high-judgment teams will outpace those stuck in coordination-heavy models.
