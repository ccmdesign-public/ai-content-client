## Key Takeaways

This video explores a developer's dramatic transition to AI agent-driven development, highlighting key mindset shifts and practical rules.

* **From coding to directing**: Karpasi shifted from writing 80% of code himself to delegating 80% to AI agents, describing his new role as 'expressing my will' through direction.

* **Think in macro actions**: The first rule is to stop writing single functions and start delegating entire features to agents running in parallel.

* **Assume skill issues, not capability limits**: When an agent fails, the problem is likely your prompt, instructions, or orchestration, not the model's inherent capability.

## Summary

The video details a profound personal evolution in software development methodology that occurred in December 2025. Karpasi describes a sudden, non-gradual shift—a 'cliff edge'—where his work composition inverted. He moved from spending 80% of his time writing code himself and 20% delegating to AI agents, to the opposite: 20% personal coding and 80% agent delegation.

He states he hasn't typed a line of code since that pivotal month, spending up to 16 hours a day purely on **directing agents**. He frames this new activity not as traditional management but as **'expressing my will'**, a more intentional and creative form of command. This new paradigm induced a state he terms **'AI psychosis'**, characterized by the overwhelming sense that everything is possible, everything remains unexplored, and that pausing for even a moment means falling irrecoverably behind.

From this intense experience, he has begun codifying rules for effective agent-based development. The first critical rule is a fundamental mindset shift: **'think in macro actions, not lines of code'**. Developers must stop micromanaging by writing individual functions and start delegating **entire features** to agents that can operate in parallel, dramatically scaling output.

The second rule addresses a common point of frustration: **'when it doesn't work, assume it's a skill issue first'**. The premise is that the AI model's capability is almost certainly sufficient for the task. The failure typically lies in the human's input—the **prompt**, the **instructions**, or the **orchestration** of the workflow. This reframes problems as opportunities to improve one's direction skills rather than limitations of the technology.

The video concludes by noting that Karpasi's complete workflow comprises ten such rules, with the most impactful ones to be covered in the next part of the series.
