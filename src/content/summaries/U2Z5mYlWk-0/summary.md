## Key Takeaways

The core message is to build portable, scalable AI agent processes that aren't locked to specific platforms. • **Skills** (markdown files with step-by-step instructions) define repeatable processes any agent can follow • Skills work across major platforms including OpenClaw, Claude, Cursor, and CodeEx • Focus on designing **jobs not tasks** and let the platform choice be determined by which removes the most friction for deploying processes

## Summary

The video addresses a common dilemma in AI development: whether to bet on the OpenClaw or Claude ecosystems, each with their own strengths and gaps. The solution presented is to avoid platform lock-in entirely by focusing on building processes rather than choosing sides.

**Skills** represent the key innovation here. These are mostly markdown files (sometimes with a few scripts and examples) that define repeatable, step-by-step instructions that any AI agent can follow to complete specific jobs. Claude introduced the concept of skills last year, but they've since been adopted across all major platforms, creating a convergence on a single standard.

This standardization is transformative because it means business processes built using skills aren't locked into any one platform. The speaker emphasizes that their "growing library of skills files" is where most of their creative and strategic work happens. When working on skills, they're literally making their agents better at their jobs.

The practical approach shifts from "OpenClaw versus Claude" to asking which platform or combination removes the most friction from deploying processes using skills. The work invested in skills can move with developers as platforms evolve, providing long-term value regardless of which ecosystem gains dominance.

The fundamental principle remains constant: **Design jobs not tasks. Define the process.** This approach ensures scalability and portability regardless of the underlying AI platform or what comes next in the rapidly evolving AI landscape.

## Context

This matters because AI development is currently fragmented between competing ecosystems like OpenClaw and Claude, creating a dilemma for developers and businesses about where to invest their efforts. The skills approach provides a solution to platform lock-in while enabling scalable, repeatable AI processes. Developers, AI engineers, and business leaders building with AI should care about this as it addresses a critical pain point in AI adoption and scaling. This connects to broader trends of standardization in AI tooling and the move toward more portable, interoperable AI workflows.
