## Key Takeaways

Meta's data practices with its smart glasses raise serious privacy concerns. Key revelations include:

- **Private footage exposure**: Videos recorded by Meta glasses, including people in bathrooms, showers, and changing rooms, are being reviewed by humans at a third-party company in Africa

- **Inadequate privacy protections**: While Meta claims to blur faces, workers at the reviewing company report the blurring technology is unreliable and fails frequently

- **Terms of service loopholes**: Meta's terms explicitly state they may review footage either through AI or human reviewers, but users are often unaware recordings are happening

## Summary

Meta is embroiled in a privacy scandal involving its smart glasses with built-in cameras. According to investigative reporting, footage captured by these devices—including highly private moments—is being transmitted to servers and then sent to a third-party company in Africa for human review.

The glasses, which have small cameras on the corners, continuously record video, often without users' full awareness. Meta's terms of service disclose this monitoring practice, stating they may review recordings either through **autonomous AI systems** or via **human reviewers**.

An investigative team traveled to Africa and interviewed employees at the company reviewing the footage. Workers reported seeing:

- People using toilets

- Individuals in showers and changing rooms

- Private financial information including credit card details

The company claims to use **face-blurring technology** before human reviewers see the footage, but employees stated the blurring is inconsistent and often fails to work properly. This gap between stated privacy measures and actual practice has created significant concerns about user privacy and data protection standards.

This situation highlights the tension between **wearable technology** capabilities and **personal privacy rights**, particularly as smart glasses become more prevalent and recording becomes more seamless and continuous.

## Context

This incident matters because it reveals how wearable technology with always-on recording capabilities creates significant privacy risks, even when companies claim to implement safeguards. As smart glasses and similar devices become more common, users need to understand what data is being collected, who can access it, and how it's being used. The case also demonstrates the global nature of data processing, where footage from one country can be reviewed by workers in another with potentially different privacy standards. This has implications for anyone using wearable cameras, smart home devices, or any technology that records without obvious indicators.
