## Key Takeaways

Here are the most important takeaways from Theo's analysis of modern issue tracking:

* **Linear** is pivoting away from traditional issue tracking, reporting that AI **agents** are already installed in over 75% of their enterprise workspaces.

* Traditional **specifications** and heavy planning phases are obsolete; because code is now incredibly cheap to generate, building a rapid **prototype** is the best way to plan a feature.

* Tools like **GStack** that force AI models to roleplay as different human corporate roles unnecessarily replicate legacy human bottlenecks.

* **Jira** and heavily process-driven tracking systems are ill-equipped for the AI era, especially as companies realize that extensive bureaucratic overhead hinders rapid iteration.

## Summary

Theo examines a recent article from the creators of Linear claiming that traditional issue tracking is dead. He reflects on his time as an engineer at Twitch, where bloated systems like Jira severely hampered productivity due to excessive complexity and slow load times.

### The Shift Away From Issue Tracking

Linear initially succeeded by stripping away the bureaucratic weight of Jira and catering directly to engineers rather than product managers. Now, Linear is pivoting again to adapt to the AI era. They argue that the traditional handoff model—where product managers write specs and engineers eventually pick up tickets—was born out of the reality that engineering time was a scarce resource.

With the rise of powerful AI tools, that constraint no longer exists. Linear reports that AI agents are already active in more than 75% of their enterprise workspaces. Furthermore, the volume of work completed by agents has grown 5x in the last three months, largely driven by advanced models and coding environments. Agents are even authoring roughly 25% of all new issues, fundamentally changing how context is turned into execution.

### Code as the Ultimate Planning Tool

Theo strongly advocates for what his former colleagues called the Theo method or the Theo prototype. In the past, teams would spend weeks writing 20-page specifications attempting to predict how a feature would work, often getting it entirely wrong. Instead, Theo would spend one to three days building a functional, scrappy prototype.

Building a prototype first immediately reveals technical shortcomings, edge cases, and user experience flaws. Once the prototype is built, the team can either polish it for shipping or write a highly accurate spec based on real learnings. In the age of AI, this approach is more relevant than ever. Because generating code is practically free, developers should stop wasting time on extensive pre-planning documents.

### The Flaw in Replicating Human Roles

Despite praising Linear's forward-thinking approach, Theo critiques the current trend of forcing AI to adopt legacy human workflows. He specifically points to tools like GStack, which use markdown prompts to make AI models roleplay as different corporate personas, such as a CEO, engineering manager, or senior designer.

Theo argues this is fundamentally flawed. We originally divided software development into these specific roles because individual humans had limited skill sets and bandwidth. If an AI model is smart enough to perform all these functions simultaneously, there is no reason to artificially fracture its workflow into a simulated corporate org chart. Instead of reinventing complex planning phases, the industry should embrace the reality that rapidly generating code is now the most efficient way to plan and iterate.

## Context

The software development lifecycle is undergoing a massive paradigm shift driven by the rapid adoption of AI coding assistants and autonomous agents. Historically, engineering bandwidth was the primary bottleneck for tech companies, necessitating heavy project management tools like Jira and rigid specification processes to prevent wasted effort. However, as AI drastically lowers the cost and time required to write code, these legacy management structures are becoming obsolete overhead. Understanding this shift is critical for engineering leaders, product managers, and developers who must transition from process-heavy planning to rapid, prototype-driven iteration to remain competitive.
