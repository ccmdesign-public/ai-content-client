## Key Takeaways

The video distinguishes between two fundamental types of learning and provides a framework for acquiring skills efficiently.

*   **Distinguish between declarative and procedural knowledge.** **Declarative knowledge** is knowing *that* something exists (e.g., a new business model), while **procedural knowledge** is knowing *how* to do it (e.g., the steps to execute it).

*   **Declarative knowledge unlocks 'unknown unknowns' and is a prerequisite.** Learning what exists (e.g., from high-level networks) reveals opportunities you didn't know were possible and must come before learning how to execute on them.

*   **Use the 'Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate' method for skill acquisition.** To efficiently learn a procedural skill, first have an expert document the process, then demonstrate it live, and finally duplicate it yourself until you can perform it without correction.

## Summary

The video presents a crucial framework for learning by distinguishing between two types of knowledge: declarative and procedural.

**Declarative knowledge** is knowledge *of existence*—learning about things you didn't know existed. This includes business models, tax strategies, or investment avenues like private equity. This type of knowledge is often gained through access to high-level networks and relationships, and it deals with the 'unknown unknowns.' It's a prerequisite for the next step because you can't learn how to do something if you don't know it exists.

**Procedural knowledge** is the *how-to*—knowing the steps to execute on an idea once you're aware of it. This is more predictable and involves clarifying specific skills, like learning to run Facebook ads at a national scale instead of a local one.

The speaker emphasizes that when investing time, trade, or money into learning, you must be clear on which type of knowledge you are buying. Confusing them leads to frustration; you might get excited about a new concept (declarative) but leave without the practical ability (procedural) to implement it.

### A Personal Case Study: Mastering Facebook Ads

The speaker illustrates this with a personal story about scaling his Facebook ad skills. He first gained **declarative knowledge** in 2013 by learning that Facebook ads existed and the basics of how they worked. Years later, needing **procedural knowledge** to run national-scale ads, he hired an expert for $750 per hour.

He used a rigorous three-step method to extract the procedural knowledge efficiently:

*   **Document:** Have the expert outline the entire process as if they were in his shoes.

*   **Demonstrate:** Watch the expert perform the task live, clicking buttons and explaining each step, which he recorded and turned into a checklist.

*   **Duplicate:** Perform the task himself in front of the expert, repeating until he could do it correctly multiple times without correction.

This process took eight focused hours (and the associated cost) to master the advanced skill, demonstrating a high-return investment in targeted procedural learning.

## Context

This framework is critical for entrepreneurs, professionals, and anyone investing in their own growth. In a world saturated with information and learning opportunities, misallocating resources on the wrong type of knowledge is a common pitfall. Understanding whether you need to discover new possibilities (declarative) or build execution muscle (procedural) allows for strategic learning, smarter investments in courses or coaching, and faster skill acquisition. It connects to broader themes of effective self-education and resource optimization in business and career development.
