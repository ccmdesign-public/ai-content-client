## Key Takeaways

Calvin Robertson, a veteran UX leader and entrepreneur, shares his framework for transitioning from individual contributor to effective leader in the tech and design space. He emphasizes that leadership is a service role focused on empowering others, not a personal achievement.

*   **Leadership is not for you—it's for those in your care.** The core purpose is to serve and develop your team, not to seek personal recognition or authority.

*   **True leadership qualities (insight, influence, integrity) are byproducts of foundational skills.** They emerge from intentionally building **trust**, sharing **knowledge**, and delegating **power** to your team members.

*   **Proactively design your transition into leadership.** Don't wait for permission or a perfect role; identify organizational gaps, build strategies, and seek mentors who can groom you for the opportunity.

*   **'Overflowing leadership' creates a positive ripple effect.** When leaders focus on empowering their teams, it fosters environments of psychological safety, recognition, and ownership, leading to higher performance and loyalty.

*   **Handle organizational politics with a long-game, metrics-driven approach.** Shift contentious debates from subjective opinions to objective business outcomes and documented performance data aligned with company goals.

*   **To build confidence, 'borrow' it from mentors and your support system.** Seek coaching, education (like an MBA for business acumen), and environments that actively invest in your growth as a leader.

## Summary

### Introduction and the Leadership Gap in UX

Calvin Robertson opens his SmashingConf talk by highlighting a critical gap in the UX and tech industry: while teams extensively discuss craft and tools, there is a significant lack of conversation about leadership development. A show of hands reveals that most people in the room who are leaders or aspire to be leaders feel unprepared for the role. Robertson frames this as the real challenge in UX—not a lack of technical skill, but a deficit in leadership capability that affects team dynamics and product success. He positions his talk as an intervention to start that essential conversation and provide a practical framework.

### Personal Journey and Defining Moments

Robertson shares his 25-year career journey, which spans full-stack development, enterprise UX, entrepreneurship, and an MBA. He identifies two transformative moments that reshaped his view of leadership. The first was a conversation with a VP in 2012, who reframed his frustration with changing managers as a deliberate learning opportunity to experience diverse leadership styles before stepping into the role himself. This shifted his mindset from passive victim to active strategist.

The second defining moment was being laid off. Instead of seeing it as a setback, he viewed it as a 'setup'—a forced opportunity to pursue his entrepreneurial ideas full-time. Reflecting on having 24 different leaders in 24 years, he analyzed what made the good ones effective. He identified three aspirational qualities they all possessed: **insight** (penetrating discernment), **influence** (shaping behavior without force), and **integrity** (alignment with values). Crucially, he realized these were not the goals they focused on, but the byproducts of deeper, more fundamental work.

### The Three Aspirations as Byproducts

Robertson deconstructs each of the three leadership aspirations, explaining how they flow from core foundational skills, not from direct pursuit.

**Insight** is the capacity to see beneath the surface and understand what truly matters. He illustrates this with the story of his manager, John, who saw Robertson's potential for greater impact in management over a technical architect track. This moment of insight was only possible because of the **trust** and invested relationship they had built over time. Insight emerges when a leader knows their team deeply and prepares them for moments before they happen.

**Influence** is the power to affect others' thoughts and behaviors through presence and conviction, not coercion. He shares how his next boss, Andy, after John's departure, acknowledged his team leadership but was unsure about his management skills. Andy then used his **knowledge** and **power** to groom Robertson, sharing his expertise and authority throughout the hiring and onboarding process. This act of sharing power to build capability is true influence.

**Integrity** is alignment with values, especially when it's hard. Robertson recounts an early-career manager at Hallmark, Mary, whose role was purely his personal development. When he couldn't transition into a creative role, she told him, 'If Hallmark can't see your value, then maybe they don't deserve to keep you.' This was an act of integrity—using her **power** and position for his benefit, not the company's retention metrics, which freed him to make the best choice for his career.

### The Foundation: Trust, Knowledge, and Power

The core of Robertson's framework is what he calls **'overflowing leadership.'** He argues that the leader's primary job is to develop three foundational assets, but not for themselves—for their team.

*   **Build Trust:** Create a safe environment where team members feel acknowledged and empowered to take risks without fear of reprisal. Distrust leads to caution, timidity, and constant second-guessing.

*   **Share Knowledge:** Hoarding information creates bottlenecks; sharing it creates capability. Leaders must actively transfer their knowledge to team members, preparing them for greater responsibility.

*   **Delegate Power:** Authority should be distributed, not centralized. A leader's role is to 'block and tackle'—remove obstacles and provide strategic cover—so the team can execute their best work 'unscathed.'

He contrasts this with the common UX problem of having 'undrainable empathy for end users' but little for internal stakeholders like product managers or legal teams. Applying these 'soft skills' (which he notes are actually very hard) internally can transform adversarial relationships into collaborative partnerships.

### The Ripple Effect and Practical Application

Focusing on trust, knowledge, and power creates a positive ripple effect: team members feel entrusted, seen, and empowered. Neglecting them creates a negative backflow of distrust, feeling unacknowledged, and disempowered. Robertson stresses that **'leadership is not for you.'** It is a service role for the benefit of those you lead. The ultimate proof is when team members choose to follow you to new companies because they trust your leadership.

He advises starting with honest self-conversation and seeking counsel from mentors or coaches. For those lacking confidence, he recommends 'borrowing' it from mentors and seeking environments that support growth, as he did by pursuing an MBA with a supportive boss.

### Q&A: Practical Leadership Challenges

In the extended Q&A session, Robertson and the host delve into specific scenarios. Key insights include:

*   **Transitioning to Leadership:** It requires both a clear business need and a person ready to step in. It shouldn't be forced.

*   **Building Trust with New Hires:** His go-to method is giving everyone a voice. He uses one-on-ones as open forums and creates 'cascading' opportunities for team members to present their work to increasingly larger audiences, building their confidence and visibility.

*   **Handling Office Politics:** Play the long game. Don't try to instantly fix deep cultural issues. Instead, shift debates from subjective opinions to objective **metrics** and business outcomes. Document disagreements and tie design decisions to agreed-upon company performance goals.

*   **The Manager-Designer Balance:** He strongly advises that upon becoming a people manager, you must 'hang up your tools' (like Figma) to focus fully on strategizing and supporting your team. There isn't enough time to do both well once the team grows beyond a handful of people.

*   **Recommended Reading:** He cites *The Other Side of Innovation* as a key book for understanding the process and rigor needed to build environments where innovation can thrive.
