## Key Takeaways

Theo, a professional content creator and long-time Apple user, delivers a passionate critique of Apple's decline across software, policy, and culture, while explaining why he remains locked into their ecosystem for professional work.

*   **Software quality has severely declined** in macOS and iOS, with glaring UI inconsistencies (like mismatched window corners in macOS 26), regressive app design (the iOS Photos app), and long-unfixed bugs (Mail search, Apple Pay UI) that waste millions of hours of user time.

*   **Apple's policy is driven by entitlement**, exemplified by the 30% App Store fee that disproportionately harms small developers while billion-dollar companies like banks pay almost nothing, and a corporate culture that punishes employee agency and transparency.

*   **The company suffers from willful ignorance**, insulating itself from superior third-party software (like VS Code, Raycast) and competitor advancements in AI, leading to poor products like Xcode and the decision to use an inferior AI model (Google's) over Anthropic's for cost reasons.

*   **Despite these failures, professionals are locked in** because Apple's hardware (M-series chips, video pipeline) and specific creative software (Final Cut Pro) remain objectively superior for high-end video and graphics work, with Android and Linux offering non-viable alternatives for these use cases.

## Summary

### Introduction and Personal History

Theo opens by framing himself as a former Apple "hater" turned "fanboy" who is now deeply frustrated with the company's direction. He structures his rant into three core categories: software, policy, and ignorance. The immediate catalyst is his 16-inch MacBook Pro being forcibly updated to macOS 26 against his will, an experience that highlights a broader loss of user control and quality.

### Software Critique: A Cascade of Failures

Theo dedicates the largest portion of the video to enumerating Apple's software failures. He contrasts the cohesive, high-quality overhaul of iOS 7 with the sloppy, inconsistent rollout of macOS/iOS 26, where even core apps like **Finder** have mismatched corner radii on the same screen. He details specific regressions:

*   **iOS Photos App**: The intuitive, gesture-based interface from iOS 18 was replaced with a cumbersome tab system due to user complaints, making a common task (accessing a recent screenshot) require three touches instead of one.

*   **Unfixed Systemic Bugs**: He references the website **bugsappleloves.com**, which quantifies the human time wasted by persistent Apple bugs. Key examples include:

*   **Mail Search**: Simply does not work reliably.

*   **Autocorrect**: Ignores user corrections, a flaw a third-party app (**Whisper Flow**) solves elegantly.

*   **Apple Pay UI**: A disastrous redesign where tapping the card graphic changes the shipping address, while a text link below changes the payment method—a violation of basic UI principles that has persisted for years.

*   **Google Contact Sync**: Adding a Gmail account silently redirects contact storage to Google, causing users to lose contacts during phone upgrades.

*   **AirDrop**: Unreliable, slow with large files, and has a dangerous UI bug where the recipient list can shift as you click, risking sending sensitive data to the wrong person.

*   **Core OS Bugs**: **macOS 26 window resizing** has an unusably small hit area in corners, and **dynamic list reordering** (e.g., in AirPlay menus) can cause misclicks, which Theo calls a "fireable offense."

He notes his own workflow relies almost entirely on third-party replacements: **Zen Browser**, **Helium**, **Cursor**, **Obsidian**, **Ghosty** terminal, **Raycast** (replacing Spotlight), and **Superhuman** (replacing Mail).

### Policy Critique: Entitlement and Exploitation

Theo argues Apple's internal and external policies are eroding the company. Internally, a culture of secrecy and lack of employee agency drives away talented people who care, as evidenced by an Xcode manager who used Theo's videos to understand external developer tools because his team was so insulated. Externally, Apple feels entitled to developer revenue and user loyalty.

*   **The 30% App Store Fee**: He condemns this as "horrifying" and "absurd," highlighting the imbalance where indie developers subsidize the costs of billion-dollar companies like Bank of America that pay only a $100/year developer fee. He details the ongoing battle with **Patreon**, where Apple is forcing the platform to use Apple's payment system (and give Apple 30%) or be banned from the App Store, effectively taking more money from creators than Patreon itself does.

*   **Cost-Cutting Over Quality**: He reveals that Apple internally uses **Anthropic**'s models but chose **Google**'s inferior model for Apple Intelligence to save money, a decision he claims Steve Jobs would never have made.

### Ignorance Critique: The Insular Bubble

Apple's historic strength—ignoring bad external ideas—has become a fatal weakness now that external software is often better. Theo states that teams working on core software like **Xcode** do not use superior tools like **VS Code**, making it impossible for them to build competitive products. This ignorance extends to AI, where competitors like **Claude Code** are far ahead of anything Apple has shipped.

### The Lock-In: Why He (and Professionals) Can't Leave

Despite the torrent of criticism, Theo explains he is functionally locked into Apple's ecosystem due to professional necessities where alternatives are non-viable.

*   **Android**: He tried a **Pixel Fold** but found the overall app quality and, critically, the **video capture pipeline** to be "garbage" compared to the iPhone's professional-grade video capabilities, which are essential for his work.

*   **Linux**: While good for servers, it is unusable for professional creative work due to lack of software (Affinity Photo, Final Cut Pro) and insurmountable hardware/driver issues, like the industry-wide impossibility of proper **HDMI 2.1** support for HDR displays.

*   **Hardware Dominance**: He dismantles claims that Intel has caught up to Apple Silicon, showing that a mid-tier **M4 Pro** chip outperforms Intel's latest flagship in multi-core benchmarks, and Apple's chips still lead in single-core performance and power efficiency.

He concludes that for creative professionals needing high-performance, portable hardware with specific software, Apple remains the only option, but issues a warning: the software quality gap has closed, and AI makes it easier for competitors to build compelling alternatives. His continued use is a pragmatic prison, not fandom.
