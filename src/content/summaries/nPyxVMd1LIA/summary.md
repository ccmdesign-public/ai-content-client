## Key Takeaways

Kris Puckett, a design leader at Stripe, demonstrates how AI tools are fundamentally changing creative workflows and career trajectories for designers.

*   **'I don't know' is a superpower:** Starting conversations with AI by admitting ignorance is the most effective way to unlock its potential as a co-creator and guide through complex tasks like API integration or iOS development.

*   **Permissionless building unlocks creativity:** AI tools like Claude Code allow designers to bypass traditional gatekeepers and technical barriers, enabling them to directly build and ship personal projects (like his book-tracking app Epilog) without needing extensive coding knowledge.

*   **AI-native design is about soul and personalization:** In an era of AI-generated sameness, the new competitive edge and hiring differentiator will be **soul**—creating deeply personal, expressive work that reflects individual identity, much like the custom MySpace pages of the past.

*   **Invest in repeatable patterns with skills and loops:** To scale AI's impact, designers should identify recurring tasks and codify them into **Claude Code skills** (instruction sets) or **loops** (automated prompts), offloading mental load for both personal productivity and team enablement.

*   **The hiring bar has shifted to curiosity and AI engagement:** Hiring managers now prioritize candidates who demonstrate active exploration with AI tools; a generic portfolio template can be a liability, while a custom, AI-assisted personal site shows engagement with the future of design.

## Summary

### From Dream to Reality: Building Epilog with Claude Code

Kris Puckett, a design leader with experience at Dropbox, Mercury, and Stripe, had a 20-year dream of building his own software but was repeatedly blocked by the complexity of learning to code. Traditional resources and courses never clicked for him. This changed dramatically with the advent of AI coding assistants, specifically Claude Code. He used it to build **Epilog**, a personal iOS app for tracking books, questions, and notes. The app features advanced integrations like the Google Books API and custom Metal shaders for animated book covers—technologies he had no prior experience with.

Puckett's breakthrough was realizing that **'I don't know'** is the most powerful prompt. Instead of needing perfect specifications, he could start a conversation with Claude Code by describing his goal and admitting his ignorance. The AI would then act as a co-conspirator, walking him through steps like creating API keys, securing them, and implementing complex UI elements. This created a powerful feedback loop of momentum, where seeing quick, tangible progress fueled further experimentation.

### The AI-Native Designer's Toolkit: Skills, Loops, and Personal Systems

Puckett's practice extends beyond building apps into systematizing his workflow and personal life using AI. He emphasizes creating **Claude Code skills**—modular, reusable instruction sets that teach the AI how to perform specific, repeatable tasks. For example, he created a 'Liquid Glass' skill to implement iOS's specific UI effects correctly after struggling with poorly documented Apple developer resources.

He also uses **loops**—scheduled, automated prompts—to handle recurring personal tasks. A key example is his setup with **OpenClaw** (a local Claude instance) and a custom iOS app that syncs his Apple Health data across different iCloud accounts. He brain-dumps ideas on walks, has his 'Shadowfax' assistant (his name for the OpenClaw instance) create Linear tickets from the transcription, and then has his local Claude Code instance work through the backlog. This system acts as an ambient, personal assistant that manages his health goals, side projects, and task lists.

### Redefining Design Value: Soul, Curiosity, and Career Positioning

A major theme is the evolving value proposition for designers. Puckett argues that as AI makes quality and craft more accessible, the new differentiator will be **soul**—the deeply human, personal touch in work. He sees a longing for the expressive, custom webpages of the MySpace era and believes portfolios and products that feel uniquely personal will stand out.

This directly impacts careers. He observes that hiring managers consistently cite **curiosity** as the top trait they seek, and in today's market, that manifests as active engagement with AI tools. A designer using a stock Squarespace template is at a disadvantage compared to one who used Claude Code to build a custom, expressive portfolio site with interactive elements like a WebGL shader that reflects their personality. AI-native design isn't just about making shaders; it's about leveraging these tools to solve problems at a systems level, demonstrate initiative, and show an understanding of where the industry is heading.

### Philosophy and Caution: Intentionality Over Autonomy

Despite his deep integration, Puckett offers a note of caution. He avoids fully autonomous agents and the 'dangerously skip permissions' mode in Claude Code, preferring to stay in the loop to ensure the output aligns with his intent and feels authentically his. He warns against the 'barrenness of a busy life'—using AI just to do more, faster, which can lead to generic, soulless output. The goal should be focused augmentation: using AI to remove friction and handle tasks you're not good at (like spreadsheet creation), freeing you to focus on high-value, creative work you're proud to put your name on.
