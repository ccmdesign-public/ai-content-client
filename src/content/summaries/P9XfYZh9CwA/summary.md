## Key Takeaways

Every's team discusses the launch of Proof, their open-source collaborative editor built for the age of AI agents. The tool represents a shift in how teams work with AI-generated content and collaborate across human-agent boundaries.

## Summary

### The Evolution of Proof from Provenance Tool to Collaborative Editor

The video documents the journey of Proof, a tool initially conceived as a Mac application focused on **provenance**—tracking what was human-written versus AI-generated. Dan Shipper, Every's CEO who built the tool as a side project, explains that the original version featured a color-coded gutter (purple for AI, green for human) to visually distinguish authorship. This was motivated by internal needs at Every, where team members like Austin questioned whether comprehensive plans generated by AI agents truly reflected human oversight and endorsement.

However, the team discovered that while provenance was interesting, the **killer feature** was real-time collaboration. The tool's adoption skyrocketed when it transitioned from a desktop app to a **live collaborative web-based document** that both humans and agents could inhabit simultaneously. This shift addressed a core workflow need: after an agent creates a plan or draft, humans need to review it, leave comments, and make edits, and they often want other team members—and their agents—to do the same.

### Core Philosophy: Agent-Native Design and the Shift in Writing

A central theme is the concept of **agent-native** design. Proof is built with the principle that AI agents are first-class users. The team discusses different approaches: internal agents (built into the product) versus external agents (like user-owned 'claws') that can connect to the product. Dan found that the most powerful mode is enabling external, context-rich personal agents to collaborate within documents, acting as co-authors.

This leads to a fundamental shift in the nature of writing and document work. The team observes that **text is now cheap to produce** but remains highly valuable. This changes how we design tools for working with text. Proof optimizes for a mode where agents do the bulk of the writing—generating plans, outlines, and drafts—while humans act more as editors or 'gods' coming down to make precise, high-value contributions.

### Practical Use Cases and Workflow Integration

The team shares diverse, concrete examples of how they use Proof internally:

*   **Creative Writing (Austin):** For his weekly food newsletter, Austin uses a running Proof doc to capture thoughts texted to his agent after meals. The agent generates an outline, which Austin then writes into, appreciating the clear visual distinction between his contributions and the AI's.

*   **Technical Collaboration & PRs (Brandon):** He describes a meta-use case where he used Codex to write a Proof doc proposing a dashboard feature. He then shared it with Dan's agent (R2) for feedback, incorporated that feedback via his agent, and ultimately submitted a PR—all without knowing how to code, demonstrating a new collaborative loop between humans and multiple agents.

*   **Daily Planning & Async Work (Dan):** Dan uses Proof for his daily to-do list, pinning the doc in Slack. He adds items throughout the day and has his agent reorganize or carry over unfinished tasks, creating a dynamic, agent-augmented planning system.

*   **Brainstorming & Sharing (Kieran):** He uses Proof as a 'sketch pad' for ideas, especially with the Compound Engineering plugin, which can automatically share brainstorm outputs to a Proof document for easy review and iteration.

### Design Principles: Lightweight, Frictionless, and Open

A key to Proof's success is its intentional **lightweight and frictionless** design. There is no login required to start a document. Sharing with an agent is as simple as copying a 'add agent' link and pasting it into a chat with your AI assistant. This low barrier to entry drove immediate internal adoption.

The team debates the need for features like a document dashboard or inbox. Kieran argues that the lack of organization is a feature, not a bug—it keeps the tool feeling like a transient sketchpad rather than another system to manage. Austin counters that for certain documents, a 'promotion' workflow to move a Proof doc into a more permanent system like Notion would be valuable.

Finally, Proof is **open-source** and free to use at proofededitor.ai. The team hopes it serves as a building block for other developers creating agent-native applications and as a practical example of how to architect collaborative tools for this new paradigm.
