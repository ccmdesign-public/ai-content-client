## Key Takeaways

Andy Welfle, a content designer and co-author of 'Writing is Designing,' explains why language is fundamental to user experience design and how to approach it systematically.

*   **Writing is designing** – Interface language is a core material of design, as integral as pixels or layout, and must be considered at every stage of the design process.

*   **Treat language as a design system** – Apply a systems-thinking approach with **foundations** (voice, tone, grammar), **elements** (terms, actions, names), and **components** (error messages, content patterns).

*   **UX writing must be usable, useful, and responsible** – Writing should help users accomplish tasks, fulfill their actual needs, and be inclusive, considering diverse users and potential stress cases.

*   **Use AI pragmatically as a tool, not a co-worker** – LLMs can help with iteration, shortening/lengthening strings, and applying style rules, but they lack strategic context, taste, and stakeholder alignment skills, so human oversight is critical.

*   **Prototype and test language early** – Use simple tools like spreadsheets or Keynote to prototype language flows, and employ user research methods like card sorting and usability testing to validate terminology and comprehension.

## Summary

### Introduction and Core Philosophy

Andy Welfle, a content designer at AI startup Glean and co-author of the book *Writing is Designing*, opens his talk by establishing the central thesis: writing is not a decorative afterthought but a fundamental act of design. He illustrates this by showing familiar interfaces like DoorDash and Notion with all the words removed, demonstrating they become unusable or incomprehensible. Language is the material that solves user problems, just as pixels or color are materials for visual designers. He critiques the common scenario where a designer asks, "What should this button say?" as an example of treating writing as a last-minute addition, arguing that effective language requires understanding the full context—user entry/exit points, subsequent actions, inverse actions, localization, and brand voice.

### A Systems Approach to Language Design

Welfle proposes modeling content design after visual design systems to scale and systematize the work. He breaks this down into three layers. The **foundations** layer includes voice, tone, grammar, and punctuation, exemplified by comparing the conversational greetings of Quip versus the businesslike tone of ChatGPT, or the fun brand of Lyft ("Howdy Andy, where are you going?") versus the serious Uber ("Where to?"). He warns against voice gone overboard, sharing a confusing Caviar email with a bizarre narrative and the call-to-action "eat a salad." Foundational elements also include style guides, like Shopify's Polaris, which establish rules for capitalization and formatting.

The **elements** layer consists of basic building blocks: terms, actions, and object names. He contrasts the massive, historical taxonomy of Adobe Photoshop menus with the simple, clear navigation of a platform like Gumroad. He highlights the importance of managed terminology, showing Adobe Spectrum's public "in-product word list" which guides teams on whether to use "app" or "application." The **components** layer involves reusable content patterns and microcopy. Error messages are a prime example, and Welfle shares a framework from Adobe Spectrum: explain what happened, the cause, and how to fix it. He also points to Reddit's consistent content patterns for explaining why a post is recommended (e.g., "Because you've shown interest in this community") as systemic thinking in action.

### Core Principles: Usable, Useful, Responsible

Welfle outlines three principles for effective UX writing. First, writing must be **usable**, meaning users can actually do things with it. He shares an example from the Adobe Creative Cloud app where changing a text link from "Tap on the search icon..." to a tappable "Search" hyperlink reduced instruction length by almost two-thirds. Conversely, he shows an unusable example from a Bay Area transit card reloading machine with the baffling option "accept e-perse upgrade," which simply added $20.

Second, writing must be **useful**, meaning it does what users want. He contrasts a convoluted hotel loyalty program sign-up form (with confusing GDPR opt-ins) with Pinterest's terms of service, which includes a "more simply put" plain-language summary alongside the legal text. Third, writing must be **responsible** and inclusive. He references Kat Holmes's book *Mismatch* on inclusive design, noting that designers often use their own abilities as a baseline. He shares a resilient-use example: Lyft drivers who were not confident English speakers used the app's "deaf or hard of hearing driver" notification feature to politely signal they preferred text communication, repurposing a tool not designed for them.

### Pragmatic Use of AI in UX Writing

As an "AI skeptic" who uses it daily, Welfle advocates for a pragmatic, tool-based approach. He warns that AI can hallucinate and produce nonsense, as seen in a weather app alert that read "avoid this subject event as per instruction." His rule is that any AI output should be reviewed by a human before reaching users. He demonstrates his personal workflow using Claude's "Projects" feature, where he uploads his company's error message guidelines, voice and tone principles, and terminology lists to provide context. He shows how he feeds a rough string ("Use AI to deterministically generate results based off of your great query") into this configured AI, which then critiques the jargon and suggests clearer, on-brand alternatives. He notes AI is good at shortening/lengthening strings, following grammar rules, and bulk iteration, but bad at writing from scratch with no context, capturing nuanced voice, and strategic decision-making. He concludes firmly that **AI is a tool, not a co-worker**, and professionals should use it to speed up process, not outsource their thinking, taste, or strategy.

### Q&A and Future Outlook

The Q&A session touched on cultural differences in AI adoption, the evolving role of UX writers, and integrating content design early. Welfle noted that in some regions like Germany, AI-generated content carries a strong negative stigma. On the future of the role, he predicts teams may shrink and the discipline may shift back towards broader content strategy, focusing on systems, metadata, and how content is ingested by both search engines and LLMs. For getting a seat at the table, he advises being "shameless" in inviting yourself to meetings and being a useful team member by helping with PRDs or slide decks, not just interface copy. When asked about designing for voice, he emphasized creating practical tone profiles (e.g., motivational for onboarding, reassuring for errors) with concrete writing tips, not just abstract character concepts. On the future, he is cautiously optimistic but acknowledges a tougher job market, suggesting the core skills of strategy and systems thinking will become even more vital as AI handles more execution.

This talk was delivered at the Smashing Conference in New York in 2025, a front-end development and design event, indicating a push to educate technical audiences on the importance of content design. The talk is highly relevant as product teams are under increasing pressure to integrate AI into their workflows, creating both opportunities and risks for content quality and strategic thinking.
