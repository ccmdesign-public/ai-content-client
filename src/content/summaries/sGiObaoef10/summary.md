## Key Takeaways

Alex Hormozi advises a real estate education founder on scaling by addressing customer churn after graduation. • **The 'More or Better' Decision:** Choose between scaling volume (more events) or building value (recurring revenue) based on your goal. • **Education's Graduation Problem:** Successful education businesses naturally graduate customers, creating a churn point that must be addressed. • **Strategic Downselling:** Convert one-time $12k mentorship graduates into a $3k-$5k annual membership for ongoing network/vendor access, turning the high-ticket sale into customer acquisition cost (CAC) and the membership into pure margin.

## Summary

Henry runs a Spanish-language real estate education business teaching fix-and-flip strategies, generating $1.3M in its second year with a goal of $3M. His model involves two large annual events in Miami with 600 attendees, where he upsells a $12,000 year-long mentorship, converting about 20% of the room. The core problem is that 80% of attendees and 100% of mentorship graduates have nothing else to buy, leading to complete churn.

Alex Hormozi frames this as the **'productiz stage'** issue, where customer success isn't connected to the next product. He presents two clear paths based on Henry's honest goal of simply making more money, not necessarily building a sellable company.

The **simplest growth path** is to run the same playbook more times: double the events by expanding geographically (e.g., East Coast, West Coast, Texas) to attract new customers. This doubles revenue with minimal operational risk.

The **value-building path** involves creating **revenue retention**. Hormozi suggests **downselling** the $12k mentorship graduates into a cheaper annual membership ($3k-$5k) for continued access to Henry's network, vendors, and events. This reframes the business model: the $12k sale covers customer acquisition cost (CAC), and the recurring membership becomes high-margin, predictable revenue that's low-effort to maintain.

The key insight is that **education businesses graduate customers**. The solution is to create 'more education'—a continuity model—or to accept that reality and focus relentlessly on acquiring new students. The chosen strategy depends entirely on whether the founder's goal is immediate cash flow or long-term business value.

## Context

This video is critical for founders, especially in the education and coaching space, who face the 'graduation problem'—clients succeed and leave, creating a constant need for new customer acquisition. It highlights a fundamental strategic crossroads in scaling a service business: choosing between linear growth (doing more of the same) and building equity (creating recurring value). The discussion connects to broader trends in business model design, customer lifetime value optimization, and the importance of aligning operational strategy with true founder goals, not aspirational ones.
