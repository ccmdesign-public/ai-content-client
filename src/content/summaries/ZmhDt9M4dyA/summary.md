## Key Takeaways

This video argues that drinking has hidden costs that extend far beyond the night itself, and that strategic lifestyle choices compound advantages for long-term success.

*   **Drinking costs you two days minimum**—the day you drink and the day you recover, plus potential fallout from poor decisions made while intoxicated.

*   **Strategic abstinence is a competitive advantage**—the speaker stopped drinking for 2+ years during intense scaling phases of his businesses (Gym Launch, Acquisition.com) to maximize focus and performance.

*   **Humble yourself to learn from others**—even if you have more raw processing power, experienced people possess valuable knowledge; choosing to be humble prevents the world from humbling you.

## Summary

The core argument is that alcohol consumption carries a significant, often overlooked time and performance tax. It's not just the night of drinking, but the following day lost to recovery, and sometimes weeks dealing with the consequences of poor decisions. This makes it a major disadvantage for anyone focused on high achievement.

### Strategic Abstinence for Scaling

The speaker shares his personal strategy of completely abstaining from alcohol during intense periods of business growth. He stopped drinking for two years while scaling Gym Launch from $0 to $4.4 million per month, and has adopted the same policy while scaling Acquisition.com. He frames this not as a permanent lifestyle, but as a temporary, strategic advantage—removing a variable that clearly does not help achieve ambitious goals. The logic is simple: argue that drinking *increases* your chances of hitting major targets. It's a tough argument to make.

### The Power of Humility and Reference Groups

The discussion expands into the mindset required for sustained growth. A key insight is the distinction between **processing power** and **knowledge**. A younger person may have faster cognitive processing, but an older, more experienced individual possesses crucial applied knowledge. Therefore, humbling oneself to learn from those further ahead is critical. The speaker references a Bible verse (Matthew 23:12): "Those who are humble will be exalted, and whoever exalts himself will be humbled."

He warns against the short-term ego boost of self-praise, which makes you look insecure to others. True respect comes from letting your work speak for you. Furthermore, your **reference group**—the five people you compare yourself to—has a disproportionate impact on your behavior and the scale of your goals. Sometimes, you must "like your goals more than you like your friends" and be willing to adjust your social circle to align with your ambitions.
