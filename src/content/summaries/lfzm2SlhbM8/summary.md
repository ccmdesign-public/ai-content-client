## Key Takeaways

Dominic Vituchi, founder of AI accounting automation company Onshore, explains why accounting is the next industry ripe for AI disruption.

* **Accounting tasks are highly automatable** - Much of the work involves simple data transfer, arithmetic, and documentation that AI models now handle better than junior/mid-level accountants.

* **Legacy firms resist innovation** - Accounting firms have structural disincentives to adopt automation because they bill by the hour, and senior partners near retirement won't invest in technologies that undermine their billing model.

* **Sell to end-users, not intermediaries** - After failing to sell automation software to accounting firms for two years, Onshore pivoted to selling directly to corporations (taxpayers) who actually benefit from the outcomes.

* **The accounting firm pyramid will invert** - Future firms will have few junior staff (replaced by AI), a middle layer for regulatory expertise, and top partners focused on sales and business development.

* **Revenue per employee will skyrocket** - Onshore targets $1M revenue per employee versus $100-150K at traditional firms, representing an order-of-magnitude efficiency improvement.

* **Start with a narrow wedge** - Successful disruption begins by excelling at one specific task (like R&D tax credits) before expanding, rather than trying to replace everything at once.

## Summary

### Introduction to Accounting Industry Disruption

Dominic Vituchi, CEO and founder of Onshore, argues that the accounting industry is on the brink of fundamental transformation through AI automation. Having worked at Grant Thornton and founded a Y Combinator-backed company, Vituchi brings insider perspective to why this centuries-old industry is vulnerable. He describes a "functional and tectonic shift" in how accounting outcomes are delivered to customers, moving away from billable hours and manual processes toward automated, technology-driven solutions.

### The Automatable Nature of Accounting Work

Vituchi breaks down accounting tasks to their essence: junior accountants often perform simple data transfer between documents, basic arithmetic, and documentation gathering. He shares a personal example from his time at Grant Thornton working on R&D tax credits, where he spent 20+ hours taking notes during employee interviews just to determine what percentage of their time qualified for the credit. The actual calculation was straightforward arithmetic

- the complexity came from substantiation and documentation. This pattern repeats across accounting: the work is rarely about complex math but about proving what happened through contemporaneous documentation.

### Why Legacy Accounting Firms Resist Change

The interview reveals deep structural reasons why traditional accounting firms won't lead their own disruption. First, their business model depends on billable hours

- automation directly threatens their revenue model. Second, there's an "asymmetric value proposition" where senior partners near retirement (often mandatory at age 65) have no incentive to invest in technologies that might pay off in 5-15 years but reduce short-term profits. Third, firms lack technical talent; Vituchi was often the only person with a software background among thousands of employees. Even when firms announce billion-dollar AI investments, the reality is often purchasing GitHub Copilot licenses without clear use cases.

### The Failed First Attempt: Selling to Accountants

Onshore initially tried selling automation software to accounting firms themselves. Despite having 8 customers and mid-six-figure revenue, Vituchi encountered constant resistance. Senior partners liked the margin improvement potential, but junior and mid-level staff resisted because automating 80% of their work threatened their billable hour requirements for career advancement. After two years of "banging my head against the wall," Vituchi realized accountants were "the least motivated group of people on the planet" to adopt automation that threatened their employment model.

### The Successful Pivot: Selling to Corporations

In summer 2022, Onshore fired all its accounting firm customers and rebuilt its software to serve corporations directly. Vituchi began cold DMing companies on LinkedIn, eventually landing a Miami-based client who appreciated the lower prices and better efficiency. This pivot coincided with acceptance into Y Combinator's winter 2023 batch. The company's unusual founding team

- Vituchi as a former junior associate and his co-founder Mark as a senior Grant Thornton partner

- provided both technical credibility and deep industry knowledge.

### The Future of Accounting Employment and Business Models

Vituchi predicts accounting industry revenue will increase but employment will shift dramatically. Revenue per employee at firms like Deloitte ($100-150K) will be dwarfed by AI-native companies like Onshore targeting $1M per employee. The traditional pyramid structure (many juniors, few seniors) will invert: AI agents replace junior staff, a middle layer handles regulatory expertise, and senior partners focus on sales. Vituchi doesn't see complete human elimination but rather new roles for technically-skilled professionals who can work alongside AI tools.

### Strategic Advice for AI Disruption

Vituchi offers several strategic insights:

* **Start with a narrow wedge** - Onshore began with R&D tax credits only, proving excellence in one area before expanding

* **Listen to customer pull** - New features emerged from customers asking "can you also do this?" after experiencing initial success

* **Build for end-users** - The real value flows to corporations, not intermediaries

* **Timing matters** - GPT-3's limitations in early 2021 made automation challenging, but today's frontier models outperform junior and mid-level accounting staff

### Broader Implications and Future Predictions

The conversation extends beyond accounting to professional services generally. Vituchi compares accounting to legal services, where companies like Lora and Harvey have successfully sold to lawyers despite hourly billing models. He predicts 2026 will bring widespread realization that AI can perform knowledge work at human levels, though mass unemployment might take 5-7 more years. For aspiring entrepreneurs, Vituchi suggests reimagining Microsoft Excel

- many professional workflows built on spreadsheets could be transformed into dedicated, AI-powered tools.
