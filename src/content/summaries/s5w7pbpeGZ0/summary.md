## Key Takeaways

Ellen Zong explains the evolution of molecular machine learning from protein folding to dynamic analysis.
• **Cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM)** enables experimental measurement of protein motions rather than just simulation
• **Machine learning solves inverse problems** - reconstructing 3D structures and dynamics from noisy 2D projection images
• **Protein dynamics understanding remains limited** - we've solved static structure prediction but still lack good models for molecular motions
• **Interdisciplinary collaboration** between computational and experimental researchers is essential for biological discovery

## Summary

Ellen Zong's research focuses on **molecular machine learning** with applications in structural biology, particularly using **cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM)** to study protein dynamics.

Her journey began at **DHAW Research** (formerly Desire) working on protein folding simulations, then moved to **DeepMind** during the AlphaFold 2 release, and now leads her own lab at Princeton. This diverse experience taught her different approaches to structuring research problems

- from DHAW's focus on reproducibility to DeepMind's emphasis on clear objective functions.

**Cryo-EM represents a paradigm shift** from computational simulations to experimental measurement of protein motions. While traditional methods like molecular dynamics require validation through simulation, cryo-EM captures actual protein movements by imaging ensembles of protein snapshots frozen in different conformations.

**The core machine learning challenge** involves solving inverse problems: reconstructing 3D atomic coordinates and dynamics from extremely noisy 2D projection images. Her group develops physics-inspired machine learning models to analyze this data and infer complex distributions of protein structures.

**Protein dynamics represents the next frontier** beyond static structure prediction. While AlphaFold solved the protein folding problem for static structures, proteins function through complex motions that remain poorly understood. Zong's work focuses on developing methods to study these molecular machines in motion.

**Collaboration drives discovery** - her group works closely with structural biologists and chemists who provide experimental data and domain expertise. This interdisciplinary approach ensures computational methods directly contribute to scientific discovery by automating analysis and revealing new information from experimental data.

**Future directions** will require new experimental technologies alongside machine learning advances. While current AI models have ingested existing data, truly new discoveries will come from combining novel experimental approaches with machine learning to bridge the gap between molecular biology and human health applications.

## Context

This interview addresses the evolution of AI in structural biology beyond the protein folding problem solved by AlphaFold. As computational methods mature, researchers are shifting focus from predicting static protein structures to understanding dynamic molecular motions that enable biological function. This work has implications for drug discovery, understanding disease mechanisms, and designing novel proteins. The conversation highlights how interdisciplinary collaboration between computational and experimental scientists drives progress in this rapidly advancing field.
