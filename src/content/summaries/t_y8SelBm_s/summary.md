## Key Takeaways

The Flower Letters is a subscription-based snail mail business that sends serialized fiction stories through physical letters, achieving remarkable growth through strategic marketing and a deep understanding of their customer base.

*   **Rapid, profitable scaling** is possible with low overhead: The business reached $36,000 in monthly recurring revenue within its first four months, with a simple cost structure of $2-$3 per letter (stamp, paper, envelope) against a $12/month subscription price.

*   **Paid acquisition via Facebook/Instagram ads** was the primary growth driver, with early customer acquisition costs (CAC) of $15-$20, though this has risen to $40-$50 as the business scaled. **Email list building** from day one proved critical, eventually driving 38-50% of revenue.

*   **The product is engineered for low churn and high lifetime value (LTV)**: The serialized story format (24 letters over 12 months) creates narrative investment, while the product's nature as a **highly giftable item** (especially for Mother's Day) means recipients often can't cancel gifts. Annual churn is ~5%, and LTV is around $180.

*   **Clear founder roles enable quality and scale**: The business thrives on a partnership where Michael's wife, Hanny, handles all creative (writing, art, design) while Michael manages business operations, marketing, and growth, allowing each to focus on their strengths.

*   **Success requires deep customer understanding**: The core demographic is women 35+, skewing older, who value the tactile, anticipatory experience. Marketing to men has not worked. The business intentionally creates 'light' stories with happy endings that resonate with this underserved market.

*   **Advice for starting a subscription business**: Know your customer intimately, consider starting with a 'customer of one' (yourself), validate demand by collecting email addresses before building the full product, and if it's a creative venture, find a business-minded partner to handle operations.

## Summary

### Business Origin and Early Traction

The Flower Letters was founded in early 2020 by Michael Clark and his wife Hanny, a talented artist and writer. The idea emerged from Hanny's successful side business selling floral paintings on Instagram and a conversation about how to grow her email list. Inspired by another subscription service, Letters From Afar, they conceptualized sending a continuous story through physical mail. They launched with a simple Instagram page, offering a subscription of two letters per month for $12, telling a year-long story. Without telling friends or family, they gained 33 initial subscribers. Within four months, they were generating $36,000 in monthly recurring revenue, which was mostly profit given the low cost of goods (approximately $2-$3 per letter for stamps, high-quality paper, and inserts).

### Growth Strategy and Marketing

Growth accelerated when they began experimenting with **Facebook and Instagram ads**, despite having no prior experience. A key hire in October 2020, a young marketer who approached them, began managing ads full-time. This paid acquisition strategy became their primary engine, though costs rose from an initial $15-$20 CAC to $40-$50. A crucial, early strategic decision was to **aggressively build an email list**. Their website funnel captured emails by offering discounts, and this owned channel now drives 38-50% of revenue. Michael emphasizes that email is a non-negotiable asset for any entrepreneur, providing a direct line to customers independent of platform algorithms.

### Product Mechanics and Business Model

The core product is a 12-month, 24-letter story subscription. They now have six concurrent stories (mostly historical fiction, with one fantasy series), offering eight total subscription options. A critical insight was offering a **prepaid annual option** for $100 (about $8.33/month), which significantly boosts cash flow and reduces churn, as customers are committed to the full narrative arc. The product is designed for very **low annual churn (~5%)**, driven by its giftable nature (it's often given by daughters to mothers) and the serialized format that creates anticipation. The average customer lifetime value is about $180, as many customers purchase subscriptions for multiple recipients or sign up for new stories. Mother's Day is their 'Super Bowl,' outperforming Christmas in sales volume.

### Operations, Team, and Financials

The business is surprisingly hands-on. All 120,000 letters sent monthly are stuffed by hand by a team of five in a warehouse in Orem, Utah (down from 15 people through operational efficiencies). Total headcount is 16. Financially, the company broke $5 million in revenue in 2024 and $7 million in the following year. They reinvest heavily, keeping net profit low for tax and growth purposes. In the last year, they spent $2.6 million on Meta ads. The business model allocates roughly one-third of revenue to cost of goods, one-third to marketing, and the remainder to operations and reinvestment.

### Future Opportunities and Founder Advice

Michael sees significant expansion opportunities in **retail** (they already have a partnership with the Newport Mansions gift shop), audio adaptations, and even potential TV series. He uses AI daily for business intelligence—analyzing ad performance, financials, and customer emails—but not for the creative writing. For aspiring entrepreneurs, especially in the subscription or snail mail space, his key advice is to **deeply know your customer** and validate the market simply by trying to collect email addresses for a 'coming soon' product. He stresses the value of a partnership where a creative mind (focused on the product) is paired with a business mind (focused on operations and marketing), which has been fundamental to their success.
