## Key Takeaways

Chris Koerner interviews a founder who revitalizes outdated driving ranges by focusing on customer experience and modern business practices. Key insights include:

* **The bar was low** - Traditional driving ranges offered stale, unwelcoming experiences, creating opportunity for improvement.

* **Membership over gift cards** - A $20/month Ranch Pass with 40% bucket discounts drives consistent visits versus one-time prepayments.

* **Experience engineering** - Focus on the 'first and last five minutes' of customer visits, welcoming atmosphere, and no time limits.

* **Strategic loss leaders** - Putting greens (expensive to maintain) and free first buckets attract serious golfers who become high-LTV customers.

* **Digital marketing advantage** - Younger entrepreneurs can leverage modern websites and paid ads where older owners haven't updated since the 1990s.

* **Instructor partnerships** - Golf coaches serve as powerful salespeople and relationship builders who drive membership and retention.

## Summary

### The Business Model Transformation

The founder's journey began with recognizing that traditional driving ranges represented an underserved market with low expectations. The industry was dated, with stale experiences, poor turf quality, and unwelcoming atmospheres. The initial insight was simple: provide something slightly better and nicer than what existed. This led to transforming a facility that went from hitting 3.5 balls per year to 12 million balls today across four locations.

Customer surveys revealed the most important improvements: ball quality and turf quality. Rather than recycling all balls like typical ranges, Golf Ranch implemented sorting processes and invests in replacing 120,000 brand new balls annually at their Richardson location alone. They sourced high-quality mats from Amazon and smaller distributors to replace the concrete-like hitting surfaces common at older ranges.

### Membership and Customer Experience Innovation

The most significant business model shift was moving from traditional gift card/prepayment systems to a membership model. The $20/month Ranch Pass provides exclusive pricing (40% less per bucket) and flexibility with cancel-anytime terms. This created consistent revenue and customer commitment, with 40% of current customers being members. The membership model also unlocked additional programming like events, competitions, and preferred pricing on lessons.

Experience engineering became central to the strategy. The founder emphasizes the 'first and last five minutes sandwich' - what customers remember most. Unlike Topgolf's time-limited sessions that end with rushed bills, Golf Ranch encourages unlimited stay times. This creates a more welcoming departure experience that leaves customers satisfied rather than feeling shortchanged.

### Atmosphere and Community Building

Creating a welcoming environment in an traditionally unwelcoming industry was a major focus. The venue welcomes casual attire, beverages with friends, and even families with homework-doing kids. Strategic elements include:

* String lights instead of harsh office lighting to create a relaxed, backyard atmosphere

* Music (historically frowned upon in golf)
* Covered bays to extend operating hours during extreme weather

* Patio areas for non-golfing companions

* Fans and heaters for temperature control

This approach transformed the driving range into a 'third place' between home and work, similar to how gyms become part of people's routines. The most serious customers, like two older gentlemen who come daily to drink coffee and people-watch, demonstrate the community aspect.

### Technology and Operational Insights

The business leverages Top Tracer Range technology licensed from Topgolf, which provides ball-tracking data and gamification. While Topgolf initially tried selling this to existing range owners (who were often in their 70s and resistant to change), Golf Ranch saw the opportunity to implement it themselves. The technology includes a SAS model with upsell opportunities like Top Tracer Coach for instructors and mobile apps for customers to track their data.

Operational decisions reflect strategic prioritization. While maintaining perfect grass across the entire outfield would cost $1 million, they focus on the first 30 yards that customers actually see. They use automated ball pickers (including Roomba-style devices) and maintain ball inventory buffers for days when weather prevents picking. Water costs are significant in the grass business, with some locations using creek water rather than city sources.

### Marketing and Growth Strategy

Digital marketing represents a major competitive advantage. While many driving range owners haven't updated their websites since the 1990s, Golf Ranch runs sophisticated paid ad campaigns targeting high-intent searches like 'golf lessons near me.' Their website optimization and compelling offers (like free first buckets) convert traffic efficiently.

The LTV-to-CAC ratio is approximately 10:1, with lifetime value north of several hundred dollars per customer. Retention is strong, though the real value lies in 5-10 year customer relationships. The business targets families and casual golfers rather than exclusively wealthy enthusiasts, expanding their addressable market beyond initial assumptions.

### Expansion and Future Vision

Growth plans target 50 locations nationwide (one in every state) with 100,000 members. The acquisition strategy focuses on existing driving ranges that need TLC rather than building from scratch. New locations like Grand Prairie represent bigger challenges since they're reviving abandoned ranges with negative reputations.

The founder emphasizes growing 'the right way' rather than taking excessive capital and expanding too quickly. Maintaining the soul and community aspect across locations is crucial. Major costs include labor, land payments, taxes, water bills, and infrastructure buildouts. The business model proves that simple doesn't mean easy

- execution matters.

### Key Differentiators from Topgolf

While Topgolf successfully made golf sexy and drove first-time visits, Golf Ranch focuses on different aspects:

* **Hospitality balance**: Topgolf is food/beverage-forward (party concept), while Golf Ranch keeps food simple (15% of revenue) and golf-primary

* **Repeat visits**: Topgolf struggles with retention due to expense and occasion-based nature, while Golf Ranch's membership model drives routine visits

* **Customer experience**: Unlimited time versus Topgolf's hourly reservations that end with rushed departures

* **Target market**: Golf Ranch attracts serious golfers alongside casual players, creating higher lifetime value customers

The business demonstrates how modernizing traditional industries through customer focus, technology, and strategic execution can create significant value.
