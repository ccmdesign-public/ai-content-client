## Key Takeaways

The core advice for scaling a private equity portfolio focuses on strategic focus and talent acquisition. • **Delegate operations and focus on high-leverage activities** like brand building and deal generation. • **Acquire larger businesses with better founders** (targeting $20M+ ARR) to avoid 'babysitting' mediocre talent. • **Recognize that the founder is often the limiting factor** in growth; seek 'dogs' (highly driven entrepreneurs) over founders who have already achieved their financial goals.

## Summary

The video features a consultation with a private equity operator whose group buys and holds software businesses, currently at $12M in revenue across four companies, with a goal of reaching $90M in five years through a mix of acquisitions and organic growth.

The operator's primary challenge is balancing time between helping existing portfolio companies grow and pursuing new acquisitions. The advisor's fundamental recommendation is to **delegate operations entirely** to separate teams. He states his highest leverage activity is **generating demand** through brand building, which in turn attracts talent and deal flow. This singular focus allows him to hire people to handle everything else.

Regarding the acquisition strategy, the advisor identifies a critical flaw: the current portfolio likely consists of smaller businesses (around $3M) with founders who may lack the ambition to scale significantly. He argues that founders who sell at that level often have already achieved their financial goals ($20-30M) and enter a 'maintenance mode' focused on stability rather than aggressive growth.

**The solution is to 'buy bigger.'** The advisor suggests targeting businesses with around **$20M in Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR)**. The logic is that an entrepreneur capable of building to that level is inherently more skilled and ambitious—a 'dog' in his terminology. These founders possess the innate drive to 10x a business, whereas mediocre founders get bogged down in day-to-day fires.

He shares a personal anecdote from doing 24 acquisitions between 2022-2023, where he exited 19 because the founders 'just weren't winners.' Winners, he asserts, either grow rapidly or fail; they don't remain stagnant. Therefore, the scaling bottleneck is a **'who' problem**, not just a 'how' problem. For a 'buy and hold' strategy, the quality and drive of the people you acquire with are paramount.

The advisor concludes that many investors 'reinvent the wheel,' thinking they can easily fix small, broken businesses, only to realize the immense difficulty of actually building them. The simpler, more effective path is to pay for quality upfront by acquiring larger, better-run companies with exceptional talent already in place.

## Context

This discussion is highly relevant for investors, private equity operators, and entrepreneurs involved in buying, selling, or scaling small to medium-sized businesses (SMBs), particularly in the software sector. It addresses the common tension in portfolio management between nurturing existing assets and pursuing new ones, offering a clear framework for prioritization based on leverage and talent quality. The insights challenge the allure of 'fixer-upper' acquisitions and emphasize the long-term value of partnering with proven, high-ambition founders.
