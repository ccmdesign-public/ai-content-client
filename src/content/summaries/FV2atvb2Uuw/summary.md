## Key Takeaways

The core message is that small service businesses should optimize and scale proven acquisition channels before diversifying. Key insights: • Implement **attribution tracking** to measure ROI on marketing spend. • **Scale what's already working** (door knocking and potentially ads) before pursuing new customer segments. • Maintain **focus on one primary acquisition channel** to avoid dilution of effort and resources.

## Summary

The video features Alex Hormozi consulting a business owner running "Supreme Power Wash," a service company doing $500k in revenue ($250k profit) primarily through residential door-to-door sales for power washing, solar panel cleaning, and window cleaning.

Hormozi diagnoses the owner's main problem: a lack of a reliable, scalable acquisition channel beyond referrals and sporadic door knocking. The owner is considering pivoting to commercial clients for recurring revenue but hasn't maximized his current methods.

### Current Business Analysis

The business spends $2,000/month on ads managed by a third party but lacks clear **attribution tracking** to know if they are profitable. Door knocking generates roughly 20 deals/month at an average of $500, bringing in ~$10k monthly. With 50% margins, the core model is solid.

### Strategic Recommendation

Hormozi's advice is a clear two-step process:
1.  **Get Attribution in Place:** The first critical step is to establish data tracking for the ad spend. The owner needs to know exactly what return he's getting on his $2k investment. Hormozi suggests the current ad agency should provide this, or find one that does.
2.  **Scale the Proven Channel:** Once attribution confirms the ads are profitable (Hormozi suspects they might be at a 5:1 or 10:1 ROI), the owner should aggressively increase the ad budget—from $2k to $4k, $6k, or even $10k/month—as long as the return remains positive.

The overarching principle is **"do more of what's already working"** before attempting to build a new acquisition channel for a different customer avatar (commercial). Hormozi notes that for local service businesses, having multiple channels can be acceptable due to limited market size, but the priority is to fully exploit the highest-ROI channel first.

The owner's fear of losing money on ads is addressed by the need for data. With proper tracking, scaling becomes a calculated business decision rather than a gamble.

## Context

This matters for small business owners and entrepreneurs in service-based industries (cleaning, contracting, landscaping) who are stuck at a revenue plateau and unsure how to grow. Many rely on inconsistent methods like referrals and consider risky pivots instead of systematically scaling their existing, profitable activities. Hormozi's framework provides a disciplined approach to growth by emphasizing data-driven decision-making and channel optimization before diversification.
