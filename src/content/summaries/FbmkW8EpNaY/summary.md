## Key Takeaways

This podcast episode explores the diverse realities of indie game development through the experiences of two distinct creators. Nolan Eie focuses on experimental, technical projects while Adam Ununice works on traditional long-form games, revealing different paths to creative satisfaction in game development.

* **Start small and make many games** - Both developers emphasize beginning with tiny projects rather than attempting massive multi-year endeavors that risk never being completed or played.

* **Find your creative niche** - Nolan discovered his strength lies in experimental, tech-focused games that explore unconventional interfaces, while Adam channels childhood gaming experiences into traditional RPG development.

* **Technical experimentation drives innovation** - Nolan's approach involves finding surprising input/output mechanisms in existing applications (like SSH, browser search, or Mac OS Finder) and building games around them, creating unique technical blog-worthy projects.

* **Community and feedback shape development** - Environments like the Recurse Center provide valuable peer feedback, while multiplayer experimental games require careful consideration of how players might ruin the experience through trolling or abuse.

* **Game jams vs. long-form projects serve different purposes** - Short jams allow for creative expression without commitment, while longer projects require crossing a threshold where they become products needing ongoing support and justification of time investment.

* **Technical challenges become learning opportunities** - From bandwidth issues with SSH games to performance optimization problems, technical hurdles often lead to deeper understanding and interesting blog content that documents the problem-solving journey.

## Summary

### Introduction and Backgrounds

The episode begins with host TJ introducing guests Adam Ununice and Nolan Eie, both indie game developers with contrasting approaches. Nolan shares how he started making games three years ago as a way to return to coding, beginning with small games in GDAU. He discovered Adam's YouTube channel while struggling with pixel art, appreciating Adam's practical, work-focused content over more common "edutainment" game development videos that offer little applicable knowledge. Nolan emphasizes the common advice to "make a ton of games" rather than attempting massive projects like MMOs that take years and may never find players.

Adam's introduction comes through a story about how he connected with the hosts during a 24-hour game development stream in a water tower, which began their friendship. The conversation establishes Adam as a traditional game developer focused on pixel art and long-form RPGs, while Nolan has evolved toward more experimental, technical projects.

### Nolan's Journey to Experimental Games

Nolan describes his pivotal experience at the Recurse Center, a programming retreat in Brooklyn where he began experimenting with "weirder" game concepts. His first unconventional project was a Wordle-like game built using the Open Search specification, which allows browsers to search websites directly. He realized that any system with input and output mechanisms could become a game, creating Wordle (which he accidentally named "Wardle" before learning about Josh Wardle) that worked through browser search suggestions.

This experience at Recurse Center, surrounded by other programmers working on unconventional projects, helped Nolan realize his strengths lay in experimental games rather than traditional genres. He recalls making a Vampire Survivors clone but feeling disconnected during playtesting feedback sessions, realizing he preferred creating "weird stuff" over conventional genre games. This led to his philosophy of finding surprising input/output mechanisms in existing applications and building games around them, like Flappy Bird running in Mac OS Finder or games using file renaming as output.

### Development Philosophies and Completion Criteria

The developers discuss how they determine when a game is finished. For Nolan's multiplayer experimental projects, completion involves ensuring systems are robust against player attempts to ruin the experience through trolling, slurs, or inappropriate content. For his smaller technical experiments, games are done when there are "no more problems left to solve" - often when technical limitations are reached, like pushing Mac OS Finder to four frames per second with double buffering.

Adam describes his more traditional approach, explaining that as a "boring case of a gamer" who grew up with Super Nintendo and PlayStation, he channels feelings from playing games like Final Fantasy 10 into his projects. His bigger games are done when they're "finished" in the conventional sense

- with credits, 20+ hours of content, and side quests. Smaller projects serve different purposes, scratching the itch of hacking things together and prototyping concepts he studied in university design programs.

### Technical Challenges and Learning Experiences

Nolan shares detailed stories about technical challenges that became significant learning experiences. His "One Million Checkboxes" project started as a simple concept but required solving major technical problems around sending massive amounts of data efficiently. He moved from Python to Go for performance reasons, learning about binary data packing and websocket optimization through firefighting real traffic issues.

His SSH-based snake game led to even deeper technical exploration, including patching the Go SSH library to disable keystroke obfuscation

- a security feature that sends junk data to mask typing patterns. This optimization was crucial for game performance but sparked controversy when he blogged about it, with some readers concerned about security implications. The game also included humorous error messages blaming terminals for poor Unicode block element rendering, particularly targeting Mac OS's outdated terminal app.

### Bandwidth and Infrastructure Lessons

Both developers share stories about unexpected bandwidth issues. The hosts recount their Terminal Coffee project, where they accidentally sent massive amounts of data (100+ gigabytes per hour) due to a constantly refreshing timer that resent a huge ASCII art image 60 times per second. This taught them that even "text-based" SSH applications can create significant bandwidth problems when not optimized properly.

Nolan discusses how his approach to infrastructure has evolved from reactive firefighting to more proactive planning, though he acknowledges this sometimes means "prematurely optimizing" and missing the excitement of solving urgent performance problems under pressure. He balances this by still taking on projects specifically to learn about particular technical areas, like building highly efficient terminal applications.

### Creative Cross-Pollination and Future Ideas

The conversation explores how ideas migrate between different types of projects. Adam shares that mechanics from his game jam project "Trauma" - a platformer where players collect cards representing abilities to chain together movement

- influenced the ability system in his larger game Insignia. Similarly, stream interactions (like a candy-based gambling game) sometimes become in-game mechanics.

The group brainstorms future game ideas, particularly around iframes

- both the HTML element and the Dark Souls reference. They discuss the potential for games that span multiple iframes or windows, with characters jumping between viewports. This leads to broader observations about how iframes are heavily used (and often abused) on modern websites, particularly news sites that load massive amounts of video content through them.

### Community and Industry Observations

The developers touch on the game development community, noting that game jams frequently feature entries with multi-window gameplay mechanics. They also discuss the reality of long-term game development, referencing a friend who spent four years developing a tower defense game. The conversation highlights the tension between creative experimentation and the practical demands of turning projects into sustainable products.

Throughout the discussion, both developers emphasize the importance of finding one's unique creative voice

- whether through technical experimentation, traditional game development, or some combination of approaches. They celebrate the diversity of indie game development while acknowledging the challenges of completion, technical hurdles, and finding audiences for unconventional projects.
