## Key Takeaways

This video argues that organic, unplanned innovation driven by motivated individuals often outperforms rigid organizational planning. • **Motivated side projects** outside OKRs can deliver the most valuable solutions. • **Leadership's role** is to recognize and integrate valuable unplanned work, not suppress it. • **Perfect organization slows growth**; embracing controlled chaos enables faster adaptation and innovation, especially in AI-native startups.

## Summary

The speaker shares experiences from their time as a product manager, where the most impactful work consistently came from engineers' unplanned, passionate side projects rather than formally planned organizational objectives. These initiatives, developed outside work hours (like on weekends), delivered excellent solutions—one involving machine learning for artwork and another for accessibility—that the team hadn't previously considered or allocated time for.

The critical leadership insight is that a product manager's job in these situations is **not to 'squish'** this organic innovation but to recognize its alignment with the team's mission, embrace it, and integrate it into the workflow, regardless of whether it appears in formal OKRs (Objectives and Key Results). This approach of wrapping in valuable unplanned work and getting it moving is presented as what good leaders do.

The video challenges the pursuit of perfect organizational structure, arguing that overly organized systems inevitably slow down, while life and innovation are inherently messy. Quoting Jurassic Park's "Life will find a way," the speaker advocates for letting this natural, chaotic process of problem-solving flourish. This is especially relevant in the age of AI, where small, agile, and seemingly messy teams—mirroring the structure of fast-growing, AI-native startups—can achieve incredible growth velocity when leaders don't over-structure their creative processes.

## Context

This matters because it challenges the prevalent business dogma of meticulous planning, rigid OKR frameworks, and top-down innovation management. In an era dominated by AI and rapid technological change, the ability to harness organic, bottom-up creativity is becoming a critical competitive advantage. Product managers, engineering leaders, startup founders, and anyone in a leadership role should care about this shift from control to cultivation, as it redefines how high-performing teams operate and innovate in dynamic environments.
