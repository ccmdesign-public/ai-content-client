## Key Takeaways

California Senator Scott Wiener, Y Combinator CEO Gary Tan, and startup founders announce SB 1074 (the BASED Act), a targeted bill to combat anti-competitive practices by dominant tech platforms.

* **The bill targets 'self-preferencing'**, a practice where trillion-dollar platforms with over 100 million US users unfairly favor their own products/services over competitors, distorting the internet.

* **Specific harms include Apple blocking alternative app stores, Google manipulating search, Amazon using seller data to clone products, and Meta demoting competitor links**, leading to higher prices, fewer choices, and stifled innovation.

* **The legislation is modeled after California's Cartwright Act but updated for the digital era**, banning a range of behaviors like search manipulation, most-favored-nation clauses, and using non-public data to compete.

* **Startup founders testify to real-world impacts**: Alt Store had to launch in Europe due to DMA, Pebble watches lack features on iOS, and AI assistants like Poke face bans or prohibitive fees from Meta.

* **The bill is framed as 'anti-corruption work'** to ensure the best idea wins, not the best-connected, and to restore public confidence that the AI boom will benefit consumers, not just big tech's bottom line.

## Summary

### Introduction and Problem Statement

California Senator Scott Wiener, alongside Y Combinator CEO Gary Tan and Economic Security California Action Vice President Terry Oolie, announces the introduction of SB 1074, the 'Blocking Anti-Competitive Self-Preferencing by Entrenched Dominant Platforms Act' (BASED Act). The press conference, hosted at Y Combinator, frames the issue as a critical threat to competition and innovation. Wiener states that the largest online platforms in history are 'rigging the internet' to undermine and box out their competitors, including startups trying to become the next big players. He argues that everyone experiences the impacts of this anti-competitive behavior daily.

Wiener provides concrete examples: **Apple** blocks users from downloading competing app stores, forcing startups to pay exorbitant fees. **Google** manipulates search results to prioritize products it owns or is invested in. **Amazon** sets anti-discount policies (most-favored-nation clauses) that raise prices and uses non-public data from third-party sellers to develop and launch competing products. **Meta** demotes links from competing platforms in its news feed, influencing the information seen by hundreds of millions. The harms include higher prices for consumers, a degraded user experience, fueled misinformation, and stifled innovation. He emphasizes this is especially important now, during an AI boom, to ensure the public benefits from innovation and has confidence in a level playing field.

### The Bill and Its Mechanism

Senator Wiener explains that SB 1074 is designed to create new tools beyond outdated federal law to protect competition online. The bill prevents any digital platform with a market capitalization greater than a trillion dollars and serving 100 million or more monthly users in the US from favoring their own products and services on the platforms they operate. It is modeled after California's nation-leading Cartwright Act but tailored for the digital era.

The bill specifies a range of prohibited self-preferencing behaviors, including:

* Manipulating search or ranking results.

* Using non-public data obtained from the platform to develop competing products.

* Employing policies, charges, or practices that put users at an unreasonable cost disadvantage.

* Conditioning access to the platform on purchasing other products from the platform.

* Most-favored-nation clauses.

* Denying access to the platform to favor the platform's own product.

* Preventing consumers from obtaining a portable copy of their own data.

Enforcement mechanisms are the same as under the Cartwright Act: private lawsuits or action by the Attorney General. Wiener acknowledges the fight will be tough against the largest corporations on Earth but believes they are on the right side with a growing grassroots coalition.

### Perspectives from Y Combinator and Economic Advocates

Y Combinator CEO Gary Tan provides the investor and builder perspective. He contrasts the open web of 20 years ago, where anyone could build and reach anyone, with today's powerful but gated environment. He highlights 'vibe coding' and AI that let founders build real services in an afternoon, but then they 'run straight into a wall' when trying to reach users. The App Store has 'arbitrary rules, opaque rejections, a 30% tax' with no appeal. Google search now terminates on Google's own services (Flights, Shopping, AI answers), swallowing the open web. He argues that freedom shrinks when a computer becomes a phone: Apple decides what you can install, what payment systems you can use, and whether your app exists.

Tan clarifies the bill is not an attack on vertical integration (which can be good, like Apple's chips) or 'big tech,' but it is 'for little tech.' It targets a 'narrow set of genuinely anti-competitive behaviors' where a platform uses control over essential infrastructure to tilt the playing field—**market corruption, not innovation**. Y Combinator, having funded over 5,000 companies, sees founders lose on access, not merits, a problem the market cannot fix because the controllers benefit from restricting it.

Terry Oolie of Economic Security California Action provides an advocacy and consumer perspective. She uses an analogy of a toll road owner launching a trucking company, where every toll paid funds that competitor and their trucks get green lights. This is what happens on digital platforms every day. She notes that when a few companies exploit dominance to stifle competition, everyone pays with higher prices, fewer choices, less innovation, and lower quality. She argues California, which built the template for the tech economy, is uniquely obligated to ensure the scrappy startup story remains possible, as federal action has stalled.

### Startup Founder Testimonies: Real-World Impacts

Four founders share specific, personal battles with platform gatekeepers.

**Shane Gil, co-founder of Alt Store**, explains they started the store to release apps Apple wouldn't allow, like a retro game emulator called Delta. After years as a hacky sideloading tool funded by Patreon, the EU's Digital Markets Act (DMA) paved the way for alternative app stores. When preparing to launch Alt Store PAL in the EU, Apple held Delta in review for weeks, then changed App Store rules worldwide to allow emulators, 'taking away our opportunity to launch with a killer exclusive app.' Delta later became the #1 app on the App Store for two weeks, proving competition works. Alt Store now hosts over 100 developers with varied business models, without taking a cut, but exists 'only because European law required Apple to allow us to exist.'

**Eric Migicovsky, founder of Pebble and Beeper**, discusses the hidden impact of self-preferencing: consumers don't know what they're missing because many startups 'die on the vine.' Pebble smartwatches are blocked from offering a great experience on iPhone. For example, Apple Watch users can respond to notifications by voice, but Apple does not allow third-party access to that feature. In Europe, under the DMA, Pebble users can use this feature. 'This bill hopefully will put pressure on these large gatekeepers to allow the same capabilities... in the US.'

**Martin van Hagen, founder of Poke (by Interaction)**, recounts being banned from WhatsApp after Meta introduced its own AI assistant. Poke is a proactive AI system inside messaging apps. Meta claimed AI put a strain on systems, but van Hagen argued billions of consumers don't pay while businesses like Poke do (WhatsApp's revenue model). Italy ruled in their favor, and the EU opened an investigation, but Meta replaced the ban with fees so high that real competition is nearly impossible. He states Europeans in Berlin and Milan now have more choice in AI assistants than Americans in the Bay Area, which should embarrass us. 'SB 1074 fixes that.'

**Peter Kro, co-founder of Blue**, left Google to build a powerful voice assistant that gives an AI model full control over your phone. To achieve this, they had to design and manufacture a USB dongle because 'the hardware incumbent has not shared access to these basic actions on their devices.' Their users include people with motor or visual impairments, for whom voice access is independence. He finds it ironic that Apple, with a great reputation for accessibility, makes it impossible for innovators to make phones universally usable. If SB 1074 passes, Blue would not require the dongle and would be available to everyone as software.

### Q&A and Closing Arguments

In a brief Q&A, Wiener addresses enforcement and the bill's applicability (publicly traded companies next year, all by 2030). He responds to potential opposition arguments about security, noting that AI tools like **Claude's code security review** have democratized secure computing, making the 'big tech protection' argument less valid. Terry Oolie rebuts a claim from the Chamber of Progress (funded by the targeted firms) that the bill conflicts with other legislation, explaining that SB 1074 is a precise, narrow bill targeting specific conduct by specific actors, complementary to broader competition law reforms like AB 1776 (the COMPETE Act).

The conference concludes with a unified call to action: to ensure the next founder can compete on the merits, not on who controls the pipes, and to make the free market win in California as it has in Europe.
