## Key Takeaways

The core message is that cheap marketing leads are often misleading and unprofitable because they don't account for conversion and sales costs. Key insights include:

*   **Cost per lead is a vanity metric** that hides the true cost of customer acquisition, which is **cost per closed deal**.

*   **Unqualified leads have a hidden cost**, burning approximately 30 minutes of sales time per lead on discovery calls, follow-ups, and proposals for prospects who never buy.

*   **Platforms differ in lead quality**: Facebook ads may generate many cheap leads that ghost, while LinkedIn ads generate fewer, more expensive leads from educated, high-intent decision-makers (like VPs) who are further along in the buying journey.

## Summary

The video debunks the common marketing mistake of prioritizing low cost per lead (CPL) over actual business results. Using a clear mathematical example, it illustrates how focusing on CPL creates a false sense of success.

### The Vanity Metric Trap

A marketer might spend $1,000 on Facebook ads, generating 200 leads at $5 each. The dashboard looks great, but reality sets in during the sales process. Out of those 200 leads, 190 may ghost after the first email, 8 might book a discovery call but lack decision-making authority, and only 2 might actually close. The real **cost per deal** in this scenario is $500, not $5.

### The Value of High-Intent Leads

In contrast, running the same $1,000 test on LinkedIn might yield only 5 leads at $200 each—seemingly expensive. However, 3 of those leads could be VPs or above, and 2 might close deals worth $50,000 to $100,000. The cost per deal remains $500, but the revenue outcome is vastly superior.

### The Hidden Cost of Bad Leads

The critical difference lies in the **hidden cost of bad leads**. Every unqualified lead consumes roughly 30 minutes of valuable sales team time on activities like discovery calls, follow-ups, and proposal preparation—all for a non-buyer. This drains resources and morale.

### Interruption vs. Intent Marketing

LinkedIn leads represent **intent marketing**: they come in already educated, having researched for months. Their questions are advanced ("Why should I choose you over your competitor?"), not basic ("What do you sell?"). Facebook leads often represent **interruption marketing**, catching people's attention while they're doing other things.

The ultimate lesson is to connect ad spend directly to revenue. If you can't track your marketing spend to actual closed deals, you're making decisions in the dark. The platform with cheaper leads is not necessarily the one that delivers better, more profitable customers.

## Context

This matters because many B2B marketers and business owners optimize their campaigns for the wrong metric, wasting budget and sales resources on leads that never convert. In an era of data-driven marketing, focusing on superficial metrics like cost per lead can lead to significant financial losses and missed opportunities. This is crucial for anyone responsible for marketing spend, sales efficiency, or overall business profitability, highlighting the shift from lead volume to lead quality and sales alignment.
