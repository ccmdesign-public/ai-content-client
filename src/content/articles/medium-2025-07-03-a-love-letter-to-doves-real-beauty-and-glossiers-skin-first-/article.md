Hindsight, as they say, is 20/20. When Alessia Cara sang about self-love, she wasn’t just performing — she was validating a movement.At her live performance at the MTV VMAs in 2017, she cast away her accessories and slipped out of a stunning red dress, revealing a simple outfit and wiping off her makeup onstage. It was raw, unfiltered, and deeply symbolic — a moment that resonated with anyone who’s ever felt uncomfortable in their own skin.

Her song became an anthem for the countless people taught to hide what makes them unique. It spoke to those who’ve covered their freckles, concealed their scars, and agonized over blemishes, wrinkles, or uneven skin tones. It was a reminder that beauty isn’t something you achieve through filters or flawless makeup. It’s the confidence to step out as you are, imperfections and all, and let your inner light shine outward.

Real-life moments like model Winnie Harlow celebrating her vitiligo or gymnast Simone Biles redefining mental strength over physical perfection prove the tides are shifting.

Dove and Glossier — two brands that didn’t just join the conversation but straight-up reshaped it. Let’s dive into how they did it, shall we?

## A Love Letter to Brands That Get It

## Dove, the Trailblazer

Dove, you beauty (pun intended). You took a look at an industry obsessed with airbrushed perfection and said, *“Nope, not today.”* Instead of models with poreless skin and unattainable curves, you gave us *us* — real women with wrinkles, cellulite, freckles, and scars.

Your **Real Beauty Campaign** wasn’t just clever advertising; it was a wake-up call. When you launched in 2004, the beauty industry was stuck in a loop of impossible ideals. You didn’t just nudge the door open; you kicked it off its hinges.

![]()

Remember those billboards? They were monumental. Women of all shapes, colors, and ages smiled down at us, unapologetically human. And that video — *Real Beauty Sketches*? Watching women describe themselves with painful self-criticism, only to hear strangers paint them as radiant? It was touching.You made us cry at our desks, Dove, and we forgave you because we needed the representation.

## Glossier, the Millennial Pink Rebel

Now, Glossier, you were the cool kid who walked in when the party was already buzzing but somehow stole the spotlight. While Dove tackled beauty standards head-on, you did a sidestep. Your mantra, **Skin First, Makeup Second**, wasn’t loud — it was quietly subversive. You told us our freckles and acne scars weren’t flaws — they were part of the vibe.

![]()

You rode in on minimalist packaging and Instagram aesthetics, serving us millennial pink realness with a side of self-love. It wasn’t just about looking good — it was about *feeling* good. You understood that heavy contour and 10-step routines were yesterday’s news. Instead, you sold us on simplicity and glowing skin, minus the fuss.

## Why These Movements Succeeded

## Dove’s Real Beauty Campaign:

**The Hypothesis:**
Dove hypothesized that celebrating diverse, authentic representations of beauty would resonate with audiences disillusioned by unattainable standards.

![]()

**Why It Worked:**

-   **Empathy-Driven Messaging:** By showcasing real women instead of professional models, Dove redefined beauty, making it relatable and inclusive.
-   **Viral Impact:** The “Evolution” video, which exposed how heavily manipulated beauty images are, became a cultural moment, sparking global conversations.
-   **Purposeful Initiatives:** Programs like the “Dove Self-Esteem Project” cemented its role as a brand with a mission beyond selling soap.

**The Legacy:**
Launched in 2004, the Real Beauty Campaign didn’t just sell products; it built trust, loyalty, and a legacy by making beauty standards about representation rather than aspiration.

## Glossier’s Skin First, Makeup Second:

**The Hypothesis:**
Glossier bet on the idea that modern consumers, especially Millennials and Gen Z, value self-expression and simplicity over heavily curated perfection.

![]()

**Why It Worked:**

-   **Social Media Savvy:** Glossier turned Instagram into its biggest ally, leveraging user-generated content and creating a community-driven narrative.
-   **Relatable Influencers:** By partnering with micro-influencers, Glossier created authentic buzz in niche, engaged audiences.
-   **Aesthetic Consistency:** From its millennial pink packaging to airy visuals, the brand cultivated an approachable and aspirational ethos.

**The Legacy:**
Glossier didn’t just ride the wave of changing beauty norms; it refined them, making self-care the new aspiration and minimalism the new luxury.

## Lessons and Hypotheses for Younger Brands

## BizWit Hypothesis :

**1.Champion Authenticity:**
Showcase unfiltered, behind-the-scenes moments to build trust and relatability. Like Dove, don’t just reflect your audience — represent them.

**2\. Foster Inclusivity:**
Make diversity a core brand value, ensuring all identities feel seen and celebrated in your campaigns.

**3\. Create Purpose-Driven Initiatives:**
Align with causes your audience cares about, whether it’s mental health, body positivity, or sustainability.

**4\. Leverage Community:**
Engage your audience through interactive social media strategies, like polls, AMAs, and user-generated content. Glossier’s mastery of turning customers into advocates is a roadmap worth following.

**5\. Simplify Offerings:**
Offer solutions that align with real-life needs, like multi-use products or minimalist alternatives to cluttered routines.

**6.Embrace Visual Consistency:**
Develop a unique aesthetic — whether it’s bold, playful, or understated — and make it synonymous with your brand identity.

## The Hypothesis for Success:

Younger brands thrive when they align their messaging with the aspirations and values of their audiences. Authenticity, inclusivity, and purpose are not just trends — they’re imperatives in a marketplace where consumers crave connection as much as quality.

In a world that profits off insecurities, Dove and Glossier chose to celebrate humanity.And that? THAT IS REAL BEAUTY! My Marketing teacher always told us that Coke didn’t sell the drink, they sold fizz. These two brands held up a mirror and made us look at the faces we forgot to love. They took the word “beauty” off of it’s lofty standards and brought it down to earth, where it belongs.

Younger brands, take note: authenticity is the clue that ties everything together. As the saying goes, “The world is a puzzle, and branding merely one piece. Choose wisely how you place it.”
