# Why Everyone Is Wrong About AI Brain Rot—And What’s Actually Happening to Us

#### *We’ve been here before. The cigarette industry rewired a generation’s health before anyone admitted it. History is rhyming again—but the story is more complicated this time.*

![https://www.flickr.com/photos/clotho98/6923384430/in/photostream/](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/483/1*E1TUHQhYE6dNQZbaUjcPSw.png)

In [1927](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1470496/), a Lucky Strike advertisement told Americans that 9,651 physicians had confirmed their cigarettes were “less irritating” to the throat.

Nobody called it manipulation. Nobody called it harm. It was science. It was modern. It was endorsed by people in white coats.

Today, a student types a question into ChatGPT and receives a polished essay in four seconds. Nobody calls it dependency. Nobody calls it cognitive erosion. It is productivity. It is modern. It is everywhere.

The parallel should give us pause. But not for the reasons most people think.

### The cigarette analogy is real—and it’s being dismissed too fast

When cigarettes swept through the 1920s and 30s, the adoption curve was explosive. By the 1930s and 1940s, a majority of American physicians actually smoked, and tobacco companies ran doctor-endorsed ads in publications including the *New England Journal of Medicine* and [*JAMA*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1470496/)*.* The harm was slow, invisible, and inconvenient to admit.

Large language models are following the same curve. Adoption is near-vertical. Institutions are endorsing them. The potential harm—to deep thinking, cognitive independence, and the ability to sit with uncertainty—is slow, unmeasurable, and inconvenient to the companies profiting from it.

*“The most dangerous harms are always the ones that feel like help.”*

The “brain rot” concern isn’t about intelligence. It’s about atrophy. Muscles you don’t use weaken. The same is true for the neural pathways involved in sustained attention, original reasoning, and tolerating not-knowing.

A 2025 MIT Media Lab [study](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.08872) measured the brain activity of 54 participants writing SAT essays—one group using ChatGPT, one using Google, and one using nothing. ChatGPT users showed the lowest neural engagement across 32 brain regions and “consistently underperformed at neural, linguistic, and behavioral levels.” By their third essay, many simply handed the prompt to ChatGPT and pasted the result. The researchers called this pattern *cognitive debt*.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/851/1*zT2NI9s9JoCcBaIPQoXfqA.png)

### But the analogy has a critical flaw

Here’s where the cigarette comparison breaks down—and where most takes become intellectually lazy.

Cigarettes have zero redeeming effect on the body. There is no dose at which they make you healthier, sharper, or more capable. The harm is unconditional.

AI is not unconditional. A surgeon using AI diagnostics saves lives. A researcher synthesizing 400 papers in an afternoon advances science. A programmer debugging faster ships better software. The tool itself is not the villain.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/843/1*1do7qSFljqN0rXFZi7EYdg.png)

A more precise analogy is GPS. A 2020 study in [*Scientific Reports*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-62877-0) found that heavier GPS use was associated with a dose-dependent steeper decline in hippocampal-dependent spatial memory over a three-year follow-up period—and that the decline was caused by GPS use, not the other way around. [4](https://6dd2aa393139a64a51bc1df939d27bb3.claudemcpcontent.com/mcp_apps?connect-src=https%3A%2F%2Fesm.sh+https%3A%2F%2Fcdnjs.cloudflare.com+https%3A%2F%2Fcdn.jsdelivr.net+https%3A%2F%2Funpkg.com&resource-src=https%3A%2F%2Fesm.sh+https%3A%2F%2Fcdnjs.cloudflare.com+https%3A%2F%2Fcdn.jsdelivr.net+https%3A%2F%2Funpkg.com+https%3A%2F%2Fassets.claude.ai&dev=true#ref4) But no one argues you should throw away maps. The question is, are you ever navigating without one?

![Photo by Tamas Tuzes-Katai on Unsplash](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/0*qypOb4EyAfm6uOd1)

Earlier, a 2011 Columbia study in [*Science*](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1207745) showed what they called the “Google Effect”: when people expect information to be searchable later, they recall the facts themselves less—but become better at remembering where to locate them. The researchers described the internet as a “transactive memory system”—a cognitive partner that restructures how the brain stores knowledge.

### The real danger nobody is naming

The most honest version of the brain rot concern isn’t “AI makes you dumb.” It’s this:

*If a generation grows up using AI to write, reason, and decide—without ever developing those muscles independently—do they lose the capacity to function without it?*

This is dependency, not damage. And it operates generationally, invisibly, with a potential 20-year lag before we see full consequences. Exactly like cigarettes.

![https://ourworldindata.org/leaded-gasoline-phase-out](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/787/1*-Lg1KzoPSbPipnixVtrAVQ.png)

### What you can actually do about it

The answer is not to stop using AI. The answer is to use it the way elite athletes use performance analysis—as a tool that sharpens your capability, not one that replaces it.

The MIT study offers a specific insight here: participants who started by writing their essay themselves, then used ChatGPT to revise, showed stronger brain engagement and better ownership of their work than those who opened with AI. Sequence matters.

**Three rules that make the difference:**

**1\. Always attempt first.** Before you prompt, think for five minutes. Even if your answer is wrong, the cognitive effort is the point. The struggle is where the learning takes place.

**2\. Critique, don’t consume.** When AI gives you an answer, interrogate it. Find its weaknesses. This keeps your critical reasoning muscles engaged rather than atrophied.

**3\. Build, don’t borrow.** Use AI to go further than you could alone—not to cover the distance you could already cover yourself. The moment AI becomes a shortcut for things you used to be able to do, you are in cigarette territory.

The cigarette generation remained unaware of the harm they were causing. That’s the uncomfortable truth. They trusted the tool because it felt like it was everywhere, and the people selling it were very confident.

We have something they didn’t: the warning from history. Whether we use it is up to us.
