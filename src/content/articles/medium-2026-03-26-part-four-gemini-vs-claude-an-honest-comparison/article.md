# Part Four — Gemini vs Claude, An Honest Comparison

#### The Thinking Partner — Part Four

![Two AI assistants, one big question: which writes better?](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*XxcZsFXzJi6kIwSv6c70_g.png)

### I Tried Writing a Novel With Gemini. Then I Switched to Claude.

I didn’t set out to use two AI tools. I started with one, assumed it would be enough, and quickly discovered it wasn’t.

For the first few weeks of writing my novel, I used Gemini for everything. The research, the planning, and when I felt ready, the writing itself. The research was fine. The planning was useful. The writing was not. The output was short, the quality was average, and after several iterations on the first chapter I had something that was, at best, acceptable. Not good. Acceptable.

I’d read that Claude was the better tool for writers, so I decided to test it properly. I uploaded my writing guide, gave it my instructions, and waited to see what came back.

The difference was immediately obvious.

### What Gemini does well

Before I get to Claude, it’s worth being clear about where Gemini earns its place, because it does earn it.

Gemini is my first call for research and fact-finding. It has access to current information from the web, which Claude doesn’t, and it handles quick sanity checks well. If I need to verify a detail, check whether something is plausible, or get a broad overview of a topic before I go deeper, Gemini is faster and more reliable for that specific job.

It’s also useful in the early planning stages of a project. When I was building the structure of my novel, Gemini helped me think through the broad shape of the story, the research behind the setting, and the plausibility of certain plot decisions. For that kind of exploratory, fact-adjacent work, it holds up well.

Gemini is also my go-to for image generation. Whether it’s a cover concept, a character reference, or a scene illustration, the quality is consistently good and the integration with the rest of the workflow is straightforward. Claude doesn’t factor into that side of things at all.

What it doesn’t do well is sustain a long, complex piece of writing. It struggles with long documents, loses the thread on extended projects, and has a tendency to drift off topic if the conversation runs for any length of time. Ask it for a chapter and you’ll get something shorter than you wanted, written in a voice that’s competent but flat.

I tried. It wasn’t good enough.

### The moment I switched

When I uploaded my writing guide to Claude and asked it to produce the first chapter, the output was noticeably different. Longer, for a start. But more than that, it had followed the instructions in the guide carefully. The voice felt closer to what I was after. The sentences had more variety. The scene had weight.

I was on the free version at that point, and I kept running out of tokens. After a few sessions it became clear that if I was serious about this project, I needed the subscription. I paid for it, and within six hours of that first proper Claude session, I had a complete first draft of the manuscript.

And I want to be clear about what a first draft means. It was a starting point, not a finished book. I went through the complete manuscript several times after that, making improvements to the story, refining the voice, and fixing everything that didn’t quite work. The six hours produced something to build on. The real work came after. If you’re expecting a magic button, this isn’t it.

### What Claude does that Gemini doesn’t

The clearest difference is in the writing. Claude produces longer, richer output and handles complex instructions more reliably. When I told it to write in a specific voice, check every chapter against the writing guide, and not proceed without my approval, it followed those instructions consistently. Gemini, in my experience, is less reliable on that kind of sustained, rule-bound task.

Claude is also significantly better at handling long documents. I could upload the entire writing guide, refer back to decisions made chapters earlier, and trust that the context was being held. That matters enormously on a project of any length.

The voice consistency was the thing that surprised me most. The more I used Claude and updated the writing guide, the better it became at staying true to the style I was after. It learned the project in a way that Gemini didn’t. When I got stuck and asked for ideas, Claude’s suggestions were more relevant, more in keeping with the tone and characters, than anything Gemini had offered.

For me, this was the difference between getting stuck and actually finishing chapters. With Gemini, the momentum kept stalling. With Claude, it didn’t.

There’s also the question of what happens when you push back. Claude handles disagreement well. Tell it a suggestion doesn’t fit and it’ll ask why, or offer an alternative, rather than just producing a slightly rephrased version of the same thing. That back-and-forth is where a lot of the real work happens, and it works better when the model engages with your reasoning rather than ignoring it.

I also used it to work through the book reviews I’d collected from other models. I uploaded the reports and asked Claude to read through the suggestions, give me an honest opinion on each one, and explain its reasoning. It listed them clearly, argued for or against each, and made a recommendation. It was right more often than not. Sometimes I disagreed and overruled it. But having that structured second opinion on every suggestion, with reasons rather than just a verdict, made the editing process considerably more focused.

### Where both let you down

Neither model is infallible, and it’s worth being honest about that.

Hallucinations are a problem in both. Confident, plausible, entirely fabricated details presented as fact. I verify everything that matters. That’s not optional.

Claude doesn’t have access to current web information in the way Gemini does, which is why I keep Gemini in the workflow for research. It’s a genuine limitation if you’re writing anything that requires up-to-date facts.

And Claude loses context as a chat grows longer. As a session approaches its limit, the quality of the output starts to drop and errors creep in. The fix is straightforward, but there’s a right way to do it. Before closing the chat, I asked it to write a prompt for the new session and advise me which documents to upload. That way, nothing important got lost in the handover. The new chat started with exactly the right context and picked up almost seamlessly. Without that step, you’d be guessing at what to carry over.

### How the workflow actually looks

In practice, the workflow is simple:

**Gemini** → research, fact-checking, planning, image generation **Claude** → writing, editing, consistency, complex feedback

Gemini handles the early stages. Anything that benefits from access to current information or a quick overview. Once the structure is in place and I’m ready to write, I switch to Claude and it takes over completely.

That handover isn’t arbitrary. It reflects what each tool is actually good at. Trying to use Gemini for everything, as I did at the start, produced mediocre results because I was asking it to do something it isn’t built for. Trying to use Claude for research would be the same mistake in reverse.

The right tool for the right job isn’t a complicated idea. It just takes a bit of experience to find out which job belongs to which tool.

### The honest verdict

Gemini is a good research assistant and a capable image generator. It’s reliable for fact-finding, useful in the planning stages, and fast enough for quick checks. For those specific tasks, I wouldn’t swap it.

Claude is the better tool for writers, and it isn’t a close call. The output is longer and richer. The voice consistency is stronger. Complex instructions are followed more reliably, and when you’re stuck the back-and-forth is genuinely useful. If you’re writing anything of length, anything that requires a consistent voice over multiple sessions, Claude is where the work gets done.

I started this project hoping one tool would be enough. It wasn’t. But once I stopped trying to force the wrong tool into the wrong role, the process became considerably clearer.

Gemini helps you start. Claude helps you finish.
