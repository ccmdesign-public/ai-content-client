# Part One: Why I’m Using AI to Fight a Wandering Mind

![Building the scaffold: Using AI to hold the structure while the attention drifts.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*iLzkG6RiPo8f7o33wOzbCA.png)

I’ve had a Medium subscription for three years, but my ‘Drafts’ folder stayed empty. It wasn’t a lack of ideas. It was the gap between having a thought and typing the first paragraph, a wall that felt almost physical. My mind wanders. I’ll start a sentence, wonder if the kettle is on, and by the time I’ve checked, the momentum is gone.

I’ve been around computers long enough to know that they should be helping with this. My first was a Sinclair ZX81, a machine with its own proprietary operating system and a version of Sinclair BASIC. Back then, if you wanted your computer to do something useful, you usually had to write the programme yourself. There were no off-the-shelf solutions. Later, the landscape shifted as IBM clones brought MS-DOS into the office, and I lived through every iteration of Windows since. Usually spent in the weeds, trying to make technology behave the way I wanted.

Now, my focus has shifted to AI, specifically Gemini and Claude. I use them as a sparring partner to help me scale that wall of concentration. Gemini tends to be my first call for quick sanity checks or pulling in up-to-date information from the web. I turn to Claude when I need a deeper, more considered critique of my logic. Claude seems to understand the nuances of a British voice better than most, which makes it a useful companion for refining the flow of a piece without losing my own perspective.

### The myth of the magic button

There is a common assumption that AI is a ‘generate’ button for writing. People think you ask for an article and, presto, out pops a finished piece. Some see this as cheating, and I agree with them, at least when there is no personal input. If you simply copy and paste whatever a machine produces, you are not a writer. You are just the postman.

In my experience, that hands-off approach produces nothing but grey slop. It is boring, generic, and often wrong. To produce something worth reading, you have to treat AI like any other tool in the shed. A hammer does not build a house. It requires a steady hand, a clear plan, and a significant amount of hard work.

I tested this properly by writing a full-length novel with AI as my partner. I want to be clear about what that actually meant in practice. It was not a matter of describing a plot and waiting for pages to appear. I spent 103 hours over the course of the project, planning sessions, structural arguments with the model, line-by-line editing passes, and more than a few moments where I threw out entire sections because they did not sound like me. The AI held the scaffolding. I built the house.

Early reviews have been excellent, and so far, not a single reader has suspected the book was AI-assisted. That is not because the AI wrote something undetectable. It is because I worked hard enough that the final voice is genuinely mine. The difference now is that I am actually able to finish what I start.

### AI as cognitive scaffolding

For someone with a wandering mind, the real value of these models is not the output. It is the structure they hold in place while your attention drifts.

Before I started using AI, I simply could not get ideas onto paper. I would go off on tangents or lose interest before the first page was finished. The blank screen was not just intimidating; it was a prompt to think about everything except the thing I was supposed to be writing.

Now I don’t start with a blank screen. I start with a conversation. I give the model my raw thoughts, half-formed, non-linear, occasionally contradictory, and ask it to help me find the structure. This initial phase is the most important one, because it bypasses the paralysis entirely. Instead of staring at a blinking cursor, I am talking through a problem with a partner that has infinite patience and no interest in checking its own kettle.

I might have three different points I want to make, but my mind has jumped between them in a way that makes no sense on paper. The model helps me pull those threads into a coherent line. It acts as a kind of external memory, keeping everything in place while I focus on the individual details. If I wander off, the outline is still there when I get back, exactly where we left it.

### The prompt is a technical specification, not a magic spell

We are at a point where knowing how to write a good prompt is becoming a genuinely valuable skill. It reminds me of the precision required to navigate a command line forty years ago. If you didn’t get the syntax right in MS-DOS, the computer simply sat there. Today, a model will always give you an answer, but if the prompt is lazy, the result will be worse than useless.

A good prompt is not just a command. It is an instruction set, context, tone, and constraints. Ask an AI to ‘write about technology’ and you will get a generic list of clichés. Tell it instead that you are a tech veteran with a wandering mind, that you want to discuss the transition from the ZX81 to modern language models, that you want British English and no corporate buzzwords, and suddenly the model has something to work with.

And you do not just fire off one prompt and walk away. You iterate. You ask the model to explain its reasoning, or to critique its own output. I often have two or three different chats running at once, one generating an idea, another acting as a hostile editor. It is a back-and-forth process that requires constant attention. Treat the prompt like a technical specification and you start to see real results. Treat it like a magic spell and you will be disappointed every time.

### The human still has to show up

The prompt is the starting gun, but it is only half the work. The other half is the edit, and that is where I earn my place in the process.

My approach is to act as the editor rather than the typist. I take a draft from one model and ask another to find the holes in the argument or suggest improvements to the flow. I then revise until I am satisfied the piece is stronger. It is the same process a good editor uses with a writer, except I am playing both roles.

I let the AI help me organise the logic, but I remain responsible for the truth. Models have a habit of presenting guesses as facts, so I verify every claim. I also watch for the AI voice, the corporate phrase, the marketing tone, the sentence that sounds like it was written by a committee. When I find one, I swap it for plain English. My job is to strip away the varnish and make sure the core idea is still mine.

Technology has changed a great deal since the days of the ZX81. We have gone from 1KB of memory to models that can process entire libraries of information in seconds. But the fundamental principle has not shifted: the machine is only as useful as the person giving the instructions.

What I cannot quite shake, though, is a question that surfaces now and then at the end of a long session. If these tools keep improving, and they will, at what point does my contribution become the smaller part of the collaboration? Right now, the voice is mine and the judgement is mine. The AI is the scaffold, not the building. But scaffolding has a way of becoming load-bearing without anyone quite noticing.

For the moment, at least, the Drafts folder is no longer empty.
