# The AI Economy Has Two Winners and One Casualty

Which one are you becoming?

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*vCMsbqATysK4Pi1PwugOdQ.jpeg)

I was interviewing candidates last month for a role we’d been trying to fill for weeks. Two finalists. Both strong on paper.

The first candidate was polished. Seven years of experience, recognizable companies, the kind of resume that would get tons of offers. Team leadership. Process improvements. Delivered on time, on budget. He walked through his career with confidence.

Then I asked the question I now ask everyone: “**How do you use AI?**”

He paused. “Umm…I use ChatGPT personally. For emails, brainstorming, that sort of thing.” He smiled like he’d checked the box.

The second candidate had two years of experience. Maybe less. We almost ruled her out, but her energy in the initial phone interview was incredible.

When I asked her the same question, she didn’t answer it. She opened her laptop, fired up Claude Code, and showed me what she’d built. A full feature she’d shipped the previous week. Solo. She walked through her decisions: what she built, what she didn’t build, why she chose to automate one piece and manually review another.

She got the offer.

We’ve been asking that question in every interview now. Not as a filter for AI enthusiasm.

As a filter for agency. The stakes for hiring are completely different than they were even a year ago.

Vincent Weisser, co-founder of Prime Intellect, put it the same way: “My first interview question: how do you use AI? If a developer isn’t using it properly, that’s usually disqualifying.”

The hiring question changed because the job changed. And what I’m seeing, interview after interview, isn’t a simple shift. It’s a split. Two profiles are winning right now. One profile is disappearing. And most people don’t realize which one they are.

### What’s Being Compressed?

Let me name what’s disappearing, because it’s not what most people think.

It’s not low-skill work. It’s not the janitor or the warehouse worker. It’s the middle. The single-discipline specialists who spent years getting good at one thing. Front-end engineer. Back-end engineer. Designer. Project manager. People whose entire professional identity is built around a narrow technical skill executed at a high level.

I’m seeing it in every conversation with founders and hiring managers. The roles getting cut or going unfilled aren’t random. They’re the ones where the work is bounded: clear inputs, clear outputs, someone else already made the decisions. The roles that are growing are the ones where someone has to decide what’s worth building in the first place.

***If your job is translating instructions into output, you’re now competing with something that does it faster, cheaper, and at 3am without asking for context.***

***The work hasn’t disappeared. It’s been absorbed. Agents do it now. What used to take a team of four takes one person directing three agents.***

Here’s what makes this moment different from every other wave of automation.

For forty years, careers were built on a model: go deep in one thing, become the expert, and let that vertical depth carry your career.

The T-shaped professional. Deep expertise in one domain, with just enough breadth to collaborate across disciplines. McKinsey coined the idea in the 1980s. IDEO’s Tim Brown popularized it. Every talent development framework since has been built around it.

That model just [broke](https://medium.com/@reuvengorsht/the-swiss-army-knife-economy-why-speed-beats-specialization-in-the-ai-era-1b73e29e6ad2).

AI compresses the time it takes to become functionally capable in something new.

A marketer can now conduct serious data analysis.

A designer can ship production code.

A product manager can build the actual product, not just the spec.

The horizontal bar of the T, the part that used to represent “enthusiasm for other disciplines,” is no longer decorative. It’s your personal execution platform. And the vertical bar, the deep specialization that used to be your primary value? It’s becoming table stakes. Not worthless. Just not enough.

PwC’s 2026 workforce analysis calls it the “rise of the generalist”: a move toward broader, outcome-focused roles already underway across industries.

They estimate AI agents can shave 40 to 50 percent of human effort in redesigned workflows. Not by eliminating people. By eliminating the need for narrow specialists to sit between a problem and its solution.

And this is the part that’s hard to say, but I think it’s true: your job title is becoming a feature request. The things that used to define a career, the specific narrow competencies that filled resumes and justified promotions.

Those are the exact capabilities that AI replicates first. Not because they’re simple. Because they’re bounded. They have clear inputs and outputs. They can be prompted into existence.

So if that’s what’s being compressed, the obvious question is: who’s winning?

### Who’s Actually Winning?

I’ve been watching this closely. In conversations with founders, in hiring, in how teams are being restructured. Two profiles keep emerging.

Not job titles. Profiles. Ways of operating that cut across industries and roles.

**The Builder.**

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*E82IURBkhi6FlbL_WcNGvg.png)

**High agency. Product sense. AI-native.**

This is the person who can take a problem and just go. They don’t wait for permission. They don’t need a team of specialists around them. They move fast, they ship, and they have taste about what’s worth building.

Harrison Chase, the founder of LangChain, described this person perfectly: “The generalist scrappy AI power user with product sense.”

Wealthsimple, a leading Canadian fintech, just launched their [AI Builders program](https://www.wealthsimple.com/en-ca/careers/ai-builders), and the criteria tell you everything. They’re not gating on degrees or years of experience.

Their message to applicants: “Your build tells us where you’re going.” They want to see what you’ve shipped, not what you’ve studied. That’s the Builder profile, formalized into a hiring pipeline.

What makes the Builder possible right now is the T-shaped inversion I just described.

A year ago, building a real product required assembling a team: someone for the front end, someone for the back end, a designer, a project manager. Each specialist contributing their narrow depth.

Now one person with AI can move across all of those domains, not as a tourist, but as a functional operator. Ben Yoskovitz, a very seasoned product manager, who wrote about the [career model AI just broke](https://www.focusedchaos.co/p/the-40-year-old-career-model-ai-just-broke), put it this way:

> “I’m not just collaborating with developers anymore. I’m doing development.”

That’s not a small shift. That’s a structural one.

The Builder doesn’t need to be an expert in every domain. They need to be capable enough in many domains, with the judgment to know when AI is carrying its weight and when it’s producing garbage.

***Their breadth isn’t a bonus on top of their specialty. Their breadth IS the specialty.***

I’ve been watching builders operate. What strikes me is how much they resemble startup founders, except now one person can do what used to require five or more.

The numbers bear this out: 36.3% of all new global startups are now solo-founded, according to Scalable.news. Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, puts the odds of a one-person billion-dollar company at 70–80%. Not in some distant future. In 2026.

**That’s the leverage available to a single high-agency builder today. Not someday. Today.**

**The Director.**

This profile looks different.

Less hands-on, more architectural. The Director isn’t executing. They’re directing. They know which problems are worth solving. That’s a harder question than it sounds. They manage agents, not people. They bring systems thinking at a senior level.

Harrison Chase again: “We still need senior architects to get the idea over the line.”

If the Builder benefits from the T-shaped inversion by going wide, the Director benefits from understanding its limits. Harvard Business School researchers recently ran an experiment at IG Group, a global trading firm, that revealed something they call “the GenAI Wall.”

They gave marketing specialists, web analysts, and software developers the same content tasks, with and without AI.

For structured work like building article templates, AI was an equalizer: everyone performed at the same level regardless of background. But for unstructured work that required real judgment, like writing the actual article, marketing specialists could cross into web analysts’ territory with AI help. Software developers couldn’t. They hit a wall.

Here’s what that means in practice. AI lets you cross into adjacent domains, but it doesn’t let you parachute into distant ones.

Professor Iavor Bojinov, who led the study, explains it plainly:

> “If it’s an area that we do not understand, we are just performing at the baseline of the model.”

The Director understands this.

**They know where AI bridges gaps and where it creates false confidence. They know which problems can be handed to an agent and which require someone who’s lived inside the domain for years.**

This is what PwC means when they describe the shift from pyramids to hourglasses: the middle management layer shrinks, but the people who remain focus on “exception handling, coaching, and high-value decision-making.”

That’s the Director. Not managing headcount. Managing capability.

I’ve noticed something about Directors that separates them from traditional senior leaders. They don’t think in headcount.

**They think in capability. How many agents can I run in parallel? What’s the right system architecture for this problem? Where does the human need to stay in the loop, and where can I let the machine run?**

What both profiles share is judgment. Not domain expertise. Not technical skill. Judgment. The ability to look at a world where execution is essentially free and decide what’s actually worth doing.

Peter Steinberger, who built OpenClaw into the fastest-growing open-source project on GitHub, said it this way:

> “The person who knows what not to build is now more valuable than the person who can build anything.”

Execution is free. Judgment is the bottleneck.

That’s the new economy in one line. And it raises an uncomfortable but important question: how do you actually develop the judgment that these profiles require?

### How Do You Become One of Them?

This is the part where it really hits, because it’s not theoretical. this transition is happening now. In real time. Some of us notice it more. Some of us Less. And the ones who succeed share a few patterns.

**Context engineering over prompt engineering.**

I hear a lot of people talk about “getting better at prompting.” Social media is flooded with courses that teach prompting and AI hacks.

That’s not it. T**he real skill is context engineering.** Not how to ask the model a question. How to architect the entire information environment the model operates in.

Harrison Chase called it directly:

> “Context engineering is the bottleneck.”

The model can run in a loop now. It can generate code, write copy, analyze data. But someone has to design the memory, the tools, the guardrails, the feedback loops around it. That’s context engineering.

**Go wide, but know the wall.**

The old advice was simple. But now it’s ancient and irrelevant:

Pick a lane, go deep, stay there.

That advice is breaking down. AI lets you become functionally capable in adjacent domains faster than any training program ever could. But there’s a catch, and the Harvard research makes it concrete.

You can cross into domains that are adjacent to what you already know.

A marketer can become a data analyst with AI.

A product manager can ship code.

**But you can’t leap into something completely foreign and expect AI to carry you.** That makes you a “[Fool with a Tool](https://reuvengorsht.substack.com/p/a-fool-with-a-tool-is-still-a-fool)”.

The GenAI Wall is real. **If you don’t have foundational understanding of a domain, you’re just performing at the baseline of the model. And the model’s baseline isn’t good enough for real work.**

So the move isn’t “become a generalist” in some vague, know-a-little-about-everything way. It’s this:

**Pick one domain you understand deeply. Then use AI to expand into adjacent territories where your existing knowledge gives you enough context to evaluate what the model produces.**

That’s how you build the kind of functional breadth that Builders and Directors need.

I wrote about this in [Why The Generalist Rises in the AI Era](https://medium.com/@reuvengorsht/why-the-generalist-rises-in-the-ai-era-aa7e46f65f79). The rarest combination right now isn’t AI skill alone.

It’s domain knowledge plus AI fluency. AI in medicine. AI in finance. AI in real estate. AI in legal.

The people who are truly irreplaceable right now aren’t the ones who know AI best. They’re the ones who understand a specific domain deeply and can see where AI creates leverage that nobody else in that domain has imagined yet.

**Ship, don’t study.**

The interview question is “how do you use AI?” Not “what courses have you taken?” Not “what certifications do you have?” The answer needs to be real, specific, and demonstrable.

**Know what NOT to build.**

This is the hardest one. And the most valuable. When execution is essentially free, when anything can be prompted into existence in minutes, the bottleneck shifts completely. It’s no longer about capability. It’s about judgment.

Practice asking: **which problem is actually worth solving? What shouldn’t we build?** Remember, just because you “could” build, doesn’t mean you “should” build. It’s a dangerous spiral and a [productivity trap](https://reuvengorsht.substack.com/p/productive-at-what).

This is the muscle that agents cannot replace. It’s the thing that separates the builder from the coder, the director from the manager.

The wage data reflects this. PwC’s 2025 AI Jobs Barometer shows that AI-skilled workers now earn a 56% wage premium over comparable roles. Last year it was 25%. It doubled in twelve months. The gap between AI-native and AI-reluctant professionals isn’t just widening. It’s accelerating. Not because AI skill is rare. Because judgment applied through AI is rare.

The interview question changed. Your answer needs to change faster.

### The Only Question That Matters

Here’s what I keep coming back to.

You’re not competing with AI.

That’s the wrong frame. I explored what happens when we [outsource the struggle that builds competence](https://generativeai.pub/i-hit-my-ai-limit-and-realized-id-become-useless-db6bb6578b1e). The answer wasn’t pretty. You’re competing with people who use AI to compress everything you spent years learning into a weekend project. That’s uncomfortable. I get it. I’ve felt it myself.

But that discomfort is also the opportunity.

Because right now, most experienced professionals haven’t changed their workflow. The tools are good enough for 10x output, but the majority of people are still operating the way they did two years ago. The gap between AI-native and AI-reluctant is widening every month. And it won’t last. In 18 months, AI-native won’t be a differentiator. It’ll be a baseline. The advantage is available right now, and only right now.

So here’s what I’d do this week: pick one AI tool. Not three. One. Go deep. Ship something real that someone else can use. Not a tutorial. Not a course project. Something with your judgment baked into it. That’s your new resume.

And longer term, stop thinking like a specialist waiting for the next project. Start thinking like a builder who directs agents, or a director who chooses which problems matter. The forty-year career model that rewarded going narrow and deep? It’s not coming back. The professionals who thrive from here will be the ones who figured out that breadth, applied with judgment, is the new depth.

That candidate with two years of experience who got the offer over the seven-year veteran? She didn’t have more experience. She had more agency. She had judgment about what to build and what to skip. She had a real answer to the only interview question that matters now.

Two profiles win. One disappears. The only question is which one you’re becoming.

Execution is free. Judgment isn’t. Choose accordingly.
