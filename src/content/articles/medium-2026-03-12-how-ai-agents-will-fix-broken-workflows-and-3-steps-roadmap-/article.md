# How AI Agents will fix broken workflows, and 3-steps roadmap enterprise leaders must follow today.

# How AI Agents will fix broken workflows, and 3-steps roadmap enterprise leaders must follow today.

[Saber CHEN](/@saberchen?source=post_page---byline--b86461296afa---------------------------------------)

9 min read·Mar 3, 2026

\--

![Face the vertigo, or face the fall.]()

\*

Where exactly do we stand right now? Absolutely no one can say for sure anymore. In the blink of an eye, the chatbots have stopped talking and started frantically clicking the mouse on your behalf. Have we entered the age of agentic AI? Or is this an infrastructure supercycle?

The release of new models simply will not stop. Claude Sonnet 4.6 crashed the party in mid-February, taking complete control of your desktop. Gemini 3.1 Pro debuted around the exact same time, effortlessly crushing the ARC-AGI-2 benchmark with a 77.1% score and doubling logical reasoning metrics overnight. OpenAI locked Codex inside a pure whiteboard environment for 25 hours; when it emerged, it brought out 30,000 lines of code and a standalone, independently developed design tool. In late February, the academic world threw FDM-1 to the public, granting everyone free access to a computer action model. The pure software era has turned the page. Digital laborers are officially in position. They are scrolling your mouse, and they will never, ever clock out.

Executives running traditional IT companies are breaking into a cold sweat; they watched helplessly as IBM’s market cap evaporated by 13% in a matter of hours, simply because Wall Street suddenly woke up to the reality that an algorithm could rewrite decades of legacy COBOL code before breakfast. SaaS middlemen are watching their business models turn to ash. Meanwhile, the companies training foundational large models are vacuuming up capital at a scale that defies gravity. OpenAI just casually swept up a $110 billion mega-round at a staggering $730 billion valuation, dragging SoftBank, Nvidia, and AWS into the fray merely to keep their server racks from melting down. AMD just snatched a massive order away from Meta. Nvidia remains the unshakeable god.

Every morning, we scroll through this panic-inducing tech news on our daily commute. Then, we walk into the office, sit down at our desks, and the business line leader walks over to ask you: why hasn’t the sales team updated the customer relationship management system for three straight weeks?

The **cognitive dissonance** is absolutely deafening. We seem to be only weeks away from an omniscient, omnipotent AI, yet internal enterprise workflows are still firmly stuck in the Stone Age. This is the stark, unvarnished dilemma facing the vast majority of enterprises today.

\*

Recently, I received a consultation request from a mid-to-large enterprise with highly complex operations. Their chief executive officer had incidentally stumbled upon a customer power map, covertly mapped out by a regional sales pod desperately trying to land a massive account.

> *As defined by Harvard Business School, a power map is a visual framework used to identify who holds influence and authority in a given situation, helping strategists navigate complex organizational dynamics.*

The chief executive officer was absolutely thrilled with the tool. He immediately issued a top-down mandate to roll this methodology out across the entire group, attempting to break down silos and integrate data across the sales operations center, the service operations center, and various independent business units to form a single, unified global view.

From the perspective of decision intelligence, this vision is practically bulletproof. Modern enterprise clients frequently purchase products across multiple business units simultaneously; they require intense upfront sales engagement and rely heavily on post-sales technical support. If information fractures anywhere along the line, the organization’s understanding of the customer becomes severely distorted. Yet, when this company actually attempted to build out this workflow, they slammed violently into two insurmountable walls.

**The first wall was the agonizing friction of internal alignment during the preparation phase.** Simply to standardize the data fields for this map, communication costs exploded exponentially. The head of the operations center had to be involved, followed by their executing team leads, and eventually even the frontline specialists doing the actual groundwork were forced to attend. Meanwhile, the IT director and their engineers had to be present to evaluate technical feasibility. The massive cross-functional span resulted in a chaotic tangle of information and completely irreconcilable demands. Teams spent weeks arguing endlessly in conference rooms, only to repeatedly report back up the chain for approval. The sheer management friction generated by establishing this structured workflow utterly drained the team’s momentum.

**The second wall was the maintenance death spiral. Even after they eventually compromised and deployed a highly complex form system, the subsequent daily data upkeep became an absolute operational disaster.** You simply cannot expect exhausted frontline sales representatives, who spend their days fighting in the trenches, to enthusiastically log into a clunky system late at night from their hotel rooms just to manually update subtle shifts in a client’s organizational hierarchy. The moment a handful of people began to slack off on their data entry, the timeliness and accuracy of the entire database instantly collapsed. Ultimately, this heavily engineered and massively expensive workflow deteriorated into an empty, useless shell.

So, caught in the middle of this tech hurricane, how are we actually supposed to tackle these two very real bottlenecks?

\*

A recent insight shared by Brian Flynn offers exactly the perspective we need to break this deadlock. He astutely points out that the entire history of sales has essentially been about capturing human attention. Billboards, landing pages, conference booths — they are all explicitly designed for humans who browse, compare, and inevitably get distracted. But Flynn is absolutely right: **AI agents don’t browse; they query.**

The traditional marketing playbook — relying on brand storytelling and emotional triggers — is completely useless on an AI. Its decision function is ruthlessly simple: Can you solve the problem? How fast? For how much? How reliable? Flynn notes that when an agent can discover a service, compare prices, and execute a call all within a single HTTP request, historically high transaction costs rapidly plummet to zero. And when the costs of search and evaluation approach zero, the default operating model for businesses undergoes a seismic shift.

Based on this, he arrives at a critical conclusion: we need to provide machine-readable APIs instead of merely trying to capture human attention.

If we internalize Flynn’s external commercial logic into our enterprise management, the answer becomes incredibly clear: the internal workflows we are painstakingly engineering today should absolutely no longer be designed just to generate pretty dashboards for executive review. Instead, they must be fundamentally built to silently accumulate high-quality, agent-readable data during daily operations.

Following this train of thought, AI agents execute a dimensional strike against the two aforementioned pain points.

**First, unstructured data ingestion completely obliterates the nightmare of internal alignment.** In the future, we really won’t need to lock people from multiple departments in a conference room to argue endlessly over what fields belong on a rigid form. Frontline sales reps will simply throw their rawest data , like an hour-long meeting recording, chat logs, or background info scraped from the web, straight into the internal AI knowledge base. Acting as a tireless, elite analyst, the agent will automatically pan for gold within this chaotic silt, dynamically piecing together the latest power relationships. Freed from the constraints of rigid forms, the upfront costs of cross-departmental communication drop practically to zero.

**Second, on-demand retrieval solves the continuous maintenance death spiral.** We will no longer have to force exhausted sales reps to painfully enter data into legacy systems late at night from their hotel rooms. Digital agents will act like invisible assistants, quietly lurking within compliant workflows, automatically parsing emails and meeting notes to update backend data in real-time. When other departments need to get up to speed on a client, they won’t have to dig through complex system menus; they can simply ask the AI, exactly as they would a veteran colleague, and instantly receive a precise answer.

However, this is exactly what keeps many managers up at night: it is crystal clear to all of us that this is the inevitable endgame. But in reality, current AI is still far from a fully hands-off “autopilot” state when it comes to flawless accuracy, long-term logical reasoning, and strict data privacy.

So, stranded in this transitional period between the old and the new, are we really just supposed to sit around and wait for the technology to mature on its own?

\*

In this disorienting transition period between the old and the new, the pragmatic actions of management must focus intensely on three highly specific levels:

**First, avoid the trap of boiling the ocean; instead, inject artificial intelligence awareness into the daily routine.** Do not attempt to roll out a massive, exorbitantly expensive enterprise-wide AI strategy all at once. Leaders should force their teams to get accustomed to using lightweight tools for basic insight transport and extraction. For instance, mandate that the sales team use off-the-shelf transcription and summarization tools to process lengthy customer meeting notes before logging the core takeaways into the system. Organizational muscle memory takes significant time to build. You must first let your employees get used to the basic cadence of collaborating with machines.

**Second, build a forward-deployed team that genuinely understands the business.** The single biggest mistake companies make right now is paying top dollar to hire external prompt engineers, only to completely isolate them from the muddy realities of the actual business. The correct approach is to handpick individuals from your own sales operations or business lines who truly grasp your decision intelligence logic and possess a high sensitivity to emerging technologies. Transform these internal experts into forward-deployed engineers. Their mandate is emphatically not to write foundational code. It is to pull up a chair, sit directly next to the sales representatives, observe exactly how painful it is for them to hunt for leads in the legacy system every day, and then utilize existing open-source plugins to surgically eradicate that specific pain point. They are the vital translators bridging raw technical capability with actual commercial demand.

**Third, use low-cost tools to validate the logic, rather than solidifying a heavy system.** Today, while the underlying technology is still rapidly shifting, absolutely do not spend fortunes on heavy, highly customized software development. When faced with a need like mapping out customer relationships, turn to the inexpensive zero-code or low-code tools readily available on the market. Spend a mere week or two running the core workflow logic at an extremely low financial risk. During this rapid prototyping process, our core objective is to force the business team to definitively answer one fundamental question: to confidently support our final commercial decisions, what specific, core pieces of information do we actually need to capture?

\*

![]()

When the inevitable day arrives that a vastly more mature, perhaps fundamentally unrecognizable, artificial intelligence agent completely rewrites the rules of the enterprise software market , perhaps no longer a legacy system with a clunky user interface, but an invisible, omnipresent decision engine , those pragmatic companies will be entirely ready. Having already meticulously figured out their core business logic and successfully accumulated vast reserves of **agent-readable data**, they will be able to seamlessly leap into the true AI era with near-zero organizational friction.

We must soberly recognize that the shelf life of technological tools is shrinking at an unprecedented rate. However, profound insights into commercial essence, a rigorously mapped internal decision chain, and high-quality business data assets will forever remain an enterprise’s most impregnable moat. We do not need to gamble on exactly how smart the next large language model will be. What we truly must do is ensure that when that brilliantly capable model finally descends, our enterprise actually possesses the structural APIs to converse with it.

Meanwhile, those who are currently paralyzed by tech vertigo, who stubbornly choose to do absolutely nothing but wait for the perfect, frictionless system to arrive, will ultimately become the digital relics of this era. They will find themselves permanently and silently buried under the avalanche of whatever revolutionary new model gets released on a random Tuesday next month. True decision intelligence will always belong to the pragmatists who keep laying down the tracks, even in the thickest of fogs.
