# AI Has Not Solved Software Engineering!

# AI Has Not Solved Software Engineering!

[Piyush Singh](/@piyush.singh.office?source=post_page---byline--81e660bbd5ea---------------------------------------)

4 min read·Mar 2, 2026

\--

![]()

There is a popular narrative circulating in tech circles that artificial intelligence has solved coding.

The argument is simple. If a model can generate thousands of lines of working code in seconds, then the core bottleneck of software engineering is gone. The rest is just scaling prompts.

It is a compelling story. It is also incomplete.

AI has dramatically reduced the cost of producing syntax. But syntax was never the true bottleneck.

Software engineering is not primarily about writing code. It is about thinking.

## The Easy Part: Writing Syntax

Once you understand a language, writing syntax becomes mechanical. It is pattern recognition and recall. Modern tools, especially those powered by AI, are extremely effective at this layer.

But beneath syntax lies a more difficult set of tasks:

-   Modeling the real problem correctly
-   Defining system boundaries
-   Designing data flow
-   Anticipating failure modes
-   Planning for scale and change
-   Making trade offs under constraints

These responsibilities do not disappear with code generation. In many cases, they become more important.

When a developer writes code by hand, they are not simply producing text. They are running a mental simulation of the system. They imagine how data moves through components. They consider edge cases. They sense where abstractions may break.

That friction is not waste. It is training.

When most implementation is outsourced to generative systems, that simulation risks being outsourced as well. Over time, skills can atrophy. Engineers become editors of artifacts that they did not deeply reason through.

The danger is not that AI writes code. The danger is that humans stop thinking through systems.

## The Hidden Cost of Cheap Implementation

Historically, implementation cost acted as a constraint.

If shipping a feature required two weeks of engineering effort, teams were forced to ask:

-   Is this worth building?
-   What must we cut?
-   What are the long term implications?
-   Is this idea coherent?

Scarcity enforced discipline.

When the marginal cost of generating a feature approaches zero, that discipline weakens. If a feature can be scaffolded in minutes, the temptation is to keep everything. Instead of killing mediocre ideas early, teams accumulate them.

This introduces a new class of risk: discipline collapse.

It does not show up immediately. The product appears richer. The roadmap moves faster. But months later, the maintenance burden grows:

-   Inconsistent abstractions
-   Duplicated logic
-   Poorly defined ownership
-   Increasing fragility

Cheap creation can produce expensive complexity.

Human constraints often improve product quality because they force prioritization and sharper thinking. Removing those constraints does not automatically produce better software. It can produce noise.

## Jevons Paradox and the Expansion of Software

There is also an economic pattern at play. When something becomes more efficient, total consumption often increases rather than decreases. This dynamic is known as Jevons Paradox.

Applied to software:

-   If code becomes cheaper to generate, more code will be created
-   More internal tools will be built
-   More automations will be deployed
-   More experiments will reach production

The surface area of software expands.

This does not simplify the ecosystem but will increase the complexity. Every new service must be monitored. Every integration must be secured. Every tool must be maintained.

Reducing the cost per feature can increase the total system burden.

This suggests that demand for engineers may not collapse but actually shift. There may be less emphasis on typing and more emphasis on architecture, systems thinking, and governance.

Efficiency at the micro level can increase complexity at the macro level.

## Market Signals Tell a Different Story

If AI had truly solved software engineering, labor markets would reflect that reality.

Yet companies building frontier AI systems continue to compete aggressively for experienced engineers. Firms across the industry emphasize sustained hiring of early career talent to avoid long term capability gaps.

This does not look like an industry preparing for the end of engineering. It looks like one adapting to new tools while still valuing human judgment.

There is also the phenomenon of AI washing, where layoffs are framed as automation driven even when the underlying causes include over hiring cycles, financial pressure, or strategic shifts. Blaming AI can be a convenient narrative.

The signals point to something more nuanced. AI changes the nature of work. It does not erase it.

## What Actually Changes

AI is extremely good at:

-   Generating boilerplate
-   Translating between frameworks
-   Prototyping quickly
-   Explaining unfamiliar code

These are powerful capabilities. They reduce friction and accelerate iteration.

But they do not answer questions like:

-   Should this feature exist?
-   What is the simplest viable abstraction?
-   What are the second order effects of this design choice?
-   How will this system behave under stress?

Those require judgment, context, and accountability.

As code generation becomes abundant, the relative value of restraint increases. Knowing what not to build becomes more important than ever.

The competitive advantage shifts upward, from typing to thinking.

## The Real Bottleneck Was Never Code

Software engineering has always been about:

-   Managing complexity
-   Making decisions under uncertainty
-   Designing for evolution
-   Balancing speed and stability

AI can accelerate implementation. It can even propose designs. But it does not own the consequences of those designs.

Humans do.

The risk is not that AI will replace engineers. The risk is that engineers will mistake generation for understanding.

If we treat AI as a thinking substitute instead of a thinking amplifier, we may end up with more software, yet not better software.

The future belongs to engineers who can leverage AI aggressively while preserving deep systems reasoning. Those who can generate quickly and still think rigorously.

Coding was never the hard part but the thinking still is.
