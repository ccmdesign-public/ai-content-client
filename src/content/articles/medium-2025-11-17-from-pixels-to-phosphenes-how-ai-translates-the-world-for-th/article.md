### **When Technology Meets Human Vision**

In 2024, Sheila Irvine, a 70 year old woman who had lost her central vision over 30 years due to advanced macular degeneration (AMD), read a book again. This miracle was made possible by the PRIMA implant, a paper thin chip surgically placed behind her retina at Moorfields Eye Hospital in London. This chip converts light into tiny electrical impulses that her brain interprets as patterns of light. “It is out of this world” these were her words after being able to read book again.\[[BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c0qpz39jpj7o)\]

Sheila’s story marks a milestone in bionic vision, a field working to restore functional sight to millions. Clinical trials show that legally blind patients can regain partial sight and can read more lines on an eye chart. But the true challenge isn’t just hardware or better cameras but teaching artificial intelligence (AI) to see what a blind person needs to see.

This is where computer vision meets neuroscience, where seeing becomes a computational challenge.

### **The Language of Light: Understanding Phosphenes**

Phosphenes are the universal pixels of bionic vision. If you press gently on your closed eyes, you can see the stars, circles or geometric shapes swirling in the darkness. These are the phosphenes, your brain’s interpretation of physical stimulation to the visual system, not images formed by light.

Electrical pulses stimulate the retina or visual cortex, producing bright flashes of light which the brain assembles into shapes, objects and even faces. But unlike the millions of photoreceptors in a natural retina creating a high definition image, current implants stimulate only hundreds or thousands of points. Imagine recreating a vibrant 4k film on a blurry handful of pixels screen. This is where AI becomes absolutely essential.

### **Hardware Foundations: Capturing and Delivering Sight**

Modern bionic vision systems like Argus II and Orion operate on a kind of similar architecture:

-   A small camera mounted on glasses captures the scene
-   An external processor converts images into electrical stimulation patterns
-   Wireless transmitters send these signals to an implanted receiver
-   The brain interprets these signals as phosphenes

Each electrode produces a pixel of light. So more electrodes means higher resolution. Yet millions of electrodes to match natural vision remains unfeasible today. Commercial systems only have dozens to hundreds of electrodes compared to the retina’s 100 million photoreceptors. So we need AI to work within these limits intelligently.

![Fundus photographs showing an electrode array surgically implanted on a patient’s retina. Each electrode delivers controlled electrical pulses that evoke phosphenes, forming the basic “pixels” of artificial vision. Source: Retina Today “The Next Generation of Artificial Vision” (2025)]()

### **The Electrical Engineering Challenge: Precision at the Neural Interface**

Delivering electrical pulses to neurons needs crucial balance:

-   Current amplitude needs to be just enough to evoke phosphenes without causing any type of damage
-   Pulse width, frequency and charge needs to be carefully controlled
-   AI constantly adjusts these parameters for optimizing visual quality and energy use

Power is another major constraint. The external processor must operate efficiently for long periods. Modern innovations in low power AI computing and dynamic processing help extend battery life ensuring a great user experience.

### **The AI Translation Challenge: Seeing What Matters**

A camera usually captures millions of pixels but a bionic eye can display only a fraction of it. AI must decide “What does this person need to see right now?” AI acts as a filter by deciding what matters most:

-   Edge Detection: Outlines of objects matter most at low resolution. Algorithms like Canny edge detector highlight the critical contours for shape and boundary recognition.
-   Object Recognition: Neural networks such as YOLO (You Only Look Once) identify important entities (people, cars, doors) & prioritize their brightness
-   Depth Perception: Monocular networks estimate 3D distance cues from 2D images, providing essential awareness for navigation
-   Text recognition: Specialized AI enhances text regions in low resolution, often combining with audio for reading aid.

### **Facing Forward: Challenges & Opportunities**

The path ahead is filled with promise and complexity. Researchers are pushing toward cortical implants for stimulating the brain directly and the resolution will increase dramatically. Meanwhile AI grows smarter, adapting with users’ need and environments with greater nuance and precision.

Yet amidst this technological progress, the field has learned a sobering lesson: innovation is worthless without commitment.

### **Ethical Imperatives in Bionic Vision**

Ethical problems in this field aren’t just about the surgery. They involve an important promise which is to ensure these devices remain sustainable and supported forever. The Argus II system shows what happens when the promise is broken.

In 2020 the maker of Argus II, Second Sight Medical Products ceased operations and stopped providing medical support. Because of this approximately 350 patients worldwide were left with devices that could no longer be serviced or repaired. Bionic eyes are complex electronic systems that require ongoing manufacturer support, software update and technical maintenance. So when batteries fail or software malfunctions, users face losing their restored vision and the worst thing is they have absolutely nowhere to turn for help. \[[IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/bionic-eye-obsolete)\]

The Argus II legacy serves as a reminder that restoring sight is not just a surgical achievement but it’s a lifelong commitment that the industry must honor. Beyond sustainability, the field must tackle other challenges like ensuring accessibility across socioeconomic barriers, overcoming algorithmic bias and maintaining privacy in devices that process visual data.

The future of bionic vision demands not only brilliant innovation but also infrastructure, ethics and commitment required to sustain the sight we restore.

### **Conclusion: The Dawn of New Vision**

Bionic vision is more than a medical breakthrough. It fundamentally changes our understanding of sight itself. The journey from pixels to phosphenes, from raw light sensations to meaningful perception marks a huge evolution in how we understand sight. We have bridged biology and artificial intelligence to transform seeing from a passive biological function to an active computational process. Sheila Irvine’s story reminds us that vision is more than sight, it’s perception, meaning and connection. But technical achievement alone isn’t enough. It requires sustainable support systems, ethical frameworks and a long term commitment to patients. We stand at the dawn of a new vision horizon. The future isn’t about choosing between natural vision and artificial systems, it’s about creating a new form of perception where human consciousness and machine intelligence work together. And for millions living in darkness, that future can’t come soon enough.
