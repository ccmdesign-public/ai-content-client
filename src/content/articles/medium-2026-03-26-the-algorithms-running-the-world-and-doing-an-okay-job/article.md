# The Algorithms Running the World (And Doing an Okay Job)

Many conversations about Artificial Intelligence swing between two extremes- either breathless utopian excitement, or post-apocalyptic dread (with ChatGPT being the apocalypse).

![Utopia or Apocalypse?](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*HXGGjJnKSbWs1IIUiRhG6g.png)

I’ve found myself veering towards the latter, and I asked myself WHY? Then I went onto Reddit. The first thing I see is a 358-comment-long thread on the reasons:

> ***“People hate AI because we live in a hyper-capitalist society that will use AI to turbo-charge enshittification, reinforce systematic biases, and bolster the police state,****” said older-than-yesterday.*

(Btw, the next sentence says “This isn’t fear-mongering, it’s happening now…” Suspicious framing…)

That was followed by a convincing list of eerie use cases that would make the directors of The Matrix proud. For the sake of preventing confirmation bias in anything that I say and write, I did a little more thinking.

I believe it’s essential that we discuss the negative impact of AI on society. The regulations that must be implemented, corporate greed, the latest ban on [**Anthropic in the Department of War**](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/u-s-governments-ban-on-anthropic-looks-like-punishment-attempt-judge-says-2ff98fe3), and so many more tough yet important conversations.

> Yet, in a world plagued by **Doomers, Headline Stress Disorder (A fancy word for news anxiety), Climate Nihilism, Techno-Pessimism,** and many more, it’s important that we take some time to switch off the news, grab some **caffeine**, and read about the positives.

Some AI tools are genuinely, measurably making the world better. Sure, some are making it worse, but isn’t that typical of technology?

Here are the top 3 that I think are changing the world.

**Protein, Evolution, and a whole lot of Science**

[**Google’s DeepMind Alpha Fold**](https://deepmind.google/science/alphafold/) solved a problem that has riddled biologists for 50 years, **predicting how proteins fold**, in a single breakthrough.

![Protein Folding](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*tgWtkeV4xzhedl8DmAL9qw.png)

Amino acids are linked together in a long chain to form a 3D protein structure. If you’re on the internet, you know about the benefits of protein (thank you, Gym Bro’s??)- essential components that compose our bodies and regulate life processes. [**Read this to understand how it works.**](https://www.nobelprize.org/uploads/2024/11/fig2_ke_en_24-5.pdf)

Predicting the structure of these proteins is challenging, and it’s essential that they fold correctly. Incorrect folding can cause diseases like **Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s**, and obtaining this knowledge means a significant advancement in **drug development.**

Google’s DeepMind Alpha has outperformed the prediction accuracy of previous models. Researchers now have access to over **200 million protein structures** to support their research. For free. The implications this has on **cancer research, antibiotic resistance, and rare diseases** are staggering!

**Understanding Symptoms on the Ringy-Thingy \*phone\***

Most people in wealthy countries take access to a doctor for granted. You feel unwell, book an appointment, and drive your car to someone with a medical degree. In rural areas, there’s a major shortage of healthcare workers. In third-world countries, many physicians are trained, but nobody stays in the country. Most doctors and nurses opt for high-paying jobs in other countries. Those with illness and disease in rural areas experience long travel, expenses, and wait times in search of healthcare

![Issues with Rural Healthcare](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*qstnmJ6KmeEoNbunjHIHEQ.jpeg)

[**Ada’s**](https://ada.com/) trying to change all of that. It’s a mobile app that assesses symptoms to help inform decisions. It can help you **track symptoms over time and share them with your doctor.** [They save time for both doctors and patients, according to an NIH study.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10595702/)

It can also help identify potential causes and suggest next steps, such as managing symptoms at home or seeing a doctor. It isn’t a diagnosis, but in a **world infiltrated with misinformation about health,** it’s a useful tool. It’s not a replacement for actual healthcare infrastrucutre, but for a mother in rural Nigeria trying to work out if her child’s fever is malaria or somehting else, not perfect is still helpful.

**Weather and Deforestation**

[**Google DeepMind’s GraphCast**](https://deepmind.google/blog/graphcast-ai-model-for-faster-and-more-accurate-global-weather-forecasting/) can now produce 10-day global weather forecasts in under a minute with accuracy that outperforms traditional forecasting systems that require enormous computational infrastructure to run. It’s **revolutionizing weather predictions,** making them faster and more efficient.

![Microsoft’s AI for Earth Program](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/900/1*kVqupxT5amqOKjPcLldugA.jpeg)

Here’s another one- [**Microsoft’s AI for Earth programme**](https://news.microsoft.com/apac/features/ai-for-earth-helping-save-the-planet-with-data-science/) has funded hundreds of projects using machine learning to **monitor deforestation in real time via satellite imagery, track wildlife populations, and model carbon sequestration in forests and oceans.** The monitoring problem in climate science has always been enormous; the planet is large, and humans are not. This isn’t solving climate change, but it’s showing us the problem, which is a prerequisite for fixing it.

**My Point**

Sure, there are issues. It’s ironic that an algorithm that’s notorious for carbon emissions is helping us solve climate change. Ada might be innacurate, and people might use the tool as a diagnosis. Protein- oh, protein’s always good (even if it’s 3 times the recommended amount).

There are issues- but what determines if AI is good for the world, isn’t really about the technology. It’s about the questions that we ask before we release it.

Here are three that I think are prerequisites to building and launching an AI software: (Try answering them about ChatGPT)

> **What are we trying to solve?**

> **Who gets harmed if this goes wrong?**

> **Who is responsible for the output?**

The question now is whether the humans nominally in charge of them are asking these questions.

Spoiler alert: often, not enough.
