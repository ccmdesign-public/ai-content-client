# AI (Coding Agents) Brain Fry

#### This month, the tech world is buzzing about a new term: **“AI Brain Fry.”** It started with a study in *Harvard Business Review* (March 2026) by researchers from BCG and UC Riverside. As someone who uses coding agents daily, Would like to share my take on this.

![Screenshot / Collage of Articles](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*-yBenXoSxhnbFYdoSzElVQ.png)

Coding agents have changed the way I work for the better, but I’m starting to notice some drawbacks the more I use them. I’d like to share these issues and how to navigate them so we can keep the experience net-positive.

### 1\. I’m no longer a Coder; I’m an Air Traffic Controller

The *HBR* study ([When Using AI Leads to “Brain Fry”](https://hbr.org/2026/03/when-using-ai-leads-to-brain-fry)) makes a point that hit me hard: **Monitoring is more exhausting than doing.**

This morning, I didn’t “write” code. I spent sixty minutes staring at my app’s logs as they blurred past. **Claude Code** was in the pilot’s seat — diagnosing a race condition, proposing fixes, and writing diffs. My entire job was to play a high-stakes game of “Spot the Error.” While fixing my application’s one tab on the virtual machine, the local environment broke. Once both were stable, another tab stopped loading correctly. I couldn’t just drop the logs and say “figure it out” — because the AI would fix one thing and silently break another. I had to read the logs myself, understand what was actually wrong, and give precise enough direction that the next fix didn’t unravel the last one. That’s the job now: not coding, but knowing enough to steer something that moves faster than you can fully track.

![(Generated by Gemini)](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*qcXZ8Qq_puk1fKXnFakM5A.png)

When I’m coding manually, I’m in **Flow**. When I’m using agents, I’m in **Hyper-vigilance**. I can’t blink because the AI might:

-   Fix one timeout but ignore the dependent threshold.
-   Swap semantically different terms because they “look” cleaner.
-   Confidently explain a root cause that is logically impossible.

Today, the agent was right. But I only know that because I didn’t look away for a second.

That is the shift from **Maker to Air Traffic Controller**. The plane flies itself 99% of the time, but you have to burn 100% of your mental energy waiting for the 1% moment where it fails. You never know which moment that will be and that uncertainty is what fries the brain.

### 2\. **“High-Velocity Win”** (The Dopamine) with the **“Agentic Wheel-Spinning”** (The Crash).

![(Generated by Gemini)](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*dhUO9kMEK2QNG503LXkAsA.png)

#### **The “Slot Machine” Workflow:** Claude Code vs. Instagram Reels

There is a chemical reason we stay glued to the screen while these agents work, and it’s uncomfortably similar to scrolling Instagram Reels.

When you’re using Claude Code or Cursor’s Composer, you’re engaging in a “High-Velocity Feedback Loop.” You hit commands, the terminal flashes, the diffs stream in, and boom the bug is fixed. That instant gratification triggers a massive dopamine hit. Unlike scrolling social media, you’re actually accomplishing something. You’re shipping. It feels like a superpower.

But this creates a dangerous psychological dependency. We start chasing that “rush” of the next successful prompt.

#### The Dopamine Crash

The “Brain Fry” turns toxic when that loop breaks. What happens when the agent spins its wheels for 32 minutes? *(happend with me last week)*

![Claude code screenshot](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/619/1*lnOALerRFIr9AxEi_wUX4g.png)

-   You’ve prompted Claude three times.
-   You’ve watched it refactor the same function into three different (but equally broken) versions.
-   You’ve stared at the logs for 1,200 seconds with 100% vigilance.

When the agent fails after you’ve invested that much “oversight energy,” the crash is brutal. You’ve burned the mental calories of an Air Traffic Controller but received zero dopamine in return. You’re left with all the exhaustion and none of the “win.”

This is where the “Fry” becomes “Burnout.” You aren’t just tired; you’re frustrated because you’ve outsourced your problem-solving to a black box that just let you down. You’ve spent 20 minutes “watching the plane” only for it to land in the wrong city. Now, you have to take the controls yourself — but your brain is already cooked from the 20 minutes of hyper-vigilance.

### 3\. The “Four-Tool Cliff” is Real

The BCG research ([When Using AI Leads to “Brain Fry”](https://hbr.org/2026/03/when-using-ai-leads-to-brain-fry)) found that productivity falls off a cliff once you use a **fourth AI tool**. My typical session looks like this:

*Cursor managing the codebase, Claude Code handling autocompletes and edits, Claude 3.5 open in a separate tab for architecture questions and drafting stakeholder messages, Copilot/Glean summarizing whatever requirements landed/to-dos on Teams/Slack, and bunch of Internal AI tools.*

Juggling these four, my brain becomes the **integration layer**. I’m stitching together logic from four different “personalities.” The articles call this “context-switching,” but it feels more like my brain is a CPU running at 100% usage just to keep the tools synced.

### 4\. The Death of “Micro-Breaks”

*Morning Brew (*[AI Can Fry Our Brains, Even When It’s Helping](https://www.morningbrew.com/stories/2026/03/14/ai-can-fry-our-brains-even-when-it-s-helping)) highlighted how AI removes the “slow work.”

> “a dozen browser tabs open in my head, all fighting for attention.” — Senior engineering manager, mentioned in the above article

In the old *(I am being dramatic, lol, just a few months ago)* days, writing boilerplate or a simple for loop gave my brain a 30-second breather to think about the *next* step. Now, with agents, that "digestive time" is gone. The code appears instantly. I am forced to make **high-level architectural decisions** back-to-back-to-back. I’ve traded physical typing effort for a 10x increase in "decision density," and my biological hardware simply wasn't built for that pace.

### 5\. The “Stochastic Noise” Headache

*Forbes* ([The Real Truth About That Onerous ‘AI Brain Fry’ That Everyone Is Talking About](https://www.forbes.com/sites/lanceeliot/2026/03/16/the-real-truth-about-that-onerous-ai-brain-fry-that-everyone-is-talking-about/)) mentions the mental toll of filtering out “AI-isms.” For me, the “fry” starts with the **“Good Enough” Trap**. Every time I prompt Claude to refactor a component, I’m forced into a relentless editorial role. I have to look at the diff and ask: *Is this actually better, or is it just different?* Filtering through this “stochastic noise” — the endless variations of AI-generated suggestions — leads to a specific, heavy kind of decision fatigue. By 3:00 PM, my ability to judge code quality is shot. I’m experiencing the “cognitive hangover” the HBR researchers warned about.

But it’s not just the big architectural decisions that drain you. It’s the **Micro-Approval Loop**.

Every action the agent takes surfaces a prompt:

-   *Allow this?* **Yes.** *Allow this?* **Yes.** *Allow this?* **Yes.** You’re clicking through a hundred micro-approvals an hour. Individually, they feel trivial. Collectively, they are exhausting. Each one demands just enough “active” attention that you can never fully let go or sink into deep work. You are permanently tethered to the “Accept” button.

This constant, low-grade confirmation loop keeps your nervous system in a state of high alert it was never designed to sustain. It’s not “coding” in the traditional sense; it’s a marathon of micro-judgments that leaves your brain feeling like it’s been vibrating for eight hours straight.

### My Survival Strategy

If we’re going to survive this “Agentic Era” without burning out, we have to change how we work. Based on the 2026 data and my own “fry” levels, I’ve started doing three things:

-   **The Three-Agent Rule:** I never have more than three AI interfaces active. If I open a new one, one has to go.
-   **One thing at a time**: When working on feature addition / code optimization, I pick one thing and work on that, push the change, merge it, and then work on a new issue.
-   **Forced Analog Time:** I’ve started sketching logic on a physical whiteboard again. It forces my brain to slow down to “human speed” and gives the “fry” a chance to cool.
-   **Respecting the 10%:** The study suggests we are most effective when AI is only **7–10%** of our day. I’m trying to move the “heavy lifting” back to my own brain for the core logic, using agents only for the “drudgery” they were actually promised for.
-   **Invest in Agents, Skills, and Plugins Upfront:
     —** I’ve started building custom agents and skills for the workflows I run repeatedly, so instead of manually steering the AI through the same 10-step debugging sequence every time, I describe it once, encode it, and let the agent follow a known-good path. It shifts me from reactive oversight to proactive configuration. The cognitive load doesn’t disappear, but it moves to a moment when my brain is fresh — setup time — rather than burning it in real-time while the plane is in the air.
     — Tools like Claude Code’s Superpowers skills, brainstorming before you build, systematic-debugging before you guess, verification-before-completion before you ship — encode the oversight process itself, so you’re not reinventing your own judgment call every single time. The cognitive load doesn’t disappear, but it moves to setup time, when your brain is fresh, instead of burning in real-time while the plane is in the air.

**AI might be 10x faster, but my brain is still 1x.** If I don’t manage the “Fry,” the 10x developer dream is going to end in a 0x burnout.
