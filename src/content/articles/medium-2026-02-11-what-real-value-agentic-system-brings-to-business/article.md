> *Agentic systems don’t create value by being more autonomous — they create value by operating at the same abstraction level where humans currently connect, interpret, and decide across isolated workflows.*

### Step 1: GenAI-in-workflow (capability-driven value)

GenAI’s first-order value does not come from autonomy. It comes from **cognitive capability**.

-   Semantic I/O (unstructured → structured → unstructured)
-   Flexible context windows
-   Reasoning over ambiguity

In practice, this means:

-   Replacing brittle, task-specific models with LLMs inside existing workflows
-   Reshaping workflows so GenAI becomes the reasoning substrate rather than a narrow component

At this stage, **the model itself is the product**. No “agency” is required — just better cognition per token. This is why GenAI adoption has progressed fastest in areas like summarization, classification, enrichment, and decision support. The value comes directly from the model’s representational power, not from orchestration or autonomy.

### Step 2: Agents (but not the way people think)

This is where the narrative often breaks down. Most business problems do **not** naturally map to fully autonomous agents acting end-to-end. What we see instead in real systems are:

-   routers
-   tool selectors,
-   workflow orchestrators.

These are useful, but incremental. They don’t create step-function value because:

-   domain logic already exists in downstream systems,
-   orchestration ≠ understanding,
-   autonomy without judgment is just faster plumbing.

If “agentic” simply means deciding which tool to call next, then the upside is limited. We already know how to build reliable workflows. Wrapping them in an agent abstraction doesn’t automatically make them smarter.

### Where the real value actually emerges

The real value of agentic systems appears at a different level — not the workflow layer, but the **human coordination layer**.

In real organizations:

-   teams are semi-isolated,
-   workflows encode *local* objectives,
-   cross-workflow meaning is carried by humans (meetings, emails, intuition).

This is the layer where context is synthesized, tradeoffs are debated, and decisions are framed. And this is precisely where agents can matter most.

A high-value agent is not: *“Which tool should I call next?”*

It is:

-   **Connecting** signals across silos
-   **Explaining** why something matters in *business language*
-   **Analyzing** tradeoffs across objectives,
-   **Suggesting decisions**, not executing them blindly

In other words:

> *Agents are valuable when they* sit beside humans*, not when they replace cron jobs.*
