## ✅ 1. Reasoning models converge

**My prediction ([more context here](https://www.whytryai.com/i/154384487/reasoning-models-converge-in-performance)):**

> *By the end of 2025, reasoning models from at least three other players will perform on par with or better than OpenAI’s o3.*

**Reality:**

This one’s pretty clear-cut.

When I wrote the prediction, o3 was a yet-to-be-released SOTA reasoner, but “[test-time compute](https://huggingface.co/blog/Kseniase/testtimecompute)” was clearly becoming the go-to paradigm. So, it was only a matter of time before other major players released their own versions.

And they did.

Here’s the latest snapshot from [Artificial Analysis](https://artificialanalysis.ai/leaderboards/models):

![Source: Artificial Analysis LLM Leaderboard](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XG02!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb6497129-5b34-490b-8938-74f1aee54056_1543x873.png)

While o3 still holds up,[1](https://www.whytryai.com/p/ai-predictions-2025#footnote-1-179390344) models from four different OpenAI competitors score higher:

-   Anthropic (Claude Opus 4.5)
    
-   Google (Gemini 3 Pro)
    
-   DeepSeek (DeepSeek V3.2)
    
-   Kimi (Kimi K2 thinking)
    

So we can safely mark this one as correct.

**Verdict:** Accurate ✅

## **🟨 2. AI video goes mainstream**

**My prediction ([more context here](https://www.whytryai.com/i/154384487/ai-video-goes-mainstream)):**

> *By the end of 2025, a major movie—either released or announced—will feature at least some scenes made entirely using text-to-video models.*

**Reality:**

This one’s iffy.

On the one hand, there’s no doubt that AI video went *very* mainstream this year.

Hell, 90% of your social media feed is just AI slop clips at this stage (thanks, [Sora 2](https://www.whytryai.com/p/sora-2)).

In July, the world’s first fully AI-generated “actor” Tilly Norwood [made a splash](https://www.leagueoffilmmakers.com/meet-tilly-norwood-the-first-ai-actor/?).

We’re seeing more and more articles like these:

On the other hand, if we stick to the letter of my prediction, I can’t find any “*major movie*” that features scenes made “*entirely using text-to-video models*.”

The closest example is the [upcoming OpenAI-backed short film “Critterz,”](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2025-09-09/ai-animated-film-debut-next-year) set to debut at the Cannes Film Festival in 2026. Hardly a massive blockbuster.

But since my prediction was directionally correct and AI video truly hit the mainstream this year, I’ll award myself a “mixed” rating. Because if I don’t, who will?

**Verdict:** Mixed 🟨

## ✅ **3\. Actually useful AI agents**

**My prediction ([more context here](https://www.whytryai.com/i/154384487/actually-useful-ai-agents)):**

> *By the end of 2025, an AI agent will be able to reliably automate at least three common, well-defined workflows in business software.*

**Reality:**

This is an easy win. My prediction was a tad conservative, now that I think about it.

Articles by the *[Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2025/11/ai-agents-arent-ready-for-consumer-facing-work-but-they-can-excel-at-internal-processes)* and the [World Economic Forum](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/05/how-ai-agents-are-driving-the-administrative-revolution/) specifically point out how AI agents aren’t always consistent for customer-facing tasks but are already reliably automating repetitive, administrative internal processes.

We have agents for [helpdesk automation](https://www.serval.com/), [email handling](https://www.shortwave.com/), [customer relationship management](https://www.hubspot.com/products/artificial-intelligence), and many more.

Most AI companies also launched their own versions of agent automation and agent creation tools, with varying degrees of complexity.

So it looks like we’ve easily passed the rather low “three common workflows” bar.

**Verdict:** Accurate ✅

## ✅ **5. AI tutors are formally integrated**

**My prediction ([more context here](https://www.whytryai.com/i/154384487/ai-tutors-are-formally-integrated)):**

> *By the end of 2025, a top-tier university will use an AI tutor to scale an accredited degree program and make it accessible for remote students from around the world.*

**Reality:**

I initially gave this one a “mixed” score.

But after some digging, I found at least two examples that suggest I wasn’t too far off, actually:

-   Penn Engineering (Ivy League, [#17 engineering school in the US](https://www.usnews.com/best-graduate-schools/top-engineering-schools/university-of-pennsylvania-02156).)
    
    -   Launched [“Jean,” a 24/7 AI Tutor](https://online.seas.upenn.edu/uncategorized/penn-engineering-ai-tutor-jean/), in May 2025, explicitly capable of helping “graduate students in different time zones stay on track and receive immediate feedback and guidance.”
        
-   The University of Texas at Austin ([#1 Public University in Texas, #7 in the US](https://news.utexas.edu/2025/09/23/ut-ranks-as-no-1-public-university-in-texas-no-7-nationally-in-u-s-news-world-report-rankings/).)
    
    -   Launched “UT Sage,” a homegrown “[AI Tutor Platform](https://tech.utexas.edu/news/ut-austin-and-aws-launch-ut-sage-ut-austins-homegrown-ai-tutor-platform)” in July 2025 that offers students “on-demand, course-specific support that complements classroom instruction and reinforces foundational knowledge.”
        

As an aside, Georgia Tech apparently had a basic AI teaching assistant called Jill Watson [since way back in 2016](https://pe.gatech.edu/blog/meet-jill-watson-georgia-techs-first-ai-teaching-assistant), and overhauled it [using OpenAI’s models this year](https://research.gatech.edu/georgia-techs-jill-watson-outperforms-chatgpt-real-classrooms).

So I feel like this one holds up, but I’m open to being challenged.

**Verdict:** Accurate ✅

## ❌ 6. Model transparency becomes mandatory

**My prediction ([more context here](https://www.whytryai.com/i/154384487/model-transparency-becomes-mandatory)):**

> *By the end of 2025, we’ll have a law that requires AI companies to publish “nutritional labels” for models of a certain size or capability, specifying things like energy consumption, data sources, etc.*

**Reality:**

I think it’s safe to say this didn’t happen.

If anything, we moved in the opposite direction, at least in the US. The executive order “[Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/)” not only sets up a lax, “minimally burdensome national standard” for AI companies but also bans states from introducing their own AI regulations.

In fact, the executive order goes directly after any identified state laws that “compel AI developers or deployers to disclose or report information,” calling such laws “onerous.”

In the EU, things are a bit closer to my prediction. Under the AI Act, the “[Guidelines on obligations for General-Purpose AI providers](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/guidelines-obligations-general-purpose-ai-providers)” state the following:

> *“Providers of general-purpose AI models must \[…\] publish a sufficiently detailed summary of the content used for training the model.”*
> 
> \[AND\]
> 
> *“Providers must draw up and maintain comprehensive technical documentation for authorities. This includes information about the model’s architecture, training process, training, testing, and validation data, computational resources, and energy consumption. This documentation must be made available to the AI Office upon request, which may also act on behalf of national competent authorities.”*

That sounds very nutrition-labely. At the same time, this isn’t a strict public-facing disclosure requirement but a “have your documentation in order in case we want to check it” kind of deal.

All in all, I don’t feel this prediction deserves even partial credit.

**Verdict:** Wrong **❌**

## ✅ 7. Google dethrones OpenAI

**My prediction ([more context here](https://www.whytryai.com/i/154384487/google-dethrones-openai)):**

> *By the end of 2025, Google, not OpenAI, will be widely seen as the dominant player in generative AI.*

**Reality:**

Look, we all know that “ChatGPT” is to chatbots what “Google” (still) is to traditional search engines. OpenAI’s mindshare among the mainstream audience is real.

But when it comes to both metrics and the evolution of general sentiment, the shift toward Google has been hard to miss.

First, the rankings.

Google now has…

-   The world’s #1 large language model ([Artificial Analysis metrics](https://artificialanalysis.ai/leaderboards/models) and [LM Arena real-world user ratings](https://lmarena.ai/leaderboard/text).)
    
-   The world’s #2 text-to-image model ([Artificial Analysis metrics](https://artificialanalysis.ai/image/leaderboard/text-to-image) and [LM Arena users](https://lmarena.ai/leaderboard/text-to-image).). It was #1 but just got overtaken by OpenAI’s new “[GPT Image 1.5](https://openai.com/index/new-chatgpt-images-is-here/).”
    
-   The world’s best video model (in both [text-to-video](https://lmarena.ai/leaderboard/text-to-video) and [image-to-video](https://lmarena.ai/leaderboard/image-to-video).)
