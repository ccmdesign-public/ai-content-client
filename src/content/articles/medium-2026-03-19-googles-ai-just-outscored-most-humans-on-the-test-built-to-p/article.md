# Google’s AI Just Outscored Most Humans on the Test Built to Prove It Couldn’t Think

#### *The benchmark’s creator designed it to expose AI hype. The machine scored higher than the average human.*

![The machine scored 77%. The average human scored 60%. The creator of the test says neither number means what you think. Made By Author.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*1aSttMxsMmI3vTCTUv8DZg.jpeg)

A year ago, every frontier AI model on Earth scored below 5% on ARC-AGI-2, a reasoning benchmark specifically built to be impossible for machines. OpenAI’s o3 crashed from 75% on the previous version to 4%. GPT-4.5 scored zero. Claude scored zero.

Last month, Google’s Gemini 3.1 Pro scored 77.1%. The average human scores 60%.

### The Test That Was Supposed to End the Debate

In 2019, François Chollet published a paper that changed how the AI field argues about intelligence. His claim was blunt: intelligence is not what you know. It is how efficiently you figure out what you don’t know.

**“Intelligence isn’t knowing the answer. It’s figuring out the answer to something you’ve never seen before.”**

He built ARC-AGI to prove it. Picture a Raven’s Progressive Matrices test, but for AI: visual pattern puzzles you cannot memorize, cannot poison with training data, and can only crack by spotting abstract rules in real time. “You cannot memorize the solution programs in advance,” Chollet told Dwarkesh Patel in 2024. “You have to synthesize a new solution program on the fly for each new task.”

When ARC-AGI-2 launched in March 2025, the wreckage was immediate. OpenAI’s o3 plummeted from 75.7% on the first version to roughly 4%. The 2024 Kaggle competition winners cratered from 53.5% to 3%.

Every frontier model in the world failed it.

Here’s where it gets uncomfortable. The benchmark was calibrated against 400 human participants in controlled testing sessions. Average test-takers, without any training, scored 60%. A panel of 10 experts approached 100%.

This is not a test where humans breeze through and AI flounders. Most humans struggle with it too. AI, until recently, couldn’t even start.

So if this test was built to be impossible for machines, what changed in eleven months?

### From Zero to Seventy-Seven in Eleven Months

The timeline tells a story nobody predicted:

-   **March 2025:** Every frontier model scores below 5%.
-   **November 2025:** Poetiq breaks 50%.
-   **February 19, 2026:** Gemini 3.1 Pro hits 77.1%.

For eight months, almost nothing moved. Then the dam broke. Poetiq cleared 50% first, pulling off in eight months what the field assumed would take years. Once someone proves the barrier isn’t real, everyone runs faster.

Jeff Dean dropped the number on X, February 19. Google described 3.1 Pro as carrying “upgraded core intelligence,” the same engine powering its Deep Think reasoning system. The jump: from its predecessor’s 31.1% to 77.1%, a 148% improvement in roughly three months.

**“The benchmark was designed to be the finish line. It turned out to be a speed bump.”**

But Chollet would tell you something the headline can’t. Solving the test isn’t the same as being intelligent. What matters is *how* you solve it.

### Skill Is Not Intelligence

Not everyone who saw 77.1% celebrated. The man who built the test has spent years drawing a line most of the AI industry would rather pretend doesn’t exist.

> *“They’re confusing skill and intelligence.”*

Chollet’s distinction cuts deep. A model that scores 77% burning through massive compute demonstrates skill. A human who scores 60% in under three minutes, at $5 a task, might demonstrate something closer to actual intelligence.

The gap between those two isn’t a technicality. It’s the whole argument.

According to ARC Prize Foundation data, OpenAI’s o3-low spends approximately $200 per task. Human expert panels solve 100% of tasks at around $17 per task. Twelve times cheaper. Perfect accuracy.

You’re probably thinking: but 77% is still higher than 60%, regardless of cost. Fair instinct. It runs into a wall.

Chollet’s “Kaleidoscope Hypothesis” proposes that the universe keeps generating novelty, but all of it recombines from a small set of conceptual atoms. Intelligence means spotting those atoms and reassembling them in unfamiliar situations. Not matching patterns on the surface. Building new solutions from scratch.

(There’s a genuine, unresolved tension here that the research community hasn’t settled: whether efficiency is a *component* of intelligence or the *definition* of it. Chollet treats it as definitional. Not everyone agrees, and the disagreement runs deeper than terminology.)

The human baseline has its own problems. A detailed LessWrong analysis found the 60% average likely understates true human ability. Participants worked 90-minute sessions with a modest $5-per-solve incentive, a setup that rewards skipping hard problems rather than grinding through them.

Some tasks contain explicit ambiguity by design, requiring two guesses to disambiguate. The 77-versus-60 comparison holds up factually. Structurally, it’s rougher than it looks.

**“Chollet built the test to end the hype. Google used it to restart it.”**

Google proved a machine can solve reasoning puzzles. Chollet’s framework says that’s not the same as reasoning. Both sides have data. Neither is bluffing.

Definitions are where the real fight lives.

### The Benchmark Treadmill

The Imbue team pushed Gemini 3.1 Pro to 95.1% using an evolutionary code harness, and almost nobody noticed. That silence matters. It signals something the 77.1% headline does not: the benchmark may already be approaching saturation through engineering rather than intelligence.

The pattern predates ARC-AGI-2. The original ARC launched in 2019. Models failed, then improved. By late 2024, o3 scored 75.7%, and the benchmark was functionally dead.

So Chollet built a harder one. Models failed again. They’re catching up faster this time.

He’s already building ARC-AGI-3, targeting interactive reasoning: exploration, memory, hypothesis-testing, acting in sequence. If every test AI eventually passes gets replaced by a harder test, then no score can ever serve as proof of intelligence. Only as evidence the previous test wasn’t hard enough.

Ask yourself: if the score resets to zero next year on ARC-AGI-3, does that erase what 77% proved?

**“Every benchmark headline that doesn’t include a cost number is telling you half the story.”**

![The benchmark treadmill: every AI test follows the same four-phase cycle. ARC-AGI has completed it twice. Made By Author.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*AHLdfGpL1O39hRPPHI-EpQ.jpeg)

### Winners, Losers, and the Leaderboard That Disagrees

The ARC-AGI-2 standings tell one story:

-   **Gemini 3 Deep Think:** 84.6%
-   **Claude Opus 4.6:** 68.8%
-   **GPT-5.2:** 52.9%

Google owns the top two slots. Chinese open-weight models lag far behind (DeepSeek V3.2 at 4%, Kimi K2.5 at 12%).

But Chatbot Arena, where real users vote on which model gives better answers, ranks Claude Opus 4.6 above Gemini 3.1 Pro for text and code. Users pick Claude. Benchmarks pick Gemini.

ARC-AGI-2 measures abstract fluid reasoning on novel puzzles. Arena measures how useful a model feels during actual work. One model can win one and lose the other, and neither leaderboard can tell you which victory matters more.

![The full ARC-AGI-2 picture. The human baselines (gold) sit in the middle of the pack, not at the top. Made By Author.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*FjUyqH178kdigYn21xRRcw.jpeg)

### The Strongest Case Against the Hype

Three objections deserve their full weight.

**1\. “77% is pattern-matching, not reasoning.”** ARC-AGI-2 was explicitly engineered to defeat memorization and data contamination. Each task is unique. No benchmark in the field resists gaming better. But the efficiency question stands: a system that brute-forces the right answer through sheer compute may be doing something qualitatively different from a human who reaches it in three minutes.

**2\. “The 60% human baseline is misleading.”** Correct. The LessWrong analysis exposes real design issues: 90-minute cap, modest incentive, built-in ambiguity on some tasks. Ten experts approached 100%. The gap between 77% and 60% is narrower and messier than headlines admit.

**3\. “The benchmark is already saturating.”** Imbue’s 95.1% with an engineering harness supports this directly. Chollet is already building ARC-AGI-3. Static benchmarks always yield to engineering eventually. The real question: does 77.1% in standard mode represent genuine progress toward general intelligence, or the peak of a specific optimization curve?

### What 77% Actually Means

**1\. The “wall” narrative is dead.** For two years, a growing chorus argued LLMs had hit a reasoning ceiling. ARC-AGI-2 was the strongest evidence: pure LLMs scored 0% at launch. At 77.1% in standard mode, Gemini 3.1 Pro showed the ceiling was a snapshot, not a permanent boundary. Betting against LLM reasoning just got expensive.

**2\. The real race is efficiency, not scores.** Chollet and the ARC Prize Foundation have said this outright. Intelligence isn’t “can you solve it” but “can you solve it cheaply.” AI still tackles these tasks at roughly ten times the cost of a human. The next result that actually moves the conversation won’t be a higher percentage. It will be the same percentage at human-level cost.

**3\. ARC-AGI-3 will reset everything. Again.** Interactive reasoning (exploration, memory, hypothesis-testing, sequential action) will almost certainly crash frontier scores back to single digits. This cycle is now the heartbeat of AI progress: benchmark rises, benchmark falls, harder benchmark arrives. Internalize this pattern and no score will blindside you again.

> **The machine scored 77%. The average human scored 60%. And the creator of the test says neither number means what you think.**

**“The machine scored 77%. The average human scored 60%. And the creator of the test says neither number means what you think.”**

In 2019, François Chollet published a paper arguing that every AI benchmark in existence measured the wrong thing. He built a test to measure the right thing. The test got solved.

He built a harder one. That one is getting solved too.

Somewhere, maybe in a San Francisco office or just a browser tab on his laptop, he’s building another. The score keeps moving. So does the question it was built to answer.

Seven years after Chollet first asked whether machines can truly reason, the honest answer hasn’t changed: not yet. But closer than anyone expected. And faster than anyone is comfortable with.
