# 7 Ways I Got My Content Cited by AI (ChatGPT and Claude)

# 7 Ways I Got My Content Cited by AI (ChatGPT and Claude)

## What I Learned After Watching AI Answer Questions With My Own Words

[Edward Tremblay](https://designful-ottawa.medium.com/?source=post_page---byline--7aed4ab4082f---------------------------------------)

6 min read·1 day ago

\--

![Illustration of a document being referenced by multiple AI systems, representing content being cited by ChatGPT and Claude.]()

I noticed it by accident.

A client asked me why their competitor kept showing up when they typed questions into ChatGPT. Not in a Google search — in the chat window. I did a few tests myself and started seeing the same thing with some of my own content. Pages I had written months ago were being referenced, paraphrased, or directly cited by AI tools.

That sent me down a rabbit hole.

Over the past year, I’ve been systematically testing what makes content “AI-citable” — what signals seem to earn a mention in ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, and other AI-powered answers. This isn’t an exact science. Nobody outside of OpenAI or Anthropic knows exactly how their retrieval systems work. But patterns are patterns.

Here’s what actually moved the needle for me.

## 1\. I stopped writing for skimmers and started writing for questions

Most blog posts are written to rank. They’re stuffed with H2s, bullet points, and keywords — formatted for Google’s featured snippets. That’s fine, but AI models don’t respond to formatting the same way.

What I noticed is that AI tends to pull from content that *answers a specific question clearly and completely in one place*. Not spread across ten subheadings. One clean, well-reasoned answer.

I started restructuring articles around a central question. Not “SEO tips” but “Why don’t businesses show up in Google Maps?” and then answering it fully, with context, nuance, and examples.

The shift in how I structure my thinking changed the structure of the writing — and that seemed to matter.

## 2\. I wrote things that nobody else had written yet

AI models cite sources when they need to fill a gap. If a topic is already covered by 200 nearly-identical articles, there’s no reason to pull from mine specifically.

The pieces that got cited most often were the ones where I had an actual opinion, a real data point from a client project, or a framing that I hadn’t seen elsewhere.

One example: I wrote a short piece on why service businesses in mid-sized cities are behind on conversion-rate optimization. It wasn’t a keyword-optimized post. It was just something I noticed after working with clients for years. That piece got referenced in AI answers several times when people asked about business marketing.

Original thinking is still the best moat. That hasn’t changed.

## 3\. I cited credible sources — consistently

This one surprised me a bit, but it makes sense in hindsight.

When AI models evaluate whether content is worth citing, they look at signals of credibility. One of those signals is whether you cite credible sources yourself. When your content links out to peer-reviewed studies, government data, or well-known institutions, it signals that you’re doing real research — not just padding a blog post.

I started treating every article like a mini-research brief. Even if I’m writing about something I know well, I find the underlying data and link to it. Stats from Statistics Canada, case studies from industry reports, published research where it exists.

It takes longer. But it changes how the content reads — and apparently how AI reads it too.

## 4\. I made my definitions clear and quotable

When I ask ChatGPT or Claude about a concept, they often pull a crisp definition or a clean explanation from somewhere. That “somewhere” has to be written in a specific way — stated clearly, confidently, and without hedging.

I started adding what I call a “definition block” to any post that covers a concept: one or two sentences that define the thing precisely, written in plain English.

Something like: *“A pricing calculator is a conversion tool, not a sales tool — its job is to reduce friction before a lead ever talks to you.”*

That’s a quotable sentence. It’s specific. It has a point of view. AI tends to surface these kinds of sentences when users ask conceptual questions.

## 5\. I kept publishing on platforms that AI already trusts

There’s a short list of platforms that AI models seem to draw from heavily: Medium, Reddit, Quora, LinkedIn articles, GitHub, and well-established blogs with real domain authority.

My own agency site has decent authority, but it doesn’t carry the same baseline trust as a platform that’s been around since 2012 and has millions of indexed pages. Medium gives me that amplification for free.

Publishing consistently here — not spamming, but publishing real articles regularly — increased my chances of being in the pool that AI pulls from. You can’t be cited if you’re not indexed and trusted.

## 6\. I structured data where it made sense

Structured data (Schema markup) has been a Google SEO play for years. What I didn’t expect is how much it seems to help with AI citation too.

When I added FAQ schema to service pages and articles, I started seeing those Q&A pairs show up in AI answers more often — almost verbatim. The reason is simple: structured data tells AI exactly what a piece of content is, who wrote it, what it’s about, and what specific questions it answers.

If you’re not doing this on your key pages, you’re leaving a lot on the table. It’s a one-time implementation that keeps paying off.

## 7\. I earned mentions — not just links

Here’s the one that took me the longest to connect: AI models pay attention to brand mentions across the web, not just backlinks. When my agency’s name, or a specific concept I coined, appears in forums, reviews, podcasts transcripts, or other articles — that builds what I’d call “citation equity.”

It’s similar to how brand mentions work for E-E-A-T in Google’s system. But for AI, the pattern is even more pronounced. If multiple independent sources reference the same idea or the same name, AI starts treating that entity as authoritative on the topic.

This means the old-school PR stuff actually matters again: getting quoted in articles, showing up on podcasts, having your name in industry conversations. It’s not just vanity. It’s infrastructure.

## What I’d focus on if I were starting over

If I had to pick three things to do first, it would be: write original content that answers real questions clearly, publish it on platforms that AI trusts, and cite your sources like you’re submitting to a journal.

The rest compounds from there.

This isn’t about tricking AI. It’s about being genuinely useful and credible enough that when an AI needs to explain something, your content is the right answer. That’s the same thing good writing has always tried to do.

It just has a new audience now.
