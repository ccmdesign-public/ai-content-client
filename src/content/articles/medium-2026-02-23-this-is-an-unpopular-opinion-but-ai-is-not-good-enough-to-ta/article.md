# This is an Unpopular Opinion, but AI Is Not Good Enough to Take Your Data Analytics Job (Yet)

# This is an Unpopular Opinion, but AI Is Not Good Enough to Take Your Data Analytics Job (Yet)

[Mithridata](/@mithridata?source=post_page---byline--361eac248ead---------------------------------------)

7 min read·3 hours ago

\--

To be honest, I’m a little tired of the AI bubble *not* bursting (not that I bet any money on it). Trust me, I am a big fan of AI, and I’m very eager to participate in the new world enabled by AI, but without overselling things like AGI or claiming it will change the way we live (yet).

I admit that I might be wrong, but the “AI will take your job” narrative is usually too blunt to describe what actually happens inside companies. AI is already changing how work gets done, and in some areas it will reduce headcount.

But in most roles, what I see is not full replacement. It’s task reshaping. Jobs are messy bundles of responsibilities tied to accountability, coordination, and real-world constraints, and current AI still struggles with the parts that make work real.

## AI feels like magic, but it is not there yet

Please don’t think of me in the context of the Luddite movement (if you never heard of it, please see [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luddite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luddite)). I am not part of it for sure, and I even love to try new tools once they appear (try to be in the first cohort to spread the knowledge later).

A lot of the job anxiety I’m seeing in the market and among colleagues comes from watching AI do a single impressive thing: write a clean email, produce code, generate a design, or summarize a long document. It can feel like **magic (**even though it’s “just” a very complex large language model, and the key word here is *language*, not some general-purpose intelligence).

But workplaces are not demo environments. People get paid to produce outcomes under constraints: deadlines, budgets, compliance rules, customer expectations, internal politics, incomplete data, and unclear requirements.

My experience says that the biggest friction in most organizations is not “we can’t generate a draft.” It’s “we can’t agree on what we want,” “we don’t trust the data,” “we can’t get approvals,” or “we can’t ship safely.” AI can help with pieces of this, but it doesn’t remove the organizational reality around the work.

## What AI is actually good at

AI tends to shine when the input is clear, the output format is clear, and the cost of being wrong is manageable. That’s why it works well for first drafts, rewriting, summarization, classification, support triage, quick analysis, and code scaffolding. It’s especially helpful for reducing “blank page” time and speeding up routine work that already had templates.

I might be wrong, but I think the best mental model is that today’s AI is a powerful pattern engine with uneven reliability. It can be extremely helpful when paired with human judgment, but it isn’t consistently dependable enough to own end-to-end outcomes in most real work environments. And most “AI tools” are still language models, not all-purpose intelligence or full automation.

To actually deliver automation, you typically need a stack of other pieces around the model , things that are developing quickly, but aren’t “AI” in and of themselves , like API integrations, connectors, workflow orchestration, permissions and identity management, retrieval/search over internal data (RAG), monitoring and logging, and good old-fashioned QA and guardrails.

But we are still doing that in the world where NOT ALL COMPANIES HAVE GOOD DATA QUALITY OR ARE NOT FULLY DIGITALIZED (sorry for the all caps)!

## Why “taking your job” is harder than “doing tasks”

Jobs are not single tasks. They are systems of tasks plus responsibility. Replacing a job means you can execute the whole bundle reliably, handle exceptions, operate inside constraints, and absorb consequences when things go wrong.

In practice, most roles include ambiguity (“what does success look like?”), hidden requirements (what not to do, what legal won’t allow, what customers will hate), exception handling (the strange cases that actually matter), and cross-functional coordination (getting other teams to align and deliver). And then there’s accountability. A person can be held responsible, coached, promoted, fired, audited, sued, or called into a meeting with leadership. AI cannot meaningfully occupy that role in the same way.

This is where my experiences really shape my view: organizations don’t just need output. They need someone who owns the output.

## Where current AI falls short in practice

I again might be wrong, but the most important limitation is not intelligence in the abstract. It’s operational reliability.

AI can be “confidently wrong,” producing plausible answers that fail quietly until they cause damage. It also struggles with staying grounded in a company’s current reality. Priorities change, policies change, products change, and a lot of institutional knowledge is tacit rather than documented. Keeping AI systems aligned with what is true today is a constant maintenance job, not a one-time install.

Then there’s integration. Even if an AI makes a good recommendation, it still has to interact with real systems: CRMs, databases, billing platforms, code repositories, approval workflows, security boundaries. That integration is expensive and fragile, and it differs by company. This is one reason why “the model is impressive” does not automatically become “the job is gone.”

## A concrete scenario: what “AI takes the job” would actually require

Take a customer success manager in a B2B software company. AI can help draft emails, summarize calls, extract themes from support tickets, and flag accounts with churn risk based on usage signals. That can genuinely change day-to-day productivity.

But to replace the role fully, the system would need to interpret shifting stakeholder goals, manage relationship dynamics, coordinate internally across support and product, negotiate within policy, escalate appropriately, and remain accountable when something goes wrong. It would also need to operate under privacy and compliance constraints and integrate reliably with the company’s tools. This is just my opinion, but that level of replacement is not primarily a “better model” problem. It is an organizational redesign problem.

## Industries and roles that are more prone to AI replacement

This is just my opinion, and I might be wrong, but AI-driven replacement tends to happen faster in environments where work is already standardized, measurable, and easy to quality-check. If your job is mostly handling high-volume, repetitive tasks with clear success criteria and low tolerance for variability, you are in a more automatable zone.

More prone (or at least more exposed) areas include basic tier-1 customer support, routine document processing, simple claims intake, straightforward transcription and summarization workflows, commodity content production, and some forms of back-office operations where outputs follow rigid templates. Certain kinds of compliance checks and monitoring can also be heavily automated when rules are explicit and the data is structured.

It’s also worth separating “replacement” from “consolidation.” In some cases, AI doesn’t remove the function, but it reduces the number of people needed because one person can do what used to require several.

## Industries and roles that are less prone to AI replacement

Less prone roles tend to be those where the work is physical, deeply interpersonal, highly regulated with complex judgment calls, or dependent on a real-world context that is hard to capture in data. Healthcare is a good example: AI can support diagnosis and documentation, but care delivery involves ethics, trust, liability, and hands-on action. Skilled trades, many onsite roles, emergency response, and complex management work are also harder to automate because the environment is dynamic and exceptions are the norm.

Even in knowledge work, roles that combine negotiation, leadership, creative direction, and accountability are less likely to be fully replaced, though they may be heavily augmented. My experience says that “human glue work” is underrated until you try to automate it.

## Where data analytics fits in

And as most of my content is about Data Analytics, I thought that I need to assure my fellow colleagues in this area. From my perspective, data analytics is an interesting middle case. Some parts are very automatable: building routine dashboards, generating standard reports, writing common SQL queries, documenting metrics, summarizing insights, and even drafting analysis narratives. A lot of the mechanical work of analysis is becoming faster.

But the less automatable parts tend to be the ones that matter most: defining the right question, aligning stakeholders on metrics, understanding data-generating processes, detecting when the data is lying, designing experiments, managing trade-offs, and making recommendations that survive organizational reality. AI can assist with these, but in my opinion it struggles to own them end to end, because they require context, judgment, and accountability.

So I’d expect analytics teams to evolve rather than disappear. More automation in the reporting layer, more emphasis on decision intelligence, experimentation, governance, and “translation” between business and technical teams. In other words, fewer hours spent producing charts and more hours spent ensuring the charts mean something and drive action.

## Conclusion

I might be wrong, but I don’t think AI is broadly good enough to take most jobs outright in the near term. It is good enough to take tasks, reshape workflows, and reduce the need for certain kinds of repetitive roles, especially where outputs are standardized and easy to verify. The bigger story is not full replacement, but re-bundling of work: automating the predictable parts and increasing the value of people who handle ambiguity, exceptions, coordination, and accountability.

My experience says the practical question is not “Will AI take my job?” It’s “Which parts of my job are predictable enough to automate, and how do I move up the stack into the parts that require judgment, context, and ownership?”
