# The Seeing Stone

## How Palantir Became the Government’s Eye

12 min read·Oct 29, 2025

\--

There’s a dinner party that changed America, and you’ve probably never heard of it.

![Gemini Generated Image]()

It happened in 1993, at a restaurant in Washington D.C. The Deputy Secretary of Defense invited executives from over a dozen major defense contractors. They walked in representing separate companies — Lockheed, Martin Marietta, Northrop, Grumman, McDonnell Douglas. They walked out representing a handful of consolidated giants.

History remembers this as “The Last Supper.” The moment when America’s defense industry went from competitive to concentrated. From innovative to financial. From building better weapons to maximizing shareholder value.

Now, thirty years later, there’s another company positioning itself for a similar transformation. Except this time, they’re not consolidating the defense industry. They’re trying to become the operating system for government itself.

Their name comes from *Lord of the Rings*. The all-seeing stones that could peer across Middle Earth, predict the future, and — in the wrong hands — corrupt everything they touched.

They call themselves Palantir.

## The PayPal Mafia’s Next Act

Malcolm Gladwell has written extensively about how context shapes success. The right person, in the right place, with the right resources, at exactly the right moment in history. Palantir’s founding is a masterclass in this principle.

The year is 2003. Two massive forces are converging.

First: September 11th has transformed America’s relationship with surveillance. The government is terrified, Congress is writing blank checks, and nobody’s asking too many questions about privacy or oversight. Fear is rocket fuel for defense budgets.

Second: The dot-com boom has created a new class of tech millionaires who think they can solve any problem with enough data and clever algorithms. Silicon Valley believes it’s smarter than everyone else, and suddenly it has the cash to prove it.

Enter Peter Thiel, fresh off selling PayPal for $1.5 billion. At PayPal, they’d built systems for detecting fraud — algorithms that could spot patterns in transaction data that human analysts would miss. Thiel had a question: What if we applied that same thinking to everything else?

Not just credit cards. Everything. Everyone.

He pulled from his network — what would later be called the PayPal Mafia — and recruited an unlikely CEO: his former Stanford Law School roommate, Alex Karp. Not an engineer. Not a business guy. A PhD in neoclassical social theory who’d spent years studying philosophy in Germany.

This is the first counterintuitive twist. You’d expect a surveillance tech company to be run by a computer scientist or a military strategist. Instead, Palantir chose a philosopher. Someone who understood not just how to build systems, but how to construct narratives. How to sell ideas.

Because Palantir’s real product isn’t software. It’s permission.

## The Question Nobody Asks

Here’s what Palantir does, stripped of marketing speak: they make software that helps organizations collect massive amounts of data, analyze it using artificial intelligence, and then act on those insights.

Simple enough. Except the organizations hiring them are the FBI, the CIA, the NSA, the Marines, the Air Force, the IRS, police departments, health insurance companies, and — somewhat inexplicably — Wendy’s.

The product is called “predicate-based research.” Karp explains it simply: “We would look at you and then we would go out and say, oh, there’s lots of different things in your life that may be indicative of someone being somewhat involved in bad behavior.”

Translation: We find patterns in your life that suggest you might be worth investigating. Your location data. Your financial transactions. Your social connections. Your internet searches. Your medical records. Everything stitched together into a profile that didn’t exist until Palantir created it.

In 2013, a journalist asked Karp if Palantir helped kill Osama bin Laden. He couldn’t confirm directly — classified information and all — but offered this: “When you look in the news about these kind of things, there’s a two-thirds chance, depending on the country involved, that somehow my company was involved.”

Two-thirds of major intelligence operations. Think about that for a moment.

But here’s the question nobody asks: If Palantir’s technology is so effective at finding terrorists, what happens when you point it at everyone else?

## The Kill Chain Goes Corporate

There’s a term Karp uses that the lawyers hate: “kill chain.”

It’s military jargon for the sequence of decisions from identifying a target to ending their life. Detect, track, target, engage, assess. Palantir’s software, now powered by artificial intelligence, speeds up that process. Makes it “quicker and better and safer and more violent,” according to Karp.

The lawyers prefer something more antiseptic. “Technology for the amelioration of unwanted outcomes,” or some equally bloodless phrase. But Karp doesn’t care. He calls it what it is: “For me, it’s the kill chain. Kill chain. Sounds good.”

This is where the Gladwellian principle of weak signals and strong outcomes emerges. The technology developed for one purpose — finding terrorists — becomes infinitely adaptable to other purposes. The same AI that identifies targets in war zones can identify “fraud” in tax returns. Can identify “high-risk” patients for insurance companies. Can identify whatever you program it to identify.

Juan Pinto, a former Palantir employee who helped market these systems, puts it this way: The technology “can be used to completely reconfigure organizations’ ontology, its reality, what systems matter, what information matters, what processes matter, how they are structured, what biases are introduced at each of those stages.”

In other words: Palantir doesn’t just analyze reality. It restructures how you perceive reality itself.

After October 7th, 2023, when Pinto watched the Israeli military operation in Gaza — an operation that evidence suggests used Palantir’s AI for target selection — something broke for him. He started speaking out. “Your whole world starts falling apart,” he says, “which is something that definitely happened to me.”

It’s one thing to build technology for finding terrorists. It’s another to watch that technology scale without boundaries. To see the “kill chain” logic migrate from battlefields to boardrooms.

Consider: Palantir works with health insurance companies on “denials management” to “protect revenue.” Read that phrase again. Denials management. Protecting revenue. This is AI designed to help insurance companies reject your claims more efficiently.

Do you want Palantir’s artificial intelligence making decisions about what medical care is covered for you and your family?

## The Disruption Narrative

Here’s where Palantir’s story takes its most fascinating turn. They’ve adopted the language of revolution.

In 2024, Palantir’s CTO Shyam Sankar released a presentation called “The Defense Reformation.” He explicitly criticized the 1993 “Last Supper” that consolidated the defense industry. Called out the “financialization of defense.” Attacked stock buybacks and profit-seeking behavior that prioritizes dividends over innovation.

This is remarkable. A defense contractor criticizing the defense industry using the exact arguments that anti-war progressives have been making for decades.

Pinto, the former employee, recognizes the playbook: “What’s interesting to me about Palantir is how they co-opt the language of revolution.”

It worked. The Defense Innovation Board — an advisory group within the Department of Defense — cited Sankar’s presentation multiple times in their 2024 report recommending that DoD divert more budget to “emerging technology.”

Translation: Give more money to Palantir.

In the months after Trump’s election, Palantir’s stock soared. They replaced Ford Motors in the S&P 100. The Trump administration is, from Palantir’s perspective, an ideal customer. Former Palantir employees are scattered across the administration — in DOGE, in foreign policy positions, in high-level technology roles. Peter Thiel, Palantir’s co-founder who retains massive influence through a special ownership structure that gives founders 49.99% voting power even if they own as little as 6% of shares, was a major campaign donor to both Trump and Vance.

And DOGE’s stated mission — streamline and unify government data systems — is exactly what Palantir does.

Elon Musk himself explained the problem Palantir solves: “The ways that the government is defrauded is that the computer systems don’t talk to each other.”

Karp is thrilled: “There’s a revolution. Some people can get their heads cut off. Like, you know, we’re expecting to see really unexpected things and to win.”

Winning, according to CTO Sankar in 2021, means “becoming the US government’s central operating system.”

Not a contractor. The operating system. Everything funneling through Palantir.

## The Operating System Question

Let’s be concrete about what this means.

Palantir already has contracts with the IRS to analyze taxpayer data, finding the “easiest audits to pursue.” Now, according to Wired, DOGE will likely hire Palantir to create unified software for the entire IRS — a “mega API” that would allow anyone with access to view and possibly alter all IRS data in one place.

That’s your financial information. Your tax returns. Your banking details. Your investments. Everything in one database, accessed through one company’s software.

Palantir also has contracts worth nearly $100 million with the Department of Health and Human Services to “manage, ingest, and access data securely across business domains.” HHS runs Medicare and Medicaid. That’s control over millions of Americans’ health records and access to healthcare.

So what does it mean for one contractor — one company, controlled by founders who openly discuss using tech to influence politics — to have all that power, all that data, and profit from all those taxpayer dollars?

Especially as artificial intelligence gets involved?

Pinto warns: “As we increasingly live in a simulated world, we lose touch with reality and the human decisions that matter. And we move closer towards governance by algorithm — not only made by decisions of automated artificial intelligence systems, which is a problem, but more importantly, subject to decisions made by the people who are influencing these AI systems and creating them in order to fulfill an agenda for whatever their profit-seeking or control-seeking objectives are.”

When DOGE goes into every agency and addresses the IT department first, Pinto sees something darker than efficiency: “I see it as an opportunity to try to change reality itself.”

## The Surveillance We Choose

Here’s the thing about surveillance: it doesn’t require bars or guards. It just requires the belief that someone might be watching.

This is the panopticon principle, identified by philosopher Michel Foucault. The circular prison where guards in a central tower can see every cell, but prisoners can’t see if anyone’s actually watching. The prisoners don’t need to know if they’re being observed. The possibility alone changes behavior.

Pinto articulates this precisely: “It’s not about whether you’re actually at risk for speaking out or not. It’s just the idea that somebody who’s watching you is enough to push people into silence.”

Palantir has no problem being seen as all-powerful, all-seeing, lethal. It’s part of the marketing. Karp says explicitly: “We are very comfortable being unpopular.”

Because controversy doesn’t lose them customers. It attracts them. Governments don’t want friendly partners — they want effective ones. And nothing says effective like being willing to do what others won’t.

Karp’s message to potential employees is clear: “If you do not feel comfortable supporting the legitimate efforts of America and its allies in the context of war, don’t join Palantir.”

But what are “legitimate efforts” when the same company works for health insurance companies optimizing claim denials? When they’re building the government’s “central operating system”? When they’re used by police departments with opaque oversight?

## The Philosophy Professor’s Long Game

Remember: Palantir’s CEO isn’t an engineer. He’s a philosopher.

In 2009, Karp spoke about civil liberties regarding Palantir’s work. His position was essentially: Trust us. We’ll be the ones with access to all this data, but we’ll police ourselves. We’ll have internal oversight about how government data is used.

In 2010, co-founder Peter Thiel spoke at Libertopia, a libertarian conference, about tech companies and government. His vision was clear: tech companies should use their power to affect politics directly.

So we have one co-founder who believes tech should reshape government, and another who believes his company should have oversight over how government data is used. And both founders have locked in permanent control through their special share structure.

The named they chose wasn’t accidental. In *Lord of the Rings*, the Palantiri were created by elves for good purposes. But they became corrupted. Powerful tools that seemed beneficial until they weren’t. Until they were used by Sauron to manipulate and control.

Tolkien understood something fundamental: the most dangerous technologies are the ones built with good intentions.

## The Pattern We’re Missing

Malcolm Gladwell often writes about how we miss obvious patterns because we’re looking for the wrong things. We expect threats to look threatening. Villains to look villainous.

But what if the real pattern is simpler? What if it’s just about power?

Palantir generated nearly $2.9 billion in revenue in 2024. 55% came from government sources, mostly American. They’re becoming indispensable to how government functions. Not because government couldn’t work without them, but because once you integrate one company’s software into everything — your IRS systems, your healthcare data, your military operations, your law enforcement — switching becomes impossible.

This is the operating system play. Not the best product. The most embedded one.

And here’s the uncomfortable truth: we’re letting it happen. Not because Palantir forced it on us, but because we keep choosing convenience over scrutiny. Efficiency over oversight. The promise of AI-powered solutions over the hard work of reforming broken systems.

Pinto, who finally spoke out despite his fear of repercussions, puts it bluntly: “We’re at the brink of using these technologies potentially to run our government, to run our battlefields and our personal lives. Using artificial intelligence as a sort of panacea solution across our federal departments, especially when they’re wielded by people with a very distinct agenda, puts everyone at risk.”

## The Tipping Point That Already Tipped

The fascinating thing about tipping points is that you usually recognize them too late. By the time you realize the moment of transformation has passed, you’re already living in the transformed world.

We might already be there with Palantir.

They’re in the IRS. In HHS. In the Pentagon. In police departments. In hospitals. In insurance companies. Making decisions about who gets audited, who gets healthcare, who gets targeted, who gets investigated.

And increasingly, those decisions are being made by artificial intelligence systems that even their creators don’t fully understand. Emergent capabilities. Unexpected behaviors. The same problems we discussed before, except now they’re embedded in government infrastructure.

The revolution Karp keeps mentioning? It’s not coming. It’s here. Quiet. Technical. Disguised as efficiency improvements and cost savings.

We can’t replace our current broken system with an even more broken one. We can’t let a handful of Silicon Valley philosophers and libertarians become our government’s operating system.

But stopping it requires something we’re not good at: saying no to technological solutions. Demanding transparency over efficiency. Choosing democratic oversight over corporate optimization.

It requires recognizing that the most dangerous surveillance isn’t the kind you see. It’s the kind that’s become so normalized, so embedded, so essential to daily operations that questioning it seems impossible.

The Palantir stones in Tolkien’s work had a fatal flaw: once you looked into them, they could look back into you. They could see your fears, your desires, your weaknesses. And once they knew you that well, they could control you without you ever realizing it.

That’s not a metaphor anymore.

It’s a business model.

*The question isn’t whether Palantir’s technology works. It clearly does. The question is whether we’re comfortable with who’s holding the seeing stone — and what they’re looking for when they peer into it. What do you think: Is this the future of government efficiency, or the end of privacy as we know it? The algorithms are listening. They always are.*
