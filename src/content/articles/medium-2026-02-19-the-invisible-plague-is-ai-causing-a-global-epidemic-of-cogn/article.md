# **The Invisible Plague: Is AI Causing a Global Epidemic of Cognitive Atrophy? (And We Don’t Even Notice)**

![]()

If someone told you there is a global epidemic spreading right now — one that affects school kids, office workers, doctors, lawyers and even CEOs — you would imagine a virus. A toxin. Maybe a new kind of pollution.

But what if I told you this epidemic has no symptoms in the beginning? No fever. No cough. No pain. And still it slowly eats away something precious.

Not your lungs. Not your liver.

Your brain.

We talk about AI like it’s only a machine problem. Bias. Hallucinations. Privacy. Security. We discuss model accuracy and regulations like the EU AI Act. All that matters, yes.

But there is one uncomfortable truth we are avoiding:

AI is not just changing the work we do. It is changing the thinking we do. And if we are not careful, we will become a society that produces content faster, communicates faster, “moves faster” — but thinks slower.

**The brain loves shortcuts. AI is the biggest shortcut ever invented.**

The human brain is not lazy by nature. It is efficient. It saves effort wherever possible. That is why we use lifts instead of stairs. Food delivery instead of cooking. Convenience is a human addiction.

But thinking is also a muscle. You need resistance. You need struggle. You need boredom sometimes. You need those moments where your mind says, “I can’t solve this,” and still you try. That is where intelligence is actually built.

Now comes AI with the sweetest offer: “Don’t struggle. I’ll do it.”

Write my email. Summarise my report. Make my strategy. Generate my ideas. Create my content. Plan my day. Even argue my point in a debate.

At first, it feels like productivity. It feels like progress. It feels smart.

But the brain does not care about feelings. It responds to practice.

When you stop practising thinking, you do not stay at the same level. You decline. Slowly. Silently.

This is called cognitive offloading — outsourcing mental effort to external tools. We have done it before. Calculators. Google. But AI takes it to a different level.

Google gives answers. AI gives finished thinking.

**Why this is different from Google (and much more dangerous)**

With Google, you still had to search. Filter. Compare. Decide. There was some friction. You still did mental work.

AI removes friction. It gives you a confident answer. A polished paragraph. A neat table. A beautiful plan. A convincing argument.

And that confidence creates a new disease: people stop questioning.

In risk management, this is called automation bias. Humans trust the machine because the machine “sounds sure”.

When your brain learns that the machine will do it, it starts conserving energy. It stops working deeply. It stops sharpening itself.

And here is the scary part: you can lose cognitive sharpness without realising it. Like weight gain. It does not happen in one day. It happens one small choice at a time.

**This problem hits every generation differently**

For kids and students, the damage is the worst. A child who never struggles will never learn how to struggle. And struggle is the foundation of learning. Not marks. Not grades.

If a student uses AI for homework, assignments, even explanations, that student may look smart today but will fail tomorrow when life gives them real problems and no prompt box.

For young professionals, it shows up as AI dependency. They become excellent at polishing AI drafts but weak at producing original thinking. They learn to operate tools, not to grow skills.

For managers and founders, AI can become a dangerous strategy drug. An AI-generated plan looks structured. It checks all boxes. But often it lacks the one thing real leadership requires: judgement.

For society, the biggest risk is not wrong answers. It is same answers.

When millions of people ask the same few models for ideas, we do not get diversity. We get average intelligence on repeat. Algorithmic monoculture. Creativity becomes standardized. Thinking becomes formatted. Opinions become copy-paste. Even humour starts sounding similar.

**We need governance. But not only for AI. Governance for human cognition.**

Today, AI governance focuses on system outputs. Fairness. Privacy. Transparency. Accountability. Frameworks like NIST AI RMF talk about safety and risk management. Regulators like the EU AI Act are trying to control high-risk AI systems.

But we need to widen the governance lens. Because even if AI is 100% fair, accurate and secure, it can still cause damage — if it makes humans cognitively weaker.

I call this missing piece: **Cognitive Impact Governance**.

Simple meaning: before deploying AI at scale, ask one question that nobody is asking loudly enough —

*What will this do to human thinking over time?*

Not morally. Not politically. Practically.

**A simple rule that can protect our minds: Assistant vs Executor**

This is the most practical idea I have found: an AI system can be an assistant, but it should not become the executor of core mental activities.

**AI can help with:**

-   Finding information
-   Checking grammar
-   Summarising long reports
-   Organising ideas
-   Comparing options

**AI should not replace:**

-   Judgement
-   Ethical reasoning
-   Final decision-making
-   Original composition
-   Critical thinking

If AI becomes the default executor, human thinking becomes an unused asset. And unused assets decay.

Companies should build this into policy. Schools should build this into education. Parents should build this into habits.

**Here is what real responsible AI adoption should look like**

Let us stop pretending responsible AI is only about ethics and compliance. It is also about mental fitness.

A responsible organisation should do these basic things:

1.  **Train employees** on how to use AI without becoming dependent.
2.  **Build a culture** of human-first thinking, AI-second support.
3.  **Keep certain tasks AI-free** — brainstorming, learning, writing first drafts.
4.  **Introduce challenge mode.** AI should not always confirm you. It should question you.
5.  **Audit not just AI errors — but human deskilling.**

Yes, audit deskilling. Because the real productivity killer in the future will not be AI mistakes.

It will be human weakness.

**The real question is not “What can AI do?”**

It is: what are humans slowly stopping?

This technology is astonishing. Nobody is denying that. It will transform healthcare, finance, education, business. I use it myself. Every day.

But if we do not protect human cognition, AI will create an ironic future: we will have the smartest machines in history and the weakest thinkers in society.

The final choice is ours.

We can use AI as a bicycle for the mind — helping humans go further.

Or we can use it as a wheelchair — making humans forget how to walk.

In my work implementing governance for financial inclusion AI, we enforce this by coding “accountability triggers” — hard system checkpoints that block a transaction if critical human review is bypassed.
