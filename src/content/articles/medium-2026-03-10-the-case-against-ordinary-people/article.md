# The Case Against Ordinary People

## Or how we’re dismantling the social contract that let ordinary people build wealth

[Camilo Lascano Tribin](https://medium.com/@camilolascanotribin?source=post_page---byline--a89f3ef3b39e---------------------------------------)

![Photo by Documerica on Unsplash]()

It’s January 5th, 1970, and the “Middle Americans” have just been named man and woman of the year. The reason? Since the end of the Second World War, the middle class has been a thriving and respected part of American life. The ‘average American’ was neither an insult nor a burden but a badge of honour and the economic engine that powered the country. Sentiment towards “averageness” was relatively consistent and positive across many developed nations, and there was an overwhelming sense that if you worked hard, showed up and put in an honest day’s work, there was a piece of the wealth pie waiting for you when you got home.

I think of L.P. Hartley’s iconic opening line to The Go-Between, “The past is a foreign country; they do things differently there.” With the way 2026 is going, the past doesn’t feel like a foreign country but an entirely different planet.

## Towards an American Dream

To fully appreciate where the economy and the job market currently are, and to get a sense of where I think we’re going, I’d like to draw your attention for a moment to where we’ve been.

For most of human history — that is, for most of the pre-industrial economy — the structure of how we organised work and labour was essentially this: At the top of the food chain, you had your landed aristocracy and nobility. Those who owned the land extracted rents and had their wealth inherited. Attached to them but in a wholly different class were the peasants or serfs. These were the people who worked the land, paid rent in either labour, crops, or sometimes coin, and had no path toward any sort of ownership. Between these was a small class of craftsmen and traders known as merchants. The social structure was fairly basic and exceptionally immobile. Wealth equalled land ownership, and labour alone never led to any sort of material prosperity. You worked to live and lived to work, and your ability to ever accumulate much of anything was severely restricted.

Then came the little engine that could, and boom — the Industrial Revolution changed everything. Or rather, ignited the spark that would eventually lead to the slow change of everything. The biggest shift that happened was that wealth moved from being solely tied to land to being tied to capital. Factories, machines, infrastructure — this is the time of the Fat Cats and Robber Barons, the Carnegies and Rockefellers who owned capital and captured those productivity gains to generate and pass on their wealth. For those in the working class — those who gained access to wealth through their wages alone — it was slightly better than serfdom, though there was still no clear or easy path to ownership. You still spent wages on rent and food and were left with not much to accumulate or pass on. Homeownership remained among the business owners and professional class (doctors, lawyers with their own firms) and farmers who owned land. An important point to remember here is that the Industrial Revolution created a more productive and wealthy economy, yet that didn’t translate to wealth being accumulated across the entire economy. We’d shifted who owned the appreciating assets, but workers were still, for the most part, locked out of ownership.

In the early 20th century, a little movement started happening, though not much. You did see small business ownership rise among shopkeepers and small-town manufacturers, and the role of the manager or “white-collar worker” began to take root. It wasn’t until the world was done bombing itself into near-complete destruction that the ingredients of what we would recognise today as a “middle class” fully came into being. These are, broadly speaking: access to asset ownership which primarily came in the form of housing; wages that fell in line with productivity and GDP so that workers shared in the economic growth of a nation rather than simply subsisting; retirement security which meant people didn’t have to solely rely on family to look after them in their old age and that work eventually had an end date; access to education and the first generation of working-class children able to access higher education en masse which resulted in upward mobility within a generation; and stable, dignified employment that meant you could work hard and buy a house and send your kids off to school to have a better life than you did, and perhaps hand something down to them in the form of an inheritance.

That was the American Dream. And with America’s post-war cultural and economic dominance, it essentially became much of the world’s dream. And yes, before you come for me and tell me that it wasn’t universal and mainly applicable to a few nations and even within those nations it excluded a host of citizens, including Black Americans, women, and immigrants — I know. The point was not that it was perfect in practice but that it opened up the franchise of economic participation and at least provided a framework for how ordinary people could feel a sense of ownership and control over their lives. It was a framework worth expanding.

## Towards an American Nightmare

If you’ve been following my writing this year, you know it’s been dominated by trying to unpack where we’re heading from an economic and labour point of view. I don’t think I need to tell you there’s a strange sense of unease almost anywhere you look, and it’s hard to sometimes tell if that sense of unease is genuine or manufactured.

Scroll through Instagram, X, or wherever you get your information fix, and it’s all a sea of panic and hype that’s difficult to distil. Every news feed tells you a new job has been destroyed. [This week, it was Hollywood](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FhjJTZ9uIWY), with viral clips of Ethan Hunt and John Wick fighting or mash-ups of DC and Marvel. But the story is relatively the same: entry-level jobs are drying up, every influencer is selling you the latest AI course that will future-proof your career, and the news reports that software is dead, that trading is dead, that the internet is dead, now it’s the intern that’s dead. Everything is dying, and people are panicked. Part of me finds it grimly amusing because this displacement has been happening in blue-collar jobs for decades, yet the angst was much less felt because those affected weren’t the ones writing the news. [Remember when manufacturing jobs were drying up and being offshored, and we simply told everyone to learn to code?](https://www.wired.com/2017/02/programming-is-the-new-blue-collar-job/#:~:text=Among%20other%20things%2C%20it%20would,states%20hit%20hardest%20by%20deindustrialization.) Well, now the coders are the first on the front line and suddenly the intelligentsia is really worried.

It’s hard to balance the fact from the fiction and to really assess just how bad or good things are. Is it the end of jobs or isn’t it? According to what was perhaps the most viral essay in AI news this week, [“Something Big Is Happening” by Matt Shumer](https://shumer.dev/something-big-is-happening), the answer is unequivocally yes. Shumer, an AI startup founder, describes how he’s no longer needed for the technical work of his own job. He describes projects to AI in plain English, walks away for hours, and returns to find finished, working applications built without his input. The new models released in early February (GPT-5.3 Codex and Opus 4.6) aren’t just better; they show something resembling judgment and taste. His warning is that what happened to tech workers over the past year is coming for everyone else. Law, finance, medicine, accounting, writing — all knowledge work. Not in 10 years, but more like in one to five, with many predicting less. He points to data showing AI can now complete tasks that take human experts nearly five hours, a capability that’s been doubling every four to seven months. His advice is urgent: spend an hour a day experimenting with AI, get your financial house in order, and prepare for the most disruptive years of your career.

It’s a compelling case, made more so by the fact that Shumer doesn’t appear to be selling anything and claims to be writing for friends and family who keep asking, “What’s the deal with AI?” But it’s also exactly the kind of essay that feeds the panic machine. The AI bosses are certainly saying the world is ending because, like the Industrial Revolution before it, they need their tech to completely shift the way the economy functions so they can justify the half-a-trillion-dollar investment needed to keep them running.

So that’s where we are. Some are saying the world is changing and becoming unrecognisable. Others are saying it’s a bubble, and reality will hit. To be perfectly honest with you, I have no idea which side is right. All I can do is look at the facts and extrapolate from them what I think is most probable.

## The End of the Middle

![Photo by jurien huggins on Unsplash]()

I’ll give it to you straight. My prediction, for whatever predictions are worth these days, is that jobs won’t disappear the way everyone fears, but prosperity certainly will, and we’ll have completely eroded the middle class. The majority of people will go back to being where they’ve been for the majority of history: subsisting with very few means of upward social mobility.

Two essays really helped me lock in on this being the most likely path forward, alongside the research I’ve done so far this year on what’s essentially turned into a jobs and economics trilogy come full-blown series: *The Hollow Generation, The Sweet Satisfaction of Being Needed*, and *En Route to Serfdom*. I’ll link those at the end of this essay. However, for now, it’s best to start with the one that really cracked the anxious freneticism that’s holding everyone today hostage and diagnosed what’s wrong with the economy: [kyla scanlon](https://open.substack.com/users/13311420-kyla-scanlon?utm_source=mentions)’s *“Buying Futures, Renting the Past.”*

What Scanlon captures so expertly in this essay is how fractured the current economic model is. She does so by splitting the economy into two realities that are no longer connected. The first is the statistical economy, one that thrives without workers and is based on speculation, GDP, stock markets, corporate profits, productivity gains, and AI valuations. The second is the material economy — the one most people live in. The wage workers, the ones that need to pay bills, have childcare to look after, the everyday ordinary middle Americans that in the 1970s made the cover of Time, but are today very quickly being thrown to the curb.

By every traditional economic measure, the economy — at least in the US — is booming. Everything’s a-okay. Until you look at the material economy. Over there, things aren’t so good.

Labour’s share of GDP has fallen seven percentage points over the past four decades, while, as previously mentioned, corporate profits have risen by four. College graduate unemployment is at [its highest level in four years at 5.7%](https://www.newyorkfed.org/research/college-labor-market#--:explore:unemployment), and it now takes graduates an average of 11 weeks to find their first job out of university. [The average first-time homebuyer is nearly 40 years old](https://www.nar.realtor/research-and-statistics/research-reports/home-buyer-and-seller-generational-trends), essentially middle-aged, and the median home they’re purchasing costs 4.4 times their annual income. People are still employed, but they can’t access what used to come with employment. [Just compare these metrics to 1996: the average first-time homebuyer was roughly 30 years old, and homes cost approximately 2–2.5 times annual income.](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/blog/home-prices-surge-five-times-median-income-nearing-historic-highs) Take it back a decade to [1986 and the first-time homebuyer was 26–28 years old](https://www.nar.realtor/research-and-statistics/research-reports/highlights-from-the-profile-of-home-buyers-and-sellers). You start to get my point. All of the things that gave the middle class their prestige and prosperity — a home, retirement security, the ability to accumulate wealth and pass it on — all of these are drying up for everyone.

One of Scanlon’s most eye-opening examples is the comparison between the world’s two most valuable companies at their peaks and just how much the relationship between wages and wealth creation has changed. [In 1985, IBM was at its peak and employed 400,000 workers](https://time.com/archive/6722015/how-ibm-was-left-behind/). That’s 400,000 who were able to have a stake in the wealth generated by the business and in turn accumulate their own wealth from it. Today, [Nvidia is the world’s most valuable company at roughly $3 trillion, yet it employs only 40,000.](https://www.reuters.com/markets/us/nvidia-becomes-worlds-most-valuable-company-2024-06-18/) A lot of value is being created. A whole lot of it isn’t going to workers.

If this sounds familiar to you, it’s because it’s the same pattern I was describing earlier during the Industrial Revolution. The economy grew, and productivity exploded, but workers were locked out of accumulating any of that wealth. What was thought to be the birth of a new healthy middle class in the post-war boom — one where when productivity rose, real wages rose with it, and workers shared in the growth — is turning out to be a blip rather than a new standard. Because, let’s face it, that’s what made a middle class possible: ordinary workers having access to what appreciated. As Scanlon so brilliantly tracks, that trend is now in complete reversal.

## Regulation Won’t Save Us

The second essay was one I’d saved from 2023 from the tech optimist Marc Andreessen, titled *Why AI Won’t Cause Unemployment*. The core argument Andreessen puts forward is that regulation will come in and stop mass unemployment because it will make AI practically illegal across most of the economy. Like Scanlon, Andreessen also splits the economy into two, categorising one half as the blue sector — those that are unregulated, like tech and consumer goods, where prices fall, quality rises, and tech can disrupt freely — and the red sector, which is heavily regulated: healthcare, education, housing, and government. The sectors that see prices usually skyrocket and where tech is usually slow to disrupt.

The argument goes that AI simply won’t be allowed to eliminate a whole bunch of jobs because many of them require licensing that won’t be easily given up. We won’t be able to use AI doctors because you need medical licensing. Can’t use AI teachers because you need credentialing. The regulated sectors — the ones eating the economy, growing as a percentage of GDP — are protected by occupational cartels and regulatory capture. At the limit, Andreessen argues, 99% of the economy will be these regulated sectors. Therefore, AI cannot cause unemployment because it will be blocked from most of the economy.

Now, before you breathe that sigh of relief and give three cheers, let me add a little bit of nuance to this take. As someone who lives in Europe, I can well imagine governments reaching for the regulation lever in a whole host of jobs — in fact, that’s very likely to happen. But these are all jobs that are focused on a salary, on wages. These are all jobs in the material economy. So while the government might come in and regulate so people are still working in them, that won’t immediately translate into those jobs delivering workers any sort of material prosperity. The teacher will still teach, the nurse will still care for patients, and the government clerk will still process forms, but their wages won’t track with the productivity gains AI generates. They draw a salary but will be unable to afford the homes that appreciate in value or have pensions that let them retire with dignity. They’ll be employed, yes, but locked out of the mechanism that made the postwar middle class possible: access to appreciating assets. The statistical economy — the one where Nvidia creates trillions more every year with fewer and fewer workers — will continue to soar, but the material economy, where regulated workers earn wages, will continue to stagnate.

Regulation might save the jobs, but that doesn’t mean it will save prosperity.

## The Crime and Punishment of Being Ordinary

![Just your average American.]()

I started this essay with a nod to Middle America, not because things were perfect in the past, but because it captured a mood that, up until very recentl,y still felt fairly representative and aspirational: being average was a perfectly acceptable, normal thing to be. Yet somewhere in the past two decades, being average or even good enough has felt like the worst possible thing someone could be.

It’s all over social media. You’ve got to wake up at 5 am. You’ve got to hustle. Are you biohacking yet? Hairline receding? How dare you? Men and women alike are getting cosmetic touch-ups and facelifts before they’ve had their first stress line. It’s all preemptive boob jobs and Botox, and how dare you even think of being fat when we’ve got Ozempic. Everyone has to be exceptional, special, different, or if you’re not, have a really good reason not to be. People aren’t simply allowed to be.

And now we’ve moved from biohacking to soul-hacking. What do I mean by this? Just listen to the rhetoric from anyone in AI leadership. What’s the solution to not being replaced by machines? Lean into your humanity. Be more human than the machine can ever be — feel more, have better taste, better intuition. The scope of what we’re being valued on, what makes us worthy, is narrowing. Now, to feel like you’ve got another 18 months of employment, you need to convince yourself you’re the most uniquely human person that’s ever existed. But how many of us are like that, really?

Here’s what this rhetoric actually means: if creativity is what makes you human, then the uncreative aren’t fully human. If taste and intuition are what matter, then the person who competently does their accounting job, goes home, watches football, and grills on weekends is worth less. The philistine — the person who doesn’t particularly care about poetry or symphonies but shows up and does solid work — is being told they’re obsolete. We’re redefining humanity as exceptional humanity. And if you’re not exceptional, you don’t deserve prosperity.

This isn’t everyone. I understand that economically, those who are building, investing, and putting capital into innovations that change the world should be rewarded greatly. This isn’t a Marxist rant to bring down the billionaires. But it is a centrist plea to point out that people’s worth and humanity shouldn’t be tied to how exceptional or risk-taking they are. Scanlon tracks how younger generations are being pushed into the speculation economy — crypto, sports betting, meme stocks — because the material economy doesn’t work anymore. But that can’t be the only option: speculate, or you’re out. Get exceptional or get left behind.

The middle class was a framework that said ordinary competence deserves ordinary prosperity. A good-enough teacher, a solid accountant, a competent nurse — these people could buy homes, retire with dignity, send their kids to college. They didn’t need to be poets or prophets or founders. They just needed to show up and do their work well. That was enough.

We’re abandoning that framework. And in doing so, we’re not just eroding the middle class economically. We’re making a moral claim: that only the exceptional deserve dignified lives. That ordinary people — the majority of people — don’t merit access to prosperity anymore.

If that’s the case we’re making, we should at least be honest about it.
