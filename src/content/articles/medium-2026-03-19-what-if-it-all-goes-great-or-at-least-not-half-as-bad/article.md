# What if it all goes great? Or at least not half as bad?

### An optimist’s guide to the AI economy

![Photo by Diana Polekhina on Unsplash](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*rb2dhYTinxvXMLDLCm46YQ.jpeg)

### The Economy is about to get very weird

If you’ve been paying attention to the economy lately, you’ll know something is shifting. As Matt Shumer so hysterically put it in his now iconically viral post, *“*[*Something Big is Happening.*](https://fortune.com/2026/02/11/something-big-is-happening-ai-february-2020-moment-matt-shumer/)*”* In the last month alone, we’ve had [Jack Dorsey, of Twitter fame, slash nearly half the workforce](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cq570d12y9do) at his new venture, Block, citing AI efficiencies. We’ve seen Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, claim that [all knowledge workers will be replaced within five years](https://time.com/7289692/when-ai-replaces-workers/). And we’ve witnessed a massive sell-off that erased over [$300 billion in market value from the software sector](https://www.forbes.com/sites/donmuir/2026/02/04/300-billion-evaporated-the-saaspocalypse-has-begun/). For just £20 a month, Anthropic’s Claude can produce what an army of junior analysts at McKinsey would charge hundreds of thousands for.

Worth noting: [some analysts have called Dorsey’s move “AI-washing”](https://www.latimes.com/business/story/2026-03-02/ai-washing-how-companies-like-block-may-use-ai-as-layoff-excuse) — the suspicion being that Block over-hired badly during COVID and needed to cut regardless, with AI providing convenient cover. Maybe. But even if that’s true, the fact that AI is now the cover story that makes investors cheer an 18% stock price jump tells you something about where we are.

Without leaning into Shumer’s hysteria, it’s worth asking the question, “Is the house really on fire?” Honestly, I don’t know. In his counterargument to Shumer, [David Oks](https://open.substack.com/users/2088240-david-oks?utm_source=mentions) made a compelling case for why he isn’t worried. Is there sense in what he’s saying? Yes. But as I argued in *The Case Against Ordinary People*, jobs can stay while prosperity completely disappears. You can read both below.

[Why I'm not worried about AI job loss](https://davidoks.blog/p/why-im-not-worried-about-ai-job-loss?utm_source=substack&utm_campaign=post_embed&utm_medium=web)

Regardless of the size of whatever’s happening, something is happening, and it’s happening now. Yet the public conversation remains stuck in one of two camps. The doomers, who tell us we’ll all be unemployed by the end of the decade. And the techno-optimists, who say relax, technology always creates more jobs than it destroys, here’s a graph, now chill. Both camps, I suspect, are missing the more interesting and more unsettling question — what happens to an economy when its assumptions, mechanisms and policy frameworks are no longer fit for purpose?

It’s a tough question. I’ve personally found it a tough question. But in the spirit of using this forum to think out loud, here’s me thinking it loudly, if perhaps not entirely accurately.

[The Case Against Ordinary People](https://generativeai.pub/the-case-against-ordinary-people-a89f3ef3b39e)

### The Deflationary Dividend

![Photo by Shane Rounce on Unsplash](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*zKSBbIfH-iidwfTo-xjt2Q.jpeg)

In *The Case Against Ordinary People*, I cited a piece by [Marc Andreessen](https://open.substack.com/users/22353-marc-andreessen?utm_source=mentions) that made an argument I keep coming back to. He drew a simple distinction between two economies — one in blue, one in red. The blue economy covers sectors where technological innovation is allowed to run: consumer electronics, software, communications. In those sectors, prices fall and quality rises, year on year, almost without exception. The red economy covers sectors where technological innovation is effectively blocked by regulation, licensing, occupational gatekeeping and government control: healthcare, education, housing. In those sectors, prices go in one direction only. Up.

His point was blunt. We live in a world heading toward a flat-screen TV that covers your entire wall costing $100, and a four-year college degree costing $1 million, and nobody has anything resembling a plan to fix it.

[Why AI Won't Cause Unemployment](https://pmarca.substack.com/p/why-ai-wont-cause-unemployment?utm_source=substack&utm_campaign=post_embed&utm_medium=web)

He’s right. And it matters here because it frames the big promise of AI in a specific way. The argument isn’t just that AI will make things faster or cheaper in the abstract. It’s that AI might be the first force powerful enough to break into the red economy. To do to housing, healthcare and legal services what semiconductors did to televisions.

Think about what a television cost in 1954. [In the UK, a basic set ran to several months of an average worker’s wages.](https://www.theguardian.com/uk-news/2022/sep/10/from-rationing-to-reality-tv-how-britain-and-the-monarchy-changed-in-elizabeths-lifetime#:~:text=But%20to%20do%20so%20most%20of%20them,nearly%20two%20months'%20wages%20for%20many%20people.) Today, you can buy a vastly superior one for less than a day’s pay, and that’s all due to the fact we got dramatically better at making them, and the efficiency gains passed through to the consumer. As a product improves, its price goes down. This is the promise technology brings when it’s allowed to run.

Now apply that same thinking to a house. In Toronto, regulatory barriers — permits, compliance costs, planning approvals and the endless procession of sign-offs — add a [staggering $350,000 to the price of a single-family home before a single brick is laid or nail hammered](https://www.bildgta.ca/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/2024-GTA-Municipal-Benchmarking-Study-Our-number-7147-Final.pdf). Paper, it seems, is one of the most expensive line items in the building of a new home. Yet imagine if AI is empowered to navigate planning systems, generate compliant architectural designs, automate legal conveyancing and compress a near 32-month approvals process into something approaching a reasonable timeframe. The cost of building a house would fall to a point where the housing crisis in Canada would most likely be solved. In all the AI doom and gloom, that’s a truly good thing.

That’s what economists call the deflationary dividend. When the overheads of an economy are hollowed out by automation, the gains flow downwards to those who benefit the most.

Now extrapolate that across everything. Legal advice that currently costs hundreds of pounds an hour, automated. Financial planning, democratised. [Drug discovery, which has been mired in a decades-long innovation drought](https://www.drugtargetreview.com/article/192951/ai-in-drug-discovery-2025-in-review/#:~:text=This%20clinical%20attrition%20underscores%20the,than%20a%20proven%20paradigm%20shift.), supercharged by AI systems capable of modelling protein interactions and identifying viable compounds at a scale no human research team could match. Energy, where AI-optimised grid management and accelerated materials science could finally make clean power genuinely cheap and universally accessible. [Diagnostics in medicine, where the difference between catching cancer at stage one and stage four is often just access to a specialist](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12570495/) — access that most of the world doesn’t have, but an AI trained on millions of scans might provide for almost nothing.

Just think about the scientific and innovation stagnation we’ve been sitting in for the past two decades. Breakthroughs across whole fields have slowed to a crawl, and AI presents something close to a genuine panacea — the potential to reignite our collective creative fire and deliver the most significant gains to human health and flourishing we’ve seen since the invention of penicillin. Wouldn’t that be something? Just imagine cancer treatment in the United States that doesn’t lead to chemistry teachers becoming drug lords. Sure, it would be less entertaining. But perhaps the White family would have survived.

I’m not mad at the techno-optimists for looking forward to this world. It sounds genuinely, almost embarrassingly, promising.

### Back to the Future

Housing, health, and a dollar that goes further than you’ve ever known it to. There’s a very rosy version of all of this that might see us go all the way back to the 1950s, sans the misogyny and discrimination. A world where a family can comfortably survive on a single income. Where people can opt to work three-day weeks because productivity is no longer tied to time and effort in the way it once was. If that’s the world we’re heading toward, sign me up.

But to get there would take a monumental effort that I fear no government, anywhere in the world, is currently prepared for. Say they were, though. Let’s do a bit of creative writing and imagine the conditions it would take for 2040 to look something like 1950.

The deflationary dividend only works if people still have income to spend. Deflation from productivity gains is a positive thing. Deflation from demand collapse is catastrophic. Oks is probably right that jobs won’t disappear overnight, but the more important variable is wage pressure. The ratio of income to costs is what actually determines living standards — not wages in isolation. AI could restore that ratio not by raising wages but simply by collapsing costs faster than wages fall. Given the wage stagnation the West has absorbed since 2008, that’s a more achievable scenario than it sounds.

The bigger risk isn’t unemployment. It’s confidence. The Great Depression was as much a psychological event as it was an economic one. When prices started falling in the early 1930s, people didn’t think, “Great, things are getting cheaper.” They thought, “Things are getting cheaper because something is very wrong.” They stopped spending. Businesses cut production. Workers lost jobs and income. Less income meant less spending, which meant prices fell further, which meant more fear, which meant less spending still. The spiral fed on itself until a decade of misery had been wrung out of the global economy. That is the version of deflation we cannot afford to repeat. The difference between that and the world we’re imagining is confidence. It is, in the end, almost entirely a question of whether people believe the falling prices are a sign of progress or a sign of collapse. Which means governments would need to do something they are historically not very good at — tell the truth, clearly and consistently, before the panic sets in, rather than after.

### Regulation, Taxation and Debt

For argument’s sake, we’ve agreed that prices going down through AI efficiency gains is a positive thing. For that to hold, however, we need all those regulated industries that Andreessen wrote about to actually embrace AI rather than fight it. And the most likely scenario is that they don’t. Lawyers will lawyer and planners will plan, and between them, there will be a lobbying movement like no other to hold on for dear life. Something completely rational given the potential existential threat they’re facing.

The trick, as [Ethan Mollick](https://open.substack.com/users/846835-ethan-mollick?utm_source=mentions) put forward in his 2023 book Co-Intelligence, would be to embrace what he calls the centaur model — a human and AI working in tandem, each doing what they do best, rather than one replacing the other. The centaur lawyer doesn’t get replaced by AI. They use AI to handle research, drafting and compliance at a fraction of the current cost, passing those savings to clients while focusing their own time on judgment, relationships and advocacy. The argument for why industries would choose this path over the lobbying path is ultimately a competitive one. The firm that embraces the centaur model undercuts the one that doesn’t. The hospital that uses AI diagnostics sees more patients at a lower cost. First movers win. And once enough first movers win, the holdouts face a choice between adapting and becoming irrelevant. An almost impossible ask in the short term, I’ll grant you. But remember, this is a creative writing exercise. In this world, it happens. Magic.

Right, so prices are dropping and regulated industries are working with AI rather than against it. Now we need to talk about taxation, because without getting that right, the whole thing unravels.

In *En Route to Serfdom*, I argued that UBI as the primary resort for human subsistence is a path to dependency — one that strips away agency from individuals as both consumers and political actors. I stand by that. But in researching that piece, I came across something worth revisiting. Since 1982, [the state of Alaska has run a Permanent Fund Dividend](https://www.forbes.com/sites/shaharziv/2025/03/19/alaska-stimulus-check-2025-when-pfd-payments-arrive-and-how-much/) that takes a portion of oil revenues, invests them in a sovereign wealth fund, and distributes the returns to every qualifying Alaskan resident annually. Around $1,600 a year on average. It has helped Alaska maintain the highest level of economic equality of any US state, and crucially, it doesn’t feel like a state handout. It rightly feels like a dividend. A share of common wealth that belongs to everyone because the resource it came from belongs to everyone.

That analogy to AI isn’t perfect, but there’s an argument to be made. The productivity gains from automation were built on publicly funded research, trained on data we all collectively generated, and deployed on infrastructure society built. There’s a case in there that those gains shouldn’t accrue entirely to shareholders. A portion could flow into a sovereign fund — fed by a tax on AI-driven efficiency gains — and deployed not as universal income but as something more targeted. Transition support for displaced workers. Something time-limited and tied to participation. Not a cheque in the post but a bridge to the future.

[En Route to Serfdom](https://generativeai.pub/en-route-to-serfdom-35afe12d2ca5)

Or perhaps we use that same fund to address the debt problem. Because those of you with an Econ 101 degree in your utility belt will be thinking, “But Camilo, you haven’t addressed the biggest hole in your argument.” So let’s address it.

Most household wealth in developed economies sits in property, bought with mortgages that were structured around the assumption of continued inflation. If AI were to genuinely deflate housing costs, existing homeowners would face their assets falling below their outstanding debt. Negative equity at scale leads to another 2008. And we don’t want another 2008. The people who most need cheaper housing are renters who can’t afford to buy at inflated prices, but the people most exposed to falling values are the mortgaged middle class that currently own them. What you end up with there is a fractured society that quickly turns into mass civil unrest. But in this version of the world — remember, magic and creative writing — we figure it out.

Why? Because we set up a sovereign productivity fund that’s intelligently deployed to cushion that from happening. It wouldn’t eliminate the problem, but cushion it sufficiently that the transition doesn’t become a confidence collapse. Remember, prices falling need to feel like progress, not a reason for mass panic.

It’s a lot of conditions, I know. An almost laughable number of things would need to go right simultaneously for any of it to work. But here’s what gives me at least some hope. The entire architecture of modern monetary policy — the 2% inflation target that every central bank on earth treats as gospel — wasn’t handed down from Ricardo or Adam Smith. It came from [an offhand remark made by a New Zealand finance minister named Roger Douglas during a television interview in 1988](https://www.cfr.org/articles/history-and-future-federal-reserves-2-percent-target-rate-inflation-0). Someone pointed a camera at him, he said he’d like inflation somewhere between zero and one percent, and the rest of the world eventually built its monetary framework around it. The head of the Reserve Bank of New Zealand later admitted the number was, in his words, “plucked out of thin air.”

If we built the world we have on that, maybe we can build something better on purpose.

### Mr Brightside

![Photo by Clay Banks on Unsplash](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*C2Tn7zJaNvRP9mM9NuY7jA.jpeg)

I appreciate this is somewhat of a far-fetched essay for me. If you’re a regular here, you’ll know this isn’t really my style. But I felt it was necessary to put some sort of hope out there — to test whether the case for optimism can be made seriously, and not just have to rely on vibes.

Because here’s the thing. We are exposed to so much information on a daily basis, from the war in Iran to $300 billion being wiped from SaaS companies in a sell-off, to every podcast on earth telling us we’ve got five years left before it’s either World War 3 or World War Z, that it’s genuinely difficult to ground yourself and work out what’s actually happening. And that’s coming from someone fairly close to the action.

Here’s what I do know. Even if the technology never improved — and it’s going to improve — it’s already having a profoundly transformative effect on education, health and knowledge work. I also know that confidence isn’t tracking well. It feels like we’re one viral post away from everyone going back to hoarding toilet paper and water bottles. The virality of both the Shumer post and the Citrini Research paper that triggered what’s now being called the SaaSpocalypse is evidence of that. What makes it worse is the burden of knowledge. All the negative coverage might be right, and the ripple effects simply haven’t landed yet — which means that when you unplug from the internet and go touch grass, everything feels normal. Maybe it is. Maybe it will be. But equally, maybe it won’t.

After spending much of this year focused on what we stand to lose, I wanted to spend some time on what we might gain and how we might protect what we have. It’s easy to sit and flag the problems. Much harder to put forward working solutions, however fantastical they might be.

One last thing before I go. The entire argument of this essay rests on an assumption I haven’t fully resolved: that the capital freed up by AI productivity flows back to people rather than straight back into compute. Right now, the highest return on capital isn’t affordable housing or cheaper healthcare. It’s more AI. More chips. More data centres. More of the machine feeding itself. I tried to wrestle that question into this essay, and it wouldn’t fit. It demands its own piece. So watch out for the follow-up, which will tackle what happens when the deflationary dividend gets swallowed before it ever reaches anyone — and what, if anything, we can do about it.
