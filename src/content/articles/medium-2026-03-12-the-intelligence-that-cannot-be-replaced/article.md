# **The Intelligence That Cannot Be Replaced**

[Sophie Bertin](https://sophie-bertin.medium.com/?source=post_page---byline--11b6f0a639c0---------------------------------------)

16 min read·Feb 25, 2026

\--

*Why the doomsayers are wrong about AI, human intelligence, and the future*

There has never been a better moment to be a thinking, feeling, curious human being.

The tools now available to us: to anyone with a phone and an internet connection, are without precedent in history. For the first time, every person has access to a tireless thinking partner that can challenge their assumptions, expand their knowledge, pressure-test their ideas, and help them grow in ways that were previously reserved for the very privileged few. The democratisation of intellectual development is happening right now, in real time, and it is extraordinary.

And yet. Every few weeks, a new piece of speculative fiction, dressed in the authority of financial analysis, floods our feeds. The latest incarnation imagines a world by 2028[\[1\]](#_ftn1): 10% unemployment. Markets down 38%. White-collar workers driving Uber while AI writes all the code and makes all the decisions. The future already decided. And bleak.

I want to push back. Not because the transition ahead is painless, it is not. Not because there are no real risks to monitor, there are. But because the story being told about human beings and their place in an AI world is profoundly, demonstrably wrong. And because the telling of that story causes real harm.

What follows is my attempt to show you why, and more importantly, to show you what the actual story looks like, and that it depends on us to make this chapter the most exciting in the human history.

## Part I : The flaws in the doomsday scenario

### First: why these scenarios are so seductive

Before examining the reasoning, we need to understand why this kind of reasoning is so compelling. The problem is not just that doom scenarios are logically flawed. It is that they are psychologically irresistible.

Human brains are wired for threat detection. This was a survival advantage on the savanna: the individual who noticed danger first lived longest. Psychologists call it negativity bias: negative information captures attention faster, is processed more deeply, and is remembered longer than positive information of equivalent magnitude. In cognitive science, this is called the availability heuristic: we assess the probability of future events based on how easily we can imagine them. A vivid, detailed, emotionally charged catastrophe scenario feels more probable simply because it is more memorable.

Doom sells.

Not because editors are malicious, but because clicks are the feedback signal, and catastrophe generates clicks. The media ecosystem has a structural incentive to produce compelling disaster narratives, they activate the exact neurological circuits that make us stop scrolling. A scenario titled ‘Things Will Mostly Work Out, With Some Necessary Adjustment’ generates no traffic. ‘The Global Intelligence Crisis: A Post-Mortem on the Pre-Crisis Economy’ generates hundreds of thousands of shares.

This does not mean the risks described are fabricated. Some are real and worth tracking. But it does mean we should apply extra critical scrutiny to doom narratives, precisely because our cognitive architecture is designed to accept them uncritically. The very feeling of ‘this is so compelling it must be right’ is precisely the signal to slow down, take a breath, and examine the assumptions, because that pause is exactly what reactivates the prefrontal cortex and allows clear thinking to re-emerge.

### The fatal premise: a thin model of intelligence

The scenario’s entire logic rests on treating human intelligence as task performance. Humans do tasks. AI does tasks faster and cheaper. Therefore, humans become economically redundant.

But this model of intelligence is impoverished. Human intelligence is not merely the execution of tasks, it is meaning-making, relationship, moral agency, embodied judgment, and the navigation of irreducibly complex social realities. A doctor who listens. A teacher who believes in a student when no one else does. A leader who creates courage in others at the moment of crisis. A group of people spending time together to discuss a piece of art and create story that shape them. These are not tasks that any sufficiently advanced AI replaces. They are modes of being that constitute what human intelligence is.

When we mistake the map for the territory, when we mistake ‘what AI can measure and replicate about intelligence’ for intelligence itself, we make a category error that distorts everything that follows.

### The lump of labor fallacy

The scenario assumes, without proving, that there is a fixed amount of work in the economy, and that AI is taking humanity’s share. This is the Lump of Labor Fallacy and it has been empirically wrong for at least 250 years of technological revolution.

Human wants are infinite, elastic, and most importantly, unpredictable before the environment shifts. When productivity rises and costs fall, new categories of desire emerge that didn’t exist before. Nobody in 1900 wanted a UX designer, a podcast producer, a life coach, a digital artist, or a wellness consultant. Those needs didn’t exist in articulated form. They emerged because rising productivity freed up human time and purchasing power to explore them.

The scenario acknowledges this: ‘Technological innovation destroys jobs and then creates even more’, but then dismisses it with a wave: ‘AI is different because it can do the new jobs too.’ But this is asserted, not demonstrated in any ways or forms. Every previous generation of technologists made exactly the same claim about their disruption being uniquely terminal. Every previous generation was wrong about permanence while being right about the pain of transition.

### The Baumol effect: why human labor becomes more valuable

There is a structural economic force the scenario entirely ignores. In the 1960s, economist William Baumol identified a powerful dynamic: productivity gains in one sector raise the relative value of human-intensive sectors that cannot be automated. Orchestras don’t get faster. A lived experience cannot be fast-tracked. Genuine mentorship, craft, live performance, and authentic human presence become more valuable as AI makes everything else cheaper.

The more AI commoditizes information processing, the more the economy tilts toward irreducibly human experiences. This is not wishful thinking. It is a structural counterweight built into the logic of markets.

### The speed problem: economies move like container ships

The scenario travels from ‘agentic coding improves’ to ‘10.2% unemployment and financial crisis’ in approximately two years. This is fantasy speed. Real enterprises operate inside legal obligations, security reviews, organizational change management, institutional inertia, union agreements, risk aversion, and human psychology. The scenario runs at startup speed. Large economies do not.

Every technology adoption curve in history — electricity, computers, the internet — showed the same pattern: capability arrived decades before broad economic transformation. The gap between ‘AI can do this’ and ‘AI has replaced this at scale across the economy’ is measured in years, often decades. That gap is where humanity adapts. And for those who venture outside the AI bubble: as of early 2026, only around 14% of humanity has meaningfully engaged with AI at all.

### Tasks are not jobs

The scenario conflates the automation of tasks within jobs with the elimination of jobs. Research shows that AI automates the routine, measurable components of knowledge work, while the irreducibly human components (organizational judgment, stakeholder trust, navigating ambiguity, cultural leadership) remain stubbornly resistant. Accountants survived spreadsheets. Lawyers survived document search. Radiologists survived early diagnostic AI. Mathematicians survived the Texas Instrument calculator. The job evolved, it didn’t disappear.

### The self-fulfilling prophecy problem

Here is the flaw the scenario cannot see in itself: it is not merely a prediction of crisis. Widely circulated, it becomes a contributor to one.

When millions of professionals read that their incomes are structurally impaired, that their jobs are next, that the economy is heading toward a 38% market crash — they respond. They defer hiring decisions. They slow capital deployment. They reduce discretionary spending. They freeze in place rather than adapt and invest. Boards become risk averse. Entrepreneurs hesitate. The very behaviors the scenario predicts begin to emerge not because the underlying economic logic was inevitable, but because the narrative shaped the behavior of the actors inside the economy.

This is the mechanism of a self-fulfilling prophecy. George Soros built an entire investment philosophy around it: in complex social systems, the beliefs of participants are not separate from outcomes, they are constitutive of them. A sufficiently widely believed financial crisis narrative can trigger the capital flight, the credit tightening, and the demand destruction that produces a financial crisis. The prediction participates in creating what it describes.

This makes doom scenarios not just intellectually flawed but actively harmful when they achieve mass circulation.

The authors disclaim: ‘This is a scenario, not a prediction.’ But the human brain does not read disclaimers. It reads vivid, emotionally charged narratives and encodes them as probable futures. The scenario plants seeds of fear in the minds of exactly the decision-makers: executives, investors, hiring managers, whose decisions will determine whether something resembling the scenario comes to pass.

A more responsible framing would spend equal effort on the conditions under which the negative feedback loops do not materialize, because those conditions are just as plausible, and because believing in them is a prerequisite for creating them.

## Part II: What two hundred and fifty years of history actually tells us

The Industrial Revolution was, by every contemporary account, uniquely catastrophic.. Mills replaced artisans. Steam engines replaced entire categories of physical labour. Children worked in coal mines. Millions were torn from agricultural communities and thrown into brutal urban conditions. Serious intellectuals of the time wrote extensively about the permanent degradation of the human worker. The pain was real, prolonged, and devastating for those who lived through it.

But then, over generations, it produced the largest sustained increase in human living standards in history. Universal literacy. Modern medicine. Life expectancy doubling. Access to art, travel, education, and leisure for people whose ancestors had none of it.

The children and grandchildren of the very type of workers whose displacement was mourned became the beneficiaries of a prosperity their grandparents could not have imagined.

The pattern has repeated with every general-purpose technology since. The printing press was going to make knowledge dangerous and uncontrollable, and there were even riots in Paris against Gutenberg’s press. Electricity was going to displace skilled craftspeople and destroy communities built around trades. The computer was going to make human calculation obsolete. The internet was going to destroy journalism, retail, and human attention spans. In every case: the disruption was real, the transition was (sometimes) painful, but the long arc bent toward abundance.

What is different this time is not that AI is more destructive than previous general-purpose technologies. What is different is the speed, and speed is where policy, institutions, and human adaptation must rise to meet the challenge. That is a solvable problem, which we can solve as humans and it is absolutely not a reason to surrender to despair.

And here is the moment where the doom scenario, despite itself, tells the truth. Buried near its end, almost as an afterthought, is this sentence:

*‘The economy can find a new equilibrium. Getting there is one of the few tasks left that only humans can do.’*

Read that again. The authors of the doom scenario, after ten thousand words of financial crisis modelling, arrive at the same conclusion: the task of building the future belongs to humans. Not to AI. Not to markets. To us. To you and me, to all of us.

They wrote the rebuttal to their own doom. They just didn’t lead with it.

## Part III: The case for a brighter future

### New scarcity creates new value

The scenario correctly identifies that human intelligence becomes less scarce in terms of raw information processing. But scarcity is not the only source of value, and the scenario’s implicit economic model stops there.

Authenticity becomes scarcer. Trust becomes scarcer. Genuine human presence and relationship become scarcer. In a world flooded with AI-generated content and AI-automated processes, the human who shows up fully, who has cultivated depth, who can be trusted, who brings earned wisdom rather than optimized output becomes more valuable, not less.

Think about what you trust most in your own life. It is not the most efficient source of information. It is the person who has demonstrated, over time, that they see you, that they care about truth, that they have wrestled with hard things and come through them. That kind of trust cannot be generated at inference time. It is built through lived experience. And it is increasingly rare.

### The amplification premium

I have written elsewhere about what I call the Great Amplifier effect: AI does not replace human intelligence, it reveals and amplifies what was already there. The human who brings clarity of thought, emotional depth, creative vision, and ethical grounding to an AI-augmented environment becomes dramatically more powerful than before. The AI handles the throughput. The human provides the direction, the meaning, the judgment.

This is not a consolation prize. It is a genuine competitive advantage and it belongs to those willing to do the inner work, bring out the curiosity and use it to progress. The bottleneck in an AI-augmented world is not cognitive throughput. It is wisdom, discernment, curiosity and character. These are distinctly human and extraordinarily difficult to develop even in humans, let alone machines.

### The economy of genuine connection

Consider what happens as AI generates more content, more analysis, more product, more service at lower and lower cost. The economy of attention shifts. The things that cannot be scaled: genuine mentorship; live artistic performance; embodied craft; deep therapeutic relationship; community; a walk in nature; meaning; they all command a premium precisely because they are irreplaceable.

We are already seeing the early signals. The explosion of interest in live experiences. The premium placed on artisanal, handmade, locally produced goods. The growing market for coaching, therapy, and personal development. These are not nostalgic retreats from modernity. They are rational responses to a world in which AI makes everything else abundant.

Abundance in one dimension creates scarcity in another. The scarce dimension, in an AI-abundant world, is the irreducibly human.

## Part IV: the real question: what kind of Human will you become?

Most conversations about AI stop at the level of economics, policy, and disruption. But the deeper question is not what AI does to the labour market. It is what AI makes possible for human development.

Because for the first time in history, every person with access to an AI has access to something that was previously reserved for the few: a tireless, infinitely patient sparring partner that can challenge your assumptions, expand your knowledge, pressure-test your ideas, and reflect your reasoning back with a clarity that accelerates growth.

### Why fear is cognitively disabling

There is a neuroscience argument against doom narratives that goes beyond philosophy or economics. Fear physiologically impairs the cognitive capacities we need most right now.

When the brain perceives threat: including the abstract, narrative threat seeded by a vivid doomsday scenario, it activates the amygdala and triggers a cortisol stress response. This is the fight-or-flight system, designed for immediate physical danger. What it does to cognition is well-documented and devastating: access to the prefrontal cortex, the seat of complex reasoning, long-term planning, creative thinking, and nuanced decision-making, is reduced. We become more reactive, more binary in our thinking, and less capable of imagining futures that diverge from the threat we perceive.

In other words: a person who has deeply internalized a doom narrative about AI and the economy is neurologically less equipped to navigate the actual challenges of the AI transition. The fear does not sharpen them. On the contrary, it dulls them. It narrows their field of possible futures to the one they dread, making adaptation, which is the very thing the situation requires, much harder to conceive and harder to execute.

This is why the choice of narrative is beyond aesthetic or philosophical. It is a practical, cognitive, and strategic decision. The person who holds a vision of possibility, be it even a sober, realistic, non-naive one, has access to more of their brain than the person paralyzed by existential dread. They can plan. They can adapt. They can see opportunities where others see only threats.

Choosing a constructive narrative in the face of genuine uncertainty is not wishful thinking. It is the precondition for intelligent action. The doomers, by flooding the information environment with maximally frightening scenarios, are not making us more prepared. They are making us less capable of preparing. And this is central to our understanding why we shall reject outright such doomers’ scenarios.

### AI as a sparring partner, as a mirror for consciousness

![]()

Think about what it means to have access to the equivalent of a Socratic interlocutor, available at any moment, on any topic, calibrated to your level of understanding. For centuries, the development of rigorous thinking was available only to those with access to great teachers, great universities, or the leisure to read deeply. Now it is available to anyone.

But more importantly, the tool only amplifies what you bring to it.

An AI sparring partner that interacts with a shallow thinker produces shallow outputs faster. An AI receiving well-crafted questions with clear intention delivers answers of corresponding depth. And an AI working alongside someone committed to genuine self-examination, clear values, and honest inquiry produces something truly extraordinary.

This is the principle the doom scenarios entirely miss. They model AI as a replacement for human cognition. The more accurate model is AI as a mirror and a lever. What it mirrors and what it leverages depends entirely on the human using it.

In spiritual and philosophical traditions across centuries, the idea of conscious creation rests on a simple but demanding premise: the quality of what you bring into the world is a direct reflection of the quality of your inner state.

Bring clarity, and you create clarity. Bring fear, and you amplify fear. Bring wisdom, and you generate wisdom.

AI has made the law of conscious creation visible and undeniable in a way not possible before, because the feedback loop between inner state and outer result is now much faster, legible, and impossible to ignore.

This means the most valuable investment any person can make right now is not only in a technical skill. It is in the development of their own thinking, their emotional intelligence, their capacity for honest self-reflection, their clarity about what they value and why, and their own intuition. These are inputs for a better life, which also equips us to approach the use of AI as an amplifier of positive use, not as a treat.

### **Elevating the human through the machine**

Here is the paradox the doomers cannot see: the better AI gets at executing, the more valuable the distinctly human capacities become. Not as a consolation, as the actual competitive frontier.

The students who will thrive in the next decade are not those who learn to code faster than AI or process data faster than AI — these are mechanical tasks that AI does better, and this is genuinely good news. They are those who learn to think better than they thought before, using AI as a rigorous sparring partner to pressure-test every idea, challenge every assumption, expose every blind spot, and reach the next level of understanding.

The leaders who will build the most enduring organisations are not those who replace the most workers with AI. They are those who cultivate the kind of wisdom, trust, and genuine vision that AI cannot synthesise, and who use AI as a tool to amplify those qualities across everything they build, bringing people with them on that journey.

The creators who will matter are not those who generate the most content. They are those who bring genuine, deeply personal perspective earned through real experience and use AI to express it more fully than they could alone. Those who saw images in their mind but could never draw them. Those who had stories to tell but not the time to tell them. Those who had insights and no tool to articulate them for others to understand.

The people who uses AI as a sparring partner and co-creator to think more clearly, challenge their own assumptions more rigorously, learn faster and explore more widely are not being replaced, and they are also freeing time for other activities. These people can reach a new level in their development, with capabilities that compound over time: judgment, wisdom, creative vision, ethical depth, genuine relationship. These are the things that AI cannot generate because they emerge from lived experience, from struggle, from the slow work of becoming.

There are all the aspects that the doomsayers do not understand or do not want to see. However, it is my personal conviction that we must point these aspects to, to help people see the opportunities ahead, and go and experience them for themselves.

### The choice in front of us: our butterfly moment

We face a choice: to be defined by the disruption, or to embrace it and grow through it. I see that our butterfly moment, when the caterpillar must go through a transition for a better future.

The doomers are not wrong that transition is painful. They are not wrong that policy needs to respond faster. They are not wrong that the gains from AI are currently accruing too narrowly. These are real problems requiring real solutions in redistribution, in education, in institutional adaptation and we must address them.

But the doomers are profoundly wrong to conflate the pain of transition with the inevitability of human obsolescence. And they are missing the most important story of our moment: that for the first time in history, the tool disrupting the economy is also the most powerful instrument for human self-development ever created.

The question is not whether AI will change everything, as it will. The deep question is: what kind of human will you be on the other side of that change?

Will you be the person who waited for the disruption pass, hoping it did not happen or that you will be spared? Or will you be the person who used this moment, this extraordinary, unprecedented moment, to embrace the change, see the opportunities, and bring something genuinely irreplaceable into the world? Your unique voice. Your earned perspective. Your irreducibly human presence.

The canary is still alive. So is human potential.

*And if you ask me, it has never had a better environment to evolve.*

*— Written as an optimist, and a firm believer in the human potential*
