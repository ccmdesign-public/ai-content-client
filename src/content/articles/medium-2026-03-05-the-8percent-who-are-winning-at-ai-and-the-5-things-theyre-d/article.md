# The 8% Who Are Winning at AI — And the 5 Things They’re Doing Differently

[Vijay Prajapati](https://scriptshub.medium.com/?source=post_page---byline--2ef3d8c45569---------------------------------------)

9 min read·2 hours ago

\--

**A small group of companies are turning AI into a structural competitive advantage. Everyone else is funding their rise. Here’s what’s actually separating them — and why 2026 is the last realistic window to cross the gap.**

I want to start with a number that stopped me cold when I read it last month.

According to PwC’s 29th Global CEO Survey — published in January 2026, drawing on responses from 4,454 CEOs across 95 countries — **only 1 in 8 CEOs say AI has delivered both cost and revenue benefits simultaneously.**

Read that again. One in eight.

This isn’t a survey of small businesses experimenting with chatbots. These are the chief executives of the world’s largest organizations, many of whom have spent hundreds of millions of dollars on AI over the past three years. And only 12% have reached the point where AI is meaningfully moving both sides of the income statement.

Here’s what makes that number genuinely alarming in March 2026: **the other 12% are pulling away from the field at a pace that may soon be impossible to close.**

## The divide is now measurable — and it’s widening fast

We spent most of 2024 and 2025 debating whether enterprise AI would deliver real returns. That debate is over. The question for 2026 is sharper and more urgent: **which side of the divide are you on — and can you still get to the other side?**

The data from the first two months of 2026 paints a picture of two entirely different business realities running in parallel.

For companies that have built genuine AI foundations, the results are compounding. PwC’s analysis found that **companies applying AI widely to products, services, and customer experiences achieved nearly four percentage points higher profit margins** than those that did not. McKinsey’s industry study goes further: **companies in the AI leader category are seeing six times the revenue growth** compared to their laggard counterparts.

Six times. That is not a margin. That is a different game entirely.

![PwC Global CEO Survey]()

NTT DATA’s 2026 Global AI Report — spanning 35 markets, 15 industries, and 2,567 C-suite decision-makers — captures why. The finding is deceptively simple: **AI leaders don’t treat AI as a side project. For them, AI doesn’t support the business strategy. It is the strategy.** They are rethinking how decisions get made, how products get built, and how teams operate — not optimizing what already exists.

Meanwhile, for the majority still in the middle, the picture is sobering. Deloitte’s 2026 State of AI in the Enterprise report found that **only 34% of organizations are truly reimagining their business** with AI. Another 30% are redesigning key processes. And a full **37% are using AI at a surface level, with little or no change to existing processes** — capturing marginal efficiency gains while competitors restructure their operating models around them.

The gap isn’t theoretical. It’s showing up in confidence, in margins, and increasingly, in market share.

## Why most companies are stuck — despite real spending

Here is the paradox that defines enterprise AI in early 2026: investment is at an all-time high, but the average AI maturity score is going down.

ServiceNow’s research revealed that **the average enterprise AI maturity score dropped nine points** — from 44 to 35 — over the past twelve months. Companies are spending more on AI while simultaneously getting worse at deploying it strategically. The speed of AI capability development has outpaced most organizations’ ability to absorb, govern, and scale it.

The PwC CEO Survey reinforces this from the top. CEO confidence in revenue growth has fallen to its lowest level in five years — **only 30% of CEOs are confident about their revenue outlook for 2026**, down from 38% a year ago and 56% in 2022. The dominant reason cited is the uneven, often invisible return on AI investment.

![Enterpise AI: Leader vs Laggard Cluster Analysis]()

And yet — crucially — **56% of CEOs acknowledge their organizations are getting nothing meaningful from their AI investments** at all.

So where is the money going? The same place it has always gone when strategy is absent: into pilots that never scale, into tools that never get adopted, and into infrastructure that gets built before anyone has defined what problem it is solving.

## What the 8% are actually doing differently

Here is what the freshest research from PwC, Deloitte, NTT DATA, and OpenAI consistently shows about the organizations generating real, compounding AI returns in 2026. It is not about which models they use or how much they spend. It comes down to five structural differences.

![Top 5 Differentiators]()

### 1\. They made AI a board-level mandate, not an IT project

PwC found that **CEOs whose organizations have established strong AI foundations are three times more likely to report meaningful financial returns.** The word “foundations” is doing a lot of work in that sentence. It means responsible AI frameworks, enterprise-wide integration capability, and critically — executive ownership that sits at the CEO and board level, not delegated to a technology team.

NTT DATA’s data shows that **55.9% of AI leaders follow a centralized AI governance model**, compared to just 33.3% of laggards. Governance sounds like a compliance function. In reality, it is the mechanism through which AI strategy gets enforced, measured, and scaled. Without it, every team makes its own decisions, and the enterprise never compounds.

### 2\. They invest in breadth, not just depth

OpenAI’s first State of Enterprise AI report — published in December 2025, drawing on data from over one million business customers — revealed a striking internal divide within organizations. **Frontier workers, the top 5% by AI usage, send 17 times more messages than the median employee** on coding tasks alone.

That number illustrates a critical failure mode: most enterprises have a handful of power users and a large passive majority. The organizations generating the highest returns are not deepening usage among their existing champions — they are systematically broadening adoption across roles, functions, and task types. The compounding returns favor organizations where AI is a general-purpose capability embedded across the business, not a specialist tool used by a few.

### 3\. They measure outcomes, not activity

One of the clearest signals separating leaders from laggards in 2026 is how they define success. Laggards measure licenses purchased, tools deployed, and hours of training delivered. Leaders measure revenue growth, cost avoidance, cycle time reduction, and margin impact.

Deloitte’s 2026 report is direct on this: organizations with AI-ready data report **26% improvement in business outcomes**. But more telling is the governance stat — **organizations with central data policies defined and monitored are three times more likely to be among the outperformers**. The measurement discipline and the data discipline are the same discipline.

### 4\. They build foundations before chasing frontiers

The research from Lucidworks — analyzing over 1,100 company deployments — found a consistent pattern: **companies that put foundational capabilities in place first see far greater returns than those chasing advanced tools before basics are solid.** Even the most advanced adopters report diminishing returns when core systems are weak.

This sounds obvious. It is not how most enterprises behave. The pull toward exciting, visible AI capabilities — agents, generative tools, autonomous workflows — is real and intense. The leaders resist it long enough to build the data infrastructure, integration architecture, and change management muscle that makes the exciting capabilities actually work at scale.

### 5\. They treat workforce redesign as the real work

PwC’s analysis of what drives AI value contains a ratio every executive should internalize: **technology delivers only about 20% of an initiative’s value. The other 80% comes from redesigning work.** This is the 2026 version of a finding that has appeared across BCG, McKinsey, and Deloitte research for three years running — and most organizations are still not acting on it.

Deloitte found that AI skills gaps are the biggest barrier to AI integration — but the response from most organizations is to educate employees on existing tools, not to redesign roles and workflows around AI capabilities. The leaders are doing the harder thing: **pioneering companies upskill more than 50% of employees with role-specific AI skills**, are six times more likely to carve out structured learning time, and five times more likely to engage in strategic workforce planning for AI.

## 2026 is the inflection point — and the window is narrowing

Multiple research reports published in early 2026 converge on a conclusion that should be uncomfortable reading for any executive still in pilot mode.

Analysts are now characterizing **2026 as the year the gap between AI leaders and laggards risks becoming permanent.** The logic is structural: AI leaders are embedding their advantages into competitive positioning, talent acquisition, product development, and customer relationships. The longer the gap persists, the harder it becomes to close — not because the technology gets locked up, but because organizational capability compounds on one side and atrophies on the other.

PwC’s own CEO survey framing from January 2026 was pointed: *“2026 is shaping up as a decisive year for AI. A small group of companies are already turning AI into measurable financial returns, while many others are still struggling to move beyond pilots. That gap is starting to show up in confidence and competitiveness — and it will widen quickly for those that don’t act.”*

The NTT DATA report puts the current state plainly: only **8% of companies are genuinely maximizing AI to its full potential** today. That leaves 92% — including many organizations that believe they are ahead — either in early stages or executing without the strategic coherence that produces compounding returns.

That 8% is not a ceiling. Right now, it is still an open door.

## Three questions to find out which side you are on

If you are a CIO, CTO, or CEO reading this, here is the honest diagnostic I use with every enterprise client before we discuss any technology:

![3 Diagnostic Questions]()

**One: Does your CEO personally sponsor your AI program?** PwC’s data shows CEO-level sponsorship is the single strongest predictor of meaningful financial returns. Not the CTO’s. Not the CDO’s. The CEO’s. If your AI strategy lives entirely inside the technology function, it will not compound into enterprise-wide competitive advantage.

**Two: Are you measuring AI by business outcomes or by deployment activity?** Licenses activated, tools deployed, and training hours completed are not ROI. Revenue growth, margin improvement, and cycle time reduction are. If your AI program cannot point to specific financial results in specific business units, you are measuring the wrong things — and optimizing toward the wrong outcomes.

**Three: Have you redesigned any workflows — or only added AI tools to existing ones?** The research is unambiguous across every major study published in 2026: bolting AI onto inefficient processes amplifies the inefficiency. The organizations generating six times the revenue growth of their peers are not using better AI models. They are using AI inside fundamentally redesigned ways of working.

The AI divide is no longer something to prepare for. It is the present competitive reality of March 2026. The question every executive needs to answer is not whether to invest in AI — that decision is already made industry-wide. The question is whether your investment is building the strategic architecture that produces compounding returns, or funding activity that keeps you busy while the leaders extend their lead.

The window is still open. But every month it gets harder to see from the other side.
