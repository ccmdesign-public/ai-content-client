# Vibe-coding has a hidden filter and it is not in how well you prompt

#### **Why some AI-built apps work on the first try**

![Image by author](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/0*NQjRtuzXAXrBLYQ6.png)

The barrier to building software just collapsed. Anyone with an idea and a browser tab can have a working app by end of day. No code, no developer, no budget. The tools are fast, the outputs are real, and the hype is entirely justified.

Here’s what nobody’s talking about: the collapse of one barrier made another one visible.

I’ve been watching designers and founders pour into Lovable, Bolt, v0 — describe a product, watch something appear, feel that particular electricity of seeing a thing exist that didn’t exist an hour ago. And I’ve noticed a pattern. Some sessions produce something genuinely useful on the first pass. Others produce a beautiful, functional, completely wrong product that requires hours of patching before it resembles the original idea. The tool is identical in both cases. The difference is what the person brought to the session before they opened it.

### The prompt is a compressed product brief

Coding agents are not collaborators. A good developer pushes back when the requirements are vague. A designer asks what problem we’re actually solving. The agent does neither. It takes your words and builds their most literal interpretation, immediately, without asking whether you’ve thought it through.

This is diagnostic in a way that design tools never were.

Figma is tolerant of incomplete thinking. You can design a confirmation screen before you’ve decided what triggers it. You can wireframe an empty state before you know what fills it. The tool lets you defer the hard decisions and because the deliverable looks complete, it’s easy to believe the thinking is too.

A coding agent ships the gaps.

When you describe a product vaguely — “a fitness tracker with meal plans and workout suggestions” — the agent produces something. It has screens, flows, a home dashboard. It looks right. What’s wrong is invisible: the logic underneath it, which was never in your prompt because you hadn’t worked it out. You didn’t specify whether meal plans adapt to dietary restrictions. You didn’t define what “workout suggestions” means: static library, daily push, personalized to fitness level. The agent made those calls. The product it built reflects its inferences, not your intentions.

And because something working now exists, the gap between what you wanted and what appeared feels like a bug rather than a design decision you never made. You patch it. The patch surfaces the next unresolved decision. A few rounds in and you’ve spent more time debugging your own thinking than building anything.

The tool was fast the whole time. The bottleneck was upstream.

### What actually determines whether a session goes clean

This is where the conversation about vibe-coding tends to go wrong. The dominant advice is about prompting: write clearer prompts, be more specific, break it into steps. That’s not wrong, but it treats the symptom. A clearer prompt is downstream of clearer thinking. You can’t write a precise prompt about something you don’t understand precisely.

What determines whether a session goes clean is what you knew about the problem before you sat down.

I built a fitness app on March 8th, the day Lovable went free for 24 hours as part of #SheBuilds, their International Women’s Day initiative, run in partnership with Anthropic. The premise of the app came from years of working through the actual science: how nutrition and recovery interact, what the research says about sustainable habit formation versus what the fitness industry packages and sells, where consistency actually breaks down for real people with real schedules. I knew what the product should and shouldn’t try to do. I knew which assumptions it was making and which of them held up. The app shipped cleanly in a single session, with almost no rework.

Not because I wrote a good prompt. Because I understood the problem well enough that the prompt almost wrote itself.

That’s the thing the tools are exposing: domain knowledge was always the foundation of good product work. It’s just that in the old model, there was enough process between insight and output , enough handoffs, reviews, iterations that shallow understanding could survive the journey. The gap between knowing the problem and not knowing it was buffered by time.

Vibe-coding removes the buffer. The session starts and the agent wants to know what you’re building, right now, in enough detail to build it. If the detail isn’t there, the product won’t be either. No process will save you from that.

### Anticipatory design is preparation, not prediction

The concept of anticipatory design usually describes something the product does for the user — surfacing what they need before they ask for it. Google autofill. Spotify’s Discover Weekly. The interface that thinks ahead.

What I mean by it now is something the designer does for the product. Thinking ahead of the agent. Answering, before the session starts, the questions the agent will otherwise answer for you.

What exactly is this product doing for the user? Not at the level of features, but at the level of behavior? What does the user do differently after using it? What’s the friction it removes, specifically, for a specific person in a specific situation?

And the question most designers skip: what are the business assumptions underneath this, and which of them could be wrong? An app can have beautiful onboarding and still be built on a premise that doesn’t hold. A feature that looks considered in Figma can still be solving for the wrong problem. Product logic errors are harder to catch than UX errors because they don’t look like errors, they look like foundations.

Running that stress-test before touching the tool is what I mean by anticipatory design at the process level. The designer who does it arrives at the session with something the agent genuinely cannot generate: a clear, pre-tested understanding of what the product is and why it works. Everything after that is execution. Execution is what the agent is fast at.

When execution becomes cheap, the scarce resource shifts. It moves to the layer the agent can’t touch — the domain knowledge, the user insight, the product clarity that has to exist in your head before the prompt is written. Those things take time to build. They compound with experience. They don’t transfer from tool to tool.

This is what makes the current moment interesting. Vibe-coding didn’t level the playing field. It changed which part of the field matters. The designers and founders who move fast and ship coherent products with agents aren’t doing it because they’re better at prompting. They’re doing it because they know something specific about a real problem. The tool just gave them a shorter path from knowing to building.

The agents who build the right things quickly aren’t the ones with the cleverest prompts. They’re the ones doing the least guessing.
