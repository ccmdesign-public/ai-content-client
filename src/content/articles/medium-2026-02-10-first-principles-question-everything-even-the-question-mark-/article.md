In this section I am going to go to the basics of asking questions and the way to question everything to identify the fundamental truth to rebuild solutions from scratch if we are asked to do so.

### Definition

First Principle analysis takes a complex problem and break it down into basic elements ie. the fundamental truth to the core beginning of the evolution to rebuild solutions that are innovative, completely ignoring the fact how it was built before, assumptions are omitted, all analogies are wiped from the past conventions.

The key questions include identifying objective facts, questioning the underlying assumptions and deconstructing to core components.

-   What do I know for sure?
-   Why do I believe this is true?
-   What are the essential building blocks?

![]()

The phases of the first principle analysis for problem solving are

### Identifying and defining the core problem

When you start with an issue or a problem, do not just look for solving the problem that is surfacing at the top. Always look for the cause which is the reason for the current problem and try to get to the root of the issue. Dig deeper and understand how to get to root cause.

Following questions should be asked when you are presented with the problem to know more about its fundamental reason for the failure.

-   What is the core problem to start with or trying to solve?
-   What is the root cause of the issue instead of symptoms?
-   What is the true goal behind the initiative?

> **Goal: Build a Rocket**

![Photo by Terence Burke on Unsplash]()

### Deconstructing the existing beliefs

Once you have the root cause of the problem try to find the assumptions which are believed to be true for you. Question why its true and get to the source of its correctness. Assumptions of those reasoning should be validated.

Question yourself on why the assumptions are correct? will it be true always? what if it is not true? Reason out for all the questions around those assumptions. Validate why the current solution is designed that way and what if the belief that was used to build the solution is outdated. Question your perspectives on the problem and try to deconstruct all the components to its source of truth. Some of the questions that helps in this phase are

-   What are the assumptions?
-   Which of those assumptions are true?
-   What is the source for correctness of those assumptions?
-   What if my assumptions are opposite?
-   Why is the conventional knowledge driving the way it is?
-   Why its done this way?
-   Are the beliefs outdated and requires a new perspective?

> **Assumptions: Rockets are expensive**

![Photo by Alexander Grey on Unsplash]()

### Breaking down to fundamental truth

Once the assumptions are validated its time for you to break down the elements and components that was used to construct the core truth. Identify the constraint in which the solution was built. Gather all the information needed to construct from the ground up. Back track and reverse engineer to identify the individual core blocks of the system.

-   What are the foundational elements of the problem?
-   What are the physical or mathematical constraints involved?
-   What are the raw, unchangeable components required to rebuild?

> **Fundamental Truth: What are rockets made of? Aluminium, Titanium, Copper and Carbon Fiber**

![Photo by Zoshua Colah on Unsplash]()

### Rebuilding and Reimagining

-   If I have to rebuild with no legacy constraints how would it look like?
-   How to achieve the same goal at a fraction of the cost or time?
-   What is the simples possible solution that solves the root problem?
-   What are the implications and consequences if the original assumptions were wrong?
-   What if goes wrong and what are the alternatives?

> **Reconstruction: Value of the raw material is 2% of the typical cost**
