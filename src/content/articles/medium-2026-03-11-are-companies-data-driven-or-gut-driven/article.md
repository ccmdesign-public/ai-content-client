# Are Companies Data-Driven or Gut-Driven?

# Are Companies Data-Driven or Gut-Driven?

[Tirtha Tanna](/@tirthatanna?source=post_page---byline--69ca899455d0---------------------------------------)

3 min read·5 days ago

\--

The term "data-driven" used to mean something. Now it’s thrown around everywhere (just like AI). Every other startup’s strategy deck has that one slide with a 30-point italicized font floating in front of a random upward-trending green-colored line chart, declaring that the organization is

> “Leveraging data to drive decisions”

![Photo by Markus Winkler on Unsplash]()

If you actually look at how they operate, the sales data sits in five different spreadsheets. Marketing tracks campaign performance in one tool, finance tracks revenue somewhere else, and operations has its own version of “the numbers.” Half the time nobody agrees which dataset is correct. The other half of the time someone is manually copying numbers into Excel right before a meeting.

And when data finally does appear in discussions, it’s often the pieces that support the narrative people already believe.

Yet the homepage still says *data-driven*.

You might assume these companies are deliberately maintaining a facade. Playing the data-driven game for investors and customers while running on hunches behind closed doors. That would almost be better. At least then there would be awareness of the gap.

But instead, the fact that they cherry-pick which data to believe and override it whenever it’s inconvenient doesn’t register as a problem.

How do you fix a problem an organization can’t even see exists?

In an ideal world, data-driven means making decisions based on evidence. But we don’t live in that world, and the instinct of upper management often feels more reliable than the data that is recorded based on actual sales to customers.

### Why does this happen?

People trust stories more than spreadsheets. Senior leaders rely on experience and instinct because that approach worked for them before. When the numbers disagree with their judgment, the instinct feels safer, and so they rely upon that.

There’s also a deeper psychological factor at play. Humans naturally look for evidence that confirms existing beliefs. Data analysis can very easily turn into a search for validation rather than a search for truth.

If the question is framed as “show me that this strategy works,” the analysis will probably find a way to support it.

### It’s getting worse

Recently, the situation has become even easier to hide.

You know what’s great about large language models? They can take almost any dataset and spin a narrative around it. The AI doesn’t know if the insight is meaningful. It just knows how to make correlations sound important. And now companies can generate endless data-flavored content that looks sophisticated while remaining completely disconnected from how decisions are actually made.

We have better visualization tools than ever. I can build a dashboard in an afternoon that looks incredibly data-driven. Whether anyone actually changes their behavior based on what it shows is a completely different question.

### What do we do?

Most organizations just want to appear to be data-driven. They don’t want the discomfort of having their assumptions challenged or the uncertainty of admitting they don’t know something. And until that changes, until there’s genuine accountability for decision-making processes and not just decision-making outcomes, we’re going to keep seeing this gap. Companies will keep investing in data infrastructure while ignoring what it tells them.

I don’t have a clean solution to this. I’m not going to wrap this up with “five steps to become truly data-driven,” because I don’t think most organizations are willing to take those steps even if they knew what they were.

What I do know is that pretending we’re data-driven when we’re not is worse than admitting we make decisions based on intuition, politics, and whoever argues most convincingly in meetings. At least then we’d be honest about it.

> The data is there. It’s just not driving anything.
