# What it’s Actually Like to Work in Data

[Tirtha Tanna](/@tirthatanna?source=post_page---byline--1b8a4be7b168---------------------------------------)

4 min read·Jan 10, 2026

\--

Working in data is often portrayed as writing complex SQL, building beautiful dashboards, and casually sharing insights that make the business millions by lunchtime. If only.

The actual day-to-day is a lot messier and slower. Data analysts live in the space between questions that are poorly formed and answers that are rarely final. And most days, SQL is just one small piece of the puzzle.

![Image by Author]()

### The Day Rarely Starts With Data

It usually starts with a question. Or something pretending to be a question.

“Can you pull some numbers on this?”
“Something feels off with this metric.”
“We just want to understand what’s happening.”

Without a precise definition of success and no agreement on what “this” even refers to.

A significant chunk of data work is translating vague requests into something measurable. Before querying anything, you’re clarifying intent, assumptions, constraints, and the unspoken reason someone is asking in the first place. The technical work comes a lot later.

### SQL is a Tool

Yes, SQL matters. You will use it often. But writing queries is rarely the hard part.

The real challenge is deciding:

-   Which tables actually represent the ask?
-   Whether the data reflects the process as it exists today or as it existed three migrations ago.
-   If the metric means the same thing across teams, regions, or time.

Most queries are not complex because of syntax. They are complex because the business logic is fuzzy, contradictory, or evolving in real time.

You spend more time thinking about that than actually typing. And a surprising amount of time staring at results, wondering whether they’re wrong, misleading, or technically correct but contextually useless.

### Dashboards don’t Answer Questions on their Own

Dashboards are often treated as the end product, but they’re closer to conversation starters.

-   A chart doesn’t explain why something happened.
-   A KPI doesn’t tell you what to do next.
-   A perfectly designed visual can still be ignored if it doesn’t align with how people make decisions.

A large part of the job is deciding what not to show, simplifying without distorting, and resisting the urge to add just one more filter or breakdown.

### Most Insights come from Context, not just Data

Data rarely speaks for itself.

To make sense of it, you need:

-   An understanding of how the business actually operates, not how it’s documented.
-   Knowledge of incentives, deadlines, and internal politics.
-   Awareness of what changed recently, even if it never made it into a changelog.

Two identical graphs can tell completely different stories depending on timing, audience, and prior decisions. This is why good data work feels closer to an investigation.

### Stakeholder Management

No one advertises this part, but it’s unavoidable.

-   You negotiate scope.
-   You push back on bad metrics.
-   You explain why a number changed even though “nothing changed.”
-   You repeat the same concept in five different ways because each team hears differently.

The job is also about helping others trust the numbers, understand them, and use them responsibly, not just create a bar chart.

### Documentation

No one dreams of writing metric definitions or documenting edge cases. Yet somehow, analysts will probably fall apart without it.

A lot of data problems are caused by missing context.

-   What does this metric actually mean?
-   When did the logic change?
-   Why does this number not match the one in that other dashboard?
-   Is this table still reliable or just technically alive?

Good documentation answers these questions before a new analyst has to ask 10 different people the exact same question and still not get an answer.

So what is the job, really?

Working in data is not constantly writing SQL or building endless dashboards or finding groundbreaking insights every day.

-   It’s structured thinking applied to messy reality.
-   It’s asking better questions when the first ones are wrong.
-   It’s knowing when data helps and when it creates false confidence.

Some days you build. Some days you debug. Some days you explain the same thing again, slightly differently, to a new audience.

And occasionally, everything lines up.

That’s the job.
