## Overview

For years, the “executive dashboard” has been the holy grail of corporate management. The promise is seductive, a single screen of gleaming charts and real-time gauges that allows you to “steer” your business like a pilot in a cockpit.

But as organizations become more data-literate, a harsh reality is emerging. For many teams, dashboards have become a source of confusion rather than clarity. Here is why you might want to stop building dashboards and start focusing on computational documents.

## The “So What?” Problem

Dashboards are excellent at showing what is happening, but they are notoriously bad at explaining why. You see a line chart trending downward (*revenue is dropping).* Without context, dashboards lead to “data theater” (*teams sitting in a room looking at numbers without any clear path to action)*.

In contrast, a narrative-first report forces the author to bridge the gap between a data point and a business decision.

## Information Overload vs. Focused Insights

There is a psychological phenomenon where, once we have a blank canvas, we feel compelled to fill it. This leads to cluttered dashboards packed with “vanity metrics” and real-time data for processes that only change month-to-month.

By moving away from the “single pane of glass,” you shift from passive monitoring to active investigation.

## The Illusion of Control

A dashboard provides a snapshot in time, often flattening complex data into simple averages. This can hide significant outliers. A single average metric can look healthy while a specific segment of your customer base is experiencing a total service failure.

## The Superior Alternative: Modern Reporting

If you ditch the traditional dashboard, how do you maintain visibility? The industry is moving toward reproducible, high-fidelity reports that combine live code, beautiful typography, and deep narrative.

## The New Standard for Web Reports

Instead of a static BI dashboard, many high-performing data teams are moving to Quarto. Quarto is an open-source technical publishing system that allows you to weave together narrative text and code (Python, R, or Julia) to produce elegant web reports.

### **Interactivity with Purpose**

Unlike a dashboard where everything is interactive (often pointlessly), a Quarto web report allows you to embed specific interactive elements — like an Observable JS plot — exactly where they support the story.

### **Version Control**

Because Quarto files are plain text, they live in Git. You can see exactly how a calculation changed over time, something nearly impossible in a drag-and-drop BI tool.

### **Self-Contained**

A Quarto HTML report can be emailed as a single file that contains all the data and charts, ensuring the recipient sees exactly what you intended.

## Typst: Precision PDF Reporting

When the stakes are high — such as board meetings or annual reviews — the “screenshot of a dashboard” look is unprofessional. This is where Typst comes in. Typst is a new, lightning-fast markup-based typesetting system designed to be an alternative to LaTeX.

### **Design-First Analytics**

Typst allows you to generate PDFs that look like high-end magazines rather than clinical data dumps.

### **Automation**

You can feed data into a Typst template to automatically generate 50-page monthly performance reviews that are perfectly formatted, paginated, and ready for print.

### **Speed**

Unlike LaTeX, which can be slow and cumbersome, Typst renders instantly, allowing for a tight feedback loop between data analysis and final presentation.

## Conclusions

Dashboards often act as a decorative layer over a lack of strategy. If your team finds themselves squinting at the same charts every Monday without changing their behavior, it’s time to retire the dashboard.

By adopting tools like Quarto for web-based storytelling or Typst for high-fidelity documentation, you ensure that your data doesn’t just sit on a screen — it actually gets read, understood, and acted upon.
