# **Stop Making Reports No One Reads: How to Build Data Decks That Drive Action**

If you are anything like me, you have probably lost count of the reports, readouts, deep-dives, scorecards, and QBRs you’ve created during your data analyst career. And how many of those were forgotten and never acted on?

I bet there are many.

A deck is often the final output of the analysis and the result of weeks of hard work. Yet, we, as data analysts, don’t spend much time learning how to do it effectively, and as a result, our recommendations never make it off the slide.

> “Why bother if we can use AI to create a deck for us?” you might ask.

It’s a fair question.

While AI can definitely be a powerful drafting partner, you are the one with a human perspective. Moreover, the ability to present insights clearly and create decks that advance your ideas and recommendations can set you apart professionally and help you avoid being replaced by AI.

I created this guide based on 13 years of experience working with data and presenting the insights, as well as multiple books I’ve read and courses I’ve taken throughout my career.

> **Disclaimer:** There are multiple deck formats, and the approach will differ depending on whether you are working on a deck to present the results of deep-dive analysis, a templatized QBR (Quarterly Business Review), an executive scorecard, or a regular performance report. For this article, I focus particularly on the first: creating a deck to present deep-dive analysis insights.

So, what are we waiting for — let’s dig in:

## **Where to start?**

You’re done with the analysis. Weeks of hard work are behind you. And now you are staring at an empty PowerPoint deck, not knowing how to fit weeks’ worth of data analysis into just a few slides.

It feels impossible, but it’s not — here’s how it’s done:

## **Structure of the Deck**

Through a lot of trial and error over the years, I’ve come up with a template I usually follow — and it goes like this:

-   The deck title, author, and date
-   Purpose of the analysis
-   Data sources, timeline, scope, methodology, data limitations, definitions *(you can either put them on one slide or have multiple)*
-   Executive Summary
-   The slides to present the insights
-   Recommendations

![]()

I always tailor my content to my audience — i.e., what they already know about the topic, the available time, and whether they’ll be reading or watching — but the structure usually stays unchanged. What also stays the same is that I strive to make the deck self-explanatory, even if I am not in the room to narrate and present.

> **Anyone who gets access to the deck afterward will need to be able to get to the main point without needing to watch a recording.**

**The first 3 slides** (deck title, goal of the analysis, data sources, timeline, scope, methodology, and definitions) are there to set the context and answer all the initial data foundation questions. If you have been in a data analyst role for a while, you know how important the context is and how drastically the insights can change depending on the data sources you use or the timeframes you include.

> And there will always be someone questioning your data, especially if your conclusions “challenge the status quo” or question someone else’s work effectiveness.

Thus, I never omit these slides, even though I rarely present them.

**The Executive Summary** is a must-have slide for everyone who is “too busy” to go through the entire deck. Along with your Recommendations, these are the two most vital slides in your deck.

Even if you have only two minutes of stakeholder attention, these two slides will get your message across and ensure your work makes an impact. If you must cut everything else, these 2 slides must stay.

I recommend spending as much time on them as on all the other slides covering the insights. I place the Executive Summary slide at the beginning of the deck, but I always create it last. This way, I am sure nothing is missed.

## **The Slides to Present the Insights**

Data storytelling books usually recommend outlining the story structure before creating slides. While I do understand the point (you don’t want to waste your time creating slides you might not need), I approach this a little differently. I’ll normally put together a rough, messy draft first without worrying too much about the narrative or overwhelming the audience with too many metrics and then build the story and decide what stays and what goes to the appendix.

![]()

When organizing slides, I usually follow **a vertical flow:** all the content on the slide should relate to its title.

Slide structure varies, but usually, there is

-   Headline
-   Sub-headline
-   Visual(s) or Table

Let’s start with the slide headline: **a*n audience* should be able to get the message you are trying to convey just from reading the headline.**

> *If you read only the slide titles, would the story still make sense?*

I avoid generic headlines like “Marketing Sourced Win Rate Trendline.” Instead, I use the headlines that explain exactly what happened: “*Q2 Marketing Sourced Win Rate dropped to 12.6% with big deals pushed to next quarter.”*

Usually, I also add a sub-headline — 1–2 lines of text that provide extra details or supplementary context not in the data. For example, *“20 large deals (about $4.5M total) slipped to next quarter. Buyers requested a short pilot to test integrations.”*

Last but not least, I never include multiple messages on the same slide. *(Do not confuse it with multiple charts per slide. It is totally fine to have more than 1 visualization on the slide if visuals are connected and help you communicate the main message).*

Each slide needs to transition smoothly into the next one (**a horizontal flow**). Like classical storytelling in books and movies, when creating a deck, I follow a narrative arc:

1.  First, reveal the facts;
2.  In the middle, “investigate” the barriers;
3.  At the end, propose a way forward.

![]()

Familiarity with the approach makes it intuitive for the audience to follow.

## **And Now the Visuals**

Always keep in mind that the chart that helped you discover the insight and the chart you include in the deck are two different charts. For example, the discovery chart might show 12 channels plotted in multiple colors, with overlapping lines. It helps you identify trends and spot the main drivers. But if left unchanged on the slide, it will overwhelm the audience. The presentation chart should focus on the main drivers only, with the rest greyed out or removed.

![]()

### **Use Color Effectively**

-   To effectively guide your audience’s focus and highlight the key message, use color, contrast, and line thickness.
-   Another point about color is consistency. If you’ve used green color for the Paid Search channel on one slide, keep using the same green color in all the charts with Paid Search.
-   A colorblind-friendly palette is usually a safe choice.

![]()

### **Focus on Readability**

-   Increase the gap between bars to improve readability.
-   Remove clutter and distractions from your visualizations (gridlines, redundant axes, unnecessary markers, data labels).
-   Replace crowded small intervals with broader timeframes to highlight big-picture trends.
-   Improve the x-axis readability by separating the year and quarter into distinct levels.
-   Do not rotate the labels.
-   Use visual hierarchy, sort in descending order.

![]()

### **Aim to Decrease Cognitive Load**

1.  Do math to reduce cognitive load for the audience.
2.  Replace busy dual-axis chart with 2 separate ones as the different units of measures and scales can make it tricky to identify meaningful trends.
3.  Label the lines directly in the chart, so the audience won’t need to check the legend back and forth.
4.  Format large numbers for readability (for example, 100K instead of 100,000).
5.  When using a table, consider grouping related rows and columns, or applying background colors for a quicker review.

## **Recommendations**

When I build a recommendation slide, I usually follow the **“What — So what — Now what”** structure.

-   “**What”** is the insight I’ve discovered.
-   “**So what”** is the reason why it is important.
-   **“Now what”** is what we should do about it. Actionable recommendation (ideally time-bound, connected to revenue, with team/person responsible indicated).

### Example:

**What:** Q2 Marketing Sourced Win Rate dropped to 12.6%, with 20 large deals (about $4.5M total) pushed to next quarter after buyers asked for pilot integrations.

**So what:** This dip in win rate and the delay in closing puts our quarterly revenue target at risk and hints at friction late in our sales process.

**Now what:** Collaborate with sales, solutions, and other teams to speed up and simplify pilots as well as create fast-start onboarding materials. The goal is to shorten deal cycles and win back those deals that have slipped into Q3.

## **Appendix**

Once you deep-dive into the analysis, you discover many findings that are relevant to a story but are not necessarily worth sharing during a presentation, or their impact is minimal. This is what the Appendix is for. And the slides here are not buried; quite the opposite.

You don’t know how many times the slides in the Appendix saved me when answering the audience’s questions.

> Trust me, there is always someone in the audience who wants to know more, dig deeper, or ask ‘have you checked…’ types of questions.

## **Final Thoughts**

So, you now know how to put together a well-organized and impactful deck.

**The last thing I want to mention** is that you also need to practice the deck delivery. You can build the most amazing deck ever, but if your speech is monotonous, has too many fillers (*um/uh/like*), if you are speaking too fast or too slow, if you are mumbling or just reading the text from a slide, who can blame people for not listening to you?

Practice-practice-practice!
