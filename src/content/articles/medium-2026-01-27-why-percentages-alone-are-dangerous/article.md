# Why Percentages Alone are Dangerous

# **Why Percentages Alone are Dangerous**

[Tirtha Tanna](/@tirthatanna?source=post_page---byline--1216e80c7522---------------------------------------)

3 min read·Jan 22, 2026

\--

1

Have you ever picked up a product in the supermarket because you saw something like:

> “40% fewer pesticides”

Impressive. You might even feel

like you’re making the right choices, treating your body like a temple. And honestly, a few years ago, I probably would have dropped it into my basket without a second thought.

But thanks to Daniel Kahneman and my love for statistics, I now know that a metric without context cannot be trusted.

![Image by Author]()

Because a percentage change without context can be deceptive.

If that product originally contained 0.00005% pesticides, a 40% reduction turns it into 0.00002%. It’s technically lower and mathematically correct, but practically, it’s almost irrelevant.

Our brains react far more strongly to “40% fewer” than to a string of zeros they don’t want to process. And unless you’re a statistician, your brain is not used to thinking like one.

This is called Base Rate Neglect.

Base rate neglect happens when we focus on a standout number, a percentage change, or a recent data point, while ignoring the baseline it came from.

Our brains love relative differences because they’re easy to grasp. Absolute values require more effort and thinking, which our brains tend to avoid.

Let’s take another example.

There’s a customer complaints dashboard that tracks percentage change. One month, customer complaints spike by 50%. The support team and the product team are suddenly expected to explain what went wrong. At first glance, it looks like something serious happened, and major changes might be needed.

Then you look at the absolute numbers.

Last month, there were 4 complaints. This month, there are 6.

The percentage change looks extreme because the starting point was tiny. And when you consider that the total customer base is around 3,000, the situation suddenly feels a lot less alarming.

Let’s take a real-world example.

You see news about a plane crash, and you decide to drive over the holidays instead of flying. Statistically, the chances of dying in a plane crash are far lower than in a car accident. But your brain focuses on the plane crash that’s on every news channel, rather than the probability data that rarely gets airtime.

This doesn’t just happen in everyday life. It happens at work too.

### Why do our brains fall for it?

Our brains are wired to prioritize vivid, emotional information over probabilistic data. A dramatic plane crash sticks in your mind. A statistical table doesn’t.

Have you ever seen someone pull up a study on the probability of dying in a car accident before going on a road trip? Probably not. Our brains rely on shortcuts so that every decision doesn’t take forever.

### How to be more wary of it

If you see a percentage on a billboard or a product label, you can probably ignore it. But when a decision has a real impact, especially at work, checking the baseline is not optional.

Before reacting upon seeing a huge jump in a weekly dashboard update, ask:

-   What does this usually look like?
-   Is this within normal variation?
-   How big is this in absolute terms?
-   What story does the long-term trend tell?

Adding this perspective into your process matters because you start developing the habit of zooming out before zooming in.

But that doesn’t mean you should stop using percentages. They’re a great way to show relative change or movement over time, especially at work. But when they risk misleading the audience, providing context upfront can prevent confusion and overreaction.
