# **Let’s Build Shazam From Scratch**

## How pure math beats AI in finding that one song in 3 seconds.

[Keerthana Nyshadham](/@keerthana-n?source=post_page---byline--a8c7da65741f---------------------------------------)

7 min read·22 hours ago

\--

We all know that agonizing feeling. A catchy song plays in a cafe or store, gets stuck in our head and we have absolutely no idea what it is.

We try humming it to our friends. That usually ends in awkward silence and some rlly good inside jokes. But still dk the song! Then we try some tools to help us find the song. Say like Shazam. Tap a button, hum the song and it knows the exact song in three seconds!!

I honestly can’t stop thinking about how it actually works. As an AI engineer, my brain immediately went to complex neural networks or deep learning models. Spoiler alert: Shazam does NOT use machine learning at all.

It relies entirely on pure, beautiful mathematical signal processing invented by Avery Wang back in 2003. It works in noisy environments, ignores people talking over the music, and searches a database of over 100 million songs instantly.

## **Step 1: Sound to Spectrogram (The Setup)**

Raw audio is just a messy wave of air pressure that changes over time. If you look at a raw audio file on your computer, it just looks like a chaotic scribble. We cannot match songs using this raw wave because background noise or volume changes will completely warp the shape. We need to split this chaotic wave into distinct musical notes over time.

### **Deep Dive**

To understand the frequencies present in the audio, we convert the signal from the time domain into the frequency domain. We downsample the audio to 8192 Hz. The vast majority of identifying musical frequencies fall below 4096 Hz, which satisfies the **Nyquist theorem** for this sampling rate.

We then apply the **Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)** over sliding overlapping chunks of the audio. This creates a 3D graph called a **spectrogram**. The x-axis is time, the y-axis is frequency, and the color intensity represents the amplitude of that specific frequency.

> Here is the discrete math that makes this transformation possible:
> 
> X\[k\] = Σ x\[n\] × e^(-i2πkn/N)

## **Step 2: Constellation Maps (Finding the Stars)**

Storing the entire spectrogram for millions of songs for matching is computationally impossible. It is too heavy. Instead we just want to find the loudest and most unique parts of the song. We want to filter out the background chatter entirely.

### **Deep Dive**

The algorithm applies a **2D max-filter** to the spectrogram. It searches for **local maxima**. These are specific time and frequency coordinates where the energy is higher than all immediate surrounding neighbors in a defined grid block.

By doing this, we isolate the dominant tones of the music and completely discard the volume data. Background noise is naturally filtered out here because ambient chatter rarely creates sharp high-energy frequency peaks. The resulting output is a sparse 2D scatter plot of time and frequency coordinates. Cuz it looks like a star field, it is called a **Constellation Map**.

> The math to determine if a point is a peak looks like this:
> 
> P(t, ω) = 1 if |STFT(t, ω)|² > |STFT(t±Δt, ω±Δω)|² else 0

## **Step 3: Combinatorial Hashing (Main trick)**

A single note tells us nothing. Thousands of songs might have the exact same note at the exact same time. But pairing two notes together, along with the exact time delay between them, creates a highly unique fingerprint.

### **Deep Dive**

Now, THIS is the absolute genius part of the architecture. Instead of looking at individual peaks, the algorithm chooses an anchor point and looks ahead into a restricted target zone of time.

It pairs the anchor peak with target peaks inside this zone. This creates a **3-tuple hash** composed of the *frequency of the anchor (f₁)*, the *frequency of the target (f₂)*, and the *time difference between them (Δt)*.

Because there are only a few specific frequencies and short time jumps, this tuple packs perfectly into a 32-bit unsigned integer. This acts as our dictionary key.

> The hash generation formula uses bitwise shifts to pack the data:
> 
> Hash = (f₁ ≪ 20) | (f₂ ≪ 8) | (Δt & 0xFF)

## **Step 4: Time Alignment (Matching)**

When we record a 10-second sample in a cafe, our phone generates these fingerprints and asks the database if it has seen them. With 100 million songs, we will inevitably get random false-positive matches. How do we mathematically prove it is the right song? We check the rhythm of the matches.

### **Deep Dive**

We execute **Time Alignment scoring**. Every hash in the database is linked to the absolute time it originally occurred in the song (t\_database). Our recorded sample also notes when the hash occurred relative to the start of our recording (t\_sample).

> For every matched hash, we calculate the time offset:
> 
> Offset = t\_database − t\_sample

If our recording perfectly matches the chorus of a track starting exactly 60 seconds into the song, every single correct hash pair will yield an identical offset of exactly 60 seconds. We plot these offsets on a histogram. Random matches will scatter randomly across the chart. The true song will form a massive statistically undeniable spike in a single histogram bin. The song with the tallest spike 📈 wins🥇!!

## **Edge Cases and Classical Solutions**

Building this at scale comes with massive edge cases. Here is how the classic architecture solved them:

**1\. Extreme Background Noise:** If you are in a loud concert, the audio is terribly distorted. The solution is massive hashing redundancy. A 15-second audio clip generates thousands of hashes. Even if 98% of your hashes are completely destroyed by background noise, the surviving 2% of matches are still statistically significant enough to form a winning histogram spike.

**2\. Playback Speed Variations:** What if a DJ plays a track 5% faster? The absolute frequencies and time delays will no longer match the database exactly. To solve this, the system can extract invariant components. Instead of hashing absolute frequencies, the algorithm hashes the ratio of frequencies (f2 / f1) and the ratio of time differences. These ratios remain mathematically constant even if the entire song is stretched or shrunk.

## **The Modern ML Evolution**

The classic math approach is brilliant. But modern music recognition systems (like Google’s Now Playing) get weirdly excited about Deep Learning to solve even more complex distortion environments.

**Surface Level Idea:** Instead of manually defining what a peak or a hash is, we feed thousands of hours of clean and noisy audio into an Artificial Intelligence model. We force the AI to learn how to compress a messy audio clip into a tiny numerical fingerprint that ignores background noise entirely.

**Deep Dive:** Modern systems use **Contrastive Learning**. They take a spectrogram and pass it through a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) like ResNet or a Transformer architecture like BEATs.

The model maps the audio into a dense 128-dimensional embedding vector. During training, we give the model a clean song and a heavily distorted version of that same song. We use a contrastive loss function to pull the embeddings of the clean and noisy versions closer together in vector space, while pushing embeddings of different songs far apart.

> The contrastive loss math looks like this:
> 
> ℒ = −log( exp(sim(zᵢ, zⱼ) / T) / Σ exp(sim(zᵢ, zₖ) / T) )

Once the vectors are generated, systems use Product Quantization to compress the database and Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) search to find the closest matching vector in milliseconds. This allows AI models to vastly outperform classic math in environments with heavy room reverberation 🌌or extreme noise.

## **TL;DR Tech Stack**

-   **Classic Signal Processing:** Fast Fourier Transform downsampled to 8192 Hz.
-   **Classic Indexing:** Combinatorial hashing packing 3-tuples into 32-bit integers.
-   **Classic Matching:** Histogram peak detection for time alignment scoring.
-   **Modern ML Stack:** CNNs or Audio Transformers trained with Contrastive Loss.
-   **Modern Retrieval:** 128-dimensional embeddings searched via Product Quantization (IVFPQ).
