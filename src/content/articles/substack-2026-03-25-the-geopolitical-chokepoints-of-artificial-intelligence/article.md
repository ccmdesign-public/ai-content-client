### ⭐ The Helium Shortage

> #### “The Iran War has created a choke point in the supply of helium, a byproduct of natural gas processing and LNG production that’s used in more than 20 steps of semiconductor fabrication….”

Helium, of all things? Helium’s unique physical properties make it irreplaceable for modern chip fabrication. Qatar is the source of [34% of global helium supply](https://pubs.usgs.gov/periodicals/mcs2026/mcs2026.pdf).

![Exponential View, read the note.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ME_S!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9b9f6b06-6598-4077-bee6-1cf5bab28721_2048x1103.png)

-   The Strait of Hormuz closure has blocked exports, leaving a significant portion of global supply stranded. South Korea is the most exposed, sourcing 64.7% of its helium from Qatar.
    

This could exasperate the HBM Bottlenecks that was already in place with rising prices. Samsung and SK Hynix produce over 60% of global memory and supply most HBM used in Nvidia’s AI GPUs.

-   HBM is a major bottleneck for AI GPUs. HBM is essentially a 3D skyscraper of DRAM dies. This creates a "manufacturing bottleneck" that slows down the entire AI supply chain.
    
-   When military strikes in the Iran War hit the [Ras Laffan Industrial City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ras_Laffan_Industrial_City) in Qatar. This single facility accounts for roughly **30% to 33% of the world's helium supply**. How geopolitics and the semiconductor industry intersect is obviously way more complicated than many of us realize.

### ⭐ DRAM Shortage - RAMageddon - related to our HBM Problem

So as of March 2026, the industry is experiencing what analysts are calling "RAMageddon"— a severe, structural shortage of both High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) and conventional DRAM.

What appears to be happening, and I’m not an expert here, is that unlike previous "boom-bust" cycles caused by temporary oversupply, this shortage is driven by a fundamental reallocation of global silicon wafer capacity toward AI. The real bottleneck for GPUs [is TSMC](https://x.com/dnystedt), but let’s not even go there today.

# **⭐ The Geopolitical Chokepoints of Artificial Intelligence**

By [Julian Alexander Brown](https://open.substack.com/users/13910911-julian-alexander-brown?utm_source=mentions), early 2026.

### **Why Chips, Power, and Geopolitics Now Decide Who Wins the AI Race**

![National AI capability index by country, decomposed across key inputs including computing power, data, algorithms, human capital, economic resources, regulatory environment, and model performance, illustrating the uneven distribution of AI capacity across the global system. — Source Harvard University, Belfer Center](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AkX4!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0737f4e6-0142-4b67-96b8-11b12971b40b_746x942.png)

**For much of the past decade, the rise of artificial intelligence appeared to be governed by inputs that [diffuse quickly](https://news.microsoft.com/source/emea/features/ai-diffusion-report-mapping-global-ai-adoption-and-innovation-2/).** Advances in architectures, algorithms, training techniques, and data practices spread rapidly through academic papers, open-source code, and global talent markets. Because these inputs were highly replicable and only weakly tied to geography, it was natural to assume that AI capability would spread broadly across countries and firms.

Comparison of AI diffusion to other earlier technologies. — Source: Microsoft AI Diffusion Report 2025.

## We Are Here

![Graph showing comparison of AI diffusion and other earlier technologies. Source: Microsoft AI Diffusion Report 2025.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-XBQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff4de4105-f405-4fae-884a-1a67e315bc83_1456x970.png)

That assumption no longer matches reality.

**Frontier AI today is increasingly constrained by [chokepoints](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/new-supply-chain-tech.html).** The binding constraints have shifted away from knowledge and technique toward physical and institutional capacity. At the center of this shift sit frontier AI data centers, where the largest models are trained and where the most demanding inference workloads are anchored. These facilities are where [electricity is converted into computation at scale](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-supply-for-ai), and their feasibility depends on assembling inputs that are slow to expand, expensive to replicate, and [unevenly distributed across the world.](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/07/ai-geopolitics-data-centres-technological-rivalry/)

In practice, frontier AI depends on the simultaneous availability of two limiting inputs: access to the most capable AI chips and access to large, [reliable supplies of electricity](https://www.csis.org/analysis/electricity-supply-bottleneck-us-ai-dominance). Software efficiency remains essential, but it now operates within these constraints rather than replacing them. Algorithms determine how efficiently compute is used, but the scale, cost, and location of frontier AI capability are set by silicon, power, [capital](https://epoch.ai/blog/how-much-does-it-cost-to-train-frontier-ai-models), and the institutions that govern them. [Empirical scaling laws](https://epoch.ai/blog/can-ai-scaling-continue-through-2030), energy economics, and [geopolitics](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/dispatches/eight-ways-ai-will-shape-geopolitics-in-2026/) together explain why the highest performance deployment of [AI is becoming geographically concentrated](https://www.cnas.org/publications/commentary/to-govern-ai-we-must-govern-compute) even as algorithmic innovation continues to diffuse globally.

### **⭐ Energy as the dominant scaling variable**

Once access to frontier AI chips exists, electricity becomes the principal variable that determines how large AI systems can grow. In 2024, data centers consumed roughly [415 terawatt hours](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-demand-from-ai) of electricity, about 1.5 percent of global electricity demand. Multiple projections suggest this could [more than double by 2030](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/ai-to-drive-165-increase-in-data-center-power-demand-by-2030) as AI workloads move from [intermittent bursts to sustained high utilization](https://datacentrereview.com/2025/11/global-data-centre-electricity-demand-set-to-double-by-2030/) on specialized hardware.

According to the **International Energy Agency (IEA)**, global data center electricity demand is projected to reach between **700 TWh and over 1,700 TWh by 2035**. Driven by AI, this doubling or tripling of consumption establishes data centers as a primary driver of future global energy demand. — [Credit to IEA](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-demand-from-ai)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qrJw!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40e97764-ef58-4a78-a2c4-39499c95b6e9_1456x959.jpeg)

At the frontier, this demand concentrates in a small number of extremely large, continuously operating facilities. Frontier AI training centers routinely draw between [100 and 500 megawatts](https://epoch.ai/blog/power-demands-of-frontier-ai-training) of power around the clock, placing them in the same category as [heavy industrial plants](https://www.iea.org/commentaries/what-the-data-centre-and-ai-boom-could-mean-for-the-energy-sector). A single 300-megawatt facility consumes roughly 2.6 terawatt hours per year, [comparable to the annual electricity use of a mid-size city](https://rpa.org/news/lab/the-rise-of-data-centers).

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_FXE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6b9e20bd-1679-4ae1-914f-5195f1abdd63_1060x1094.png)

Projected power growth of leading AI data centers, rising from near zero to gigawatt scale by 2026 and 2027 and approaching the electricity demand of major cities. — [Credit to Epoch AI](https://epoch.ai/data/data-centers).

Many AI data centers announced or under construction in 2025 and early 2026 are even larger, designed as multi-building campuses that begin in the hundreds of megawatts and scale toward [gigawatt-level power demand](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/ai-to-drive-165-increase-in-data-center-power-demand-by-2030) over time. Instead of expanding incrementally, these projects are planned from the outset to host [multiple frontier training clusters](https://epoch.ai/blog/power-demands-of-frontier-ai-training). This raises the baseline for what qualifies as [large-scale AI infrastructure](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-supply-for-ai) and pushes electricity from an operating expense into a system-level constraint.

Geographic footprint of major planned frontier AI data centers, illustrating the land area and scale of xAI Colossus, OpenAI Stargate, and Meta Hyperion projects relative to dense urban environments. — Source [Epoch AI](https://epoch.ai/data/data-centers).

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lxgQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb260ee6b-5b37-400e-aac0-282eb5ce3ae1_933x949.jpeg)

### Power delivery as a timing constraint

Falling energy prices alone do not remove this constraint. Solar power and battery storage have become much cheaper over the past decade, but lower prices do not make large amounts of electricity available faster. Frontier AI data centers require hundreds of megawatts of reliable power, delivered to specific sites on timelines set by [competitive model development](https://www.goldmansachs.com/pdfs/insights/goldman-sachs-research/data-center-power-demand-the-6-ps-driving-growth-and-constraints/redacted_report.pdf) rather than utility planning cycles. Meeting that demand depends on [permitting speed](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-future-of-data-centers/), [grid interconnection](https://www.carbon-direct.com/insights/ai-scale-and-climate-commitments-a-2026-outlook), transmission capacity, and the ability to [bring new generation online quickly](https://www.latitudemedia.com/news/the-grid-cant-build-fast-enough/). Only a small number of locations can do this at scale.

[As I have written about previously](https://substack.com/@julianalexanderbrown/p-175426511), under these near-term constraints, AI demand is pushing investment toward [modular generation and storage technologies](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/renewable-energy/renewable-energy-industry-outlook.html) such as utility-scale solar and batteries. These technologies dominate marginal capacity additions not because they fully solve reliability challenges, but because they can be [permitted and built far faster](https://www.energy-transitions.org/publications/planning-and-permitting/) than large thermal plants or long-lead grid expansions. At frontier scale, [delays matter more than electricity prices](https://www.guinnessgi.com/insights/ai-expansion-and-demand-new-datacentres). A year without power means a year without training runs, missed model iterations, and lost compounding advantage. Energy abundance sets the long-run ceiling of AI capability, but the speed and location of power delivery determine who reaches that ceiling first.

### Software efficiency, scaling laws, and why algorithmic efficiency is not a chokepoint

Algorithmic efficiency advancements have reduced the amount of compute required to reach a given level of performance by roughly an order of magnitude. OpenAI has estimated that [algorithmic progress alone](https://openai.com/index/ai-and-efficiency/) reduced the compute needed to achieve fixed language-model performance by about [tenfold between 2012 and 2022](https://openai.com/index/ai-and-efficiency/), independent of hardware improvements. [A 2024 study by Epoch AI](https://epoch.ai/blog/algorithmic-progress-in-language-models) found that for LLMs, the compute required to reach a performance threshold has actually [halved roughly every 8 months](https://epoch.ai/trends), significantly outpacing Moore’s Law. Comparable efficiency gains have appeared across vision, speech, and multimodal systems, where breakthroughs are mirroring the rapid progress seen in text-based models.

Training compute of notable machine learning models over time, illustrating the exponential growth in compute used at the AI frontier. — Source Epoch AI

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Vqcx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8bc18b17-cc19-42b2-af68-3eaed859f54e_1089x734.jpeg)

Crucially, these gains have occurred across the entire frontier rather than within any single firm or country. Nearly all leading AI labs have adopted similar techniques over the same period. [As these methods spread rapidly](https://aiindex.stanford.edu/report/) through academic publications, open-source frameworks, shared tooling, and global talent markets, software efficiency diffuses faster than any other input in the AI stack. It cannot be stockpiled, export controlled or reliably confined to a particular geography.

**For this reason, algorithmic efficiency is [not a geopolitical chokepoint](https://www.cnas.org/publications/reports/global-compute-and-national-security).** It does not determine who can build frontier AI systems. Instead, it determines how quickly the frontier advances for everyone.

### ⭐ Scaling laws and the limits of efficiency

Empirical scaling laws also explain why these efficiency gains do not level outcomes. Improvements in training methods reduce the compute required to reach a given level of performance, but as documented in the [Epoch AI Capabilities Index](https://epoch.ai/benchmarks/eci), **additional compute continues to produce meaningful gains at the frontier.** As a result, efficiency triggers a Jevons paradox: the phenomenon where increasing the efficiency of a resource leads to an increase, rather than a decrease, in its total consumption. [Recent industry analysis](https://www.bain.com/insights/how-can-we-meet-ais-insatiable-demand-for-compute-power-technology-report-2025/) and the [IMF](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/fandd/issues/2025/12/inside-the-ai-led-resource-race-thijs-van-de-graaf) notes that **lowering the price of intelligence through better algorithms makes it economically viable to scale even further,** effectively driving a “resource race” that far outpaces traditional efficiency gains.

Epoch Capabilities Index (ECI) scores of major AI models by release date, illustrating the pace and distribution of frontier capability growth across leading organizations. — Source [Epoch AI](https://epoch.ai/benchmarks/eci)

### DeepSeek as a “stress test”

[China’s DeepSeek provides a concrete stress test of these dynamics.](https://epoch.ai/gradient-updates/algorithmic-progress-likely-spurs-more-spending-on-compute-not-less) DeepSeek achieved [significant advances in training efficiency](https://www.bain.com/insights/deepseek-a-game-changer-in-ai-efficiency/), delivering strong benchmark performance at far lower compute cost than many observers expected. [These gains narrowed the apparent distance to leading U.S. closed models](https://www.iiss.org/publications/strategic-comments/2025/04/deepseeks-release-of-an-open-weight-frontier-ai-model/) relative to open models. The initial reaction to DeepSeek reflected a genuine surprise at how much performance could be extracted from a given amount of compute through better algorithms and training regimes.

Cost of the cheapest large language model per million tokens by performance level (MMLU), illustrating the rapid decline in inference costs across leading model providers. — Source [Bain](https://www.bain.com/insights/deepseek-a-game-changer-in-ai-efficiency/)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IIOx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4a03bcc5-2d79-47a8-a250-b7393e99672b_1440x810.png)

**But narrowing the gap is not the same as overtaking the frontier.** [At no point did DeepSeek clearly surpass the leading models](https://artificialanalysis.ai/models) from OpenAI, Google, or Anthropic across the broader capabilities that define frontier performance. More importantly, the frontier did not stand still. U.S.-aligned labs quickly [absorbed similar efficiency techniques](https://www.bain.com/insights/deepseek-a-game-changer-in-ai-efficiency/) and applied them to much larger compute budgets, continuing to advance overall capability. **What initially appeared as a disruption increasingly resembled a compression of timelines rather than a reordering of leadership.**

The conditions that enabled this compression are unlikely to persist. DeepSeek’s models were trained on [export-compliant Nvidia accelerators](https://www.cfr.org/articles/chinas-ai-chip-deficit-why-huawei-cant-catch-nvidia-and-us-export-controls-should-remain) that, while constrained relative to the most advanced U.S. systems, belonged to the same general hardware generation and supported similar software stacks. At the time, many U.S. frontier labs were still training at scale on A100- and early H100-class hardware, limiting the effective [system-level gap](https://www.exxactcorp.com/blog/hpc/comparing-nvidia-tensor-core-gpus). That proximity mattered. **Algorithmic efficiency is most powerful when underlying hardware generations and [system characteristics remain broadly comparable](https://ifp.org/the-b30a-decision/)**, allowing software optimizations to bridge marginal performance deficits.

That parity is now breaking down. As [Chris McGuire documents in his Council on Foreign Relations analysis](https://www.cfr.org/articles/new-ai-chip-export-policy-china-strategically-incoherent-and-unenforceable), **U.S. export controls are designed to prevent China from accessing successive generations of frontier AI hardware**. Even where recent **policy changes** have allowed limited access to H200-class accelerators, these permissions do not extend to [Blackwell](https://vast.ai/article/nvidia-h200-vs-b200-comparing-datacenter-grade-accelerators) and subsequent platforms. These upcoming platforms represent step changes in system-level performance, efficiency, and memory.

Comparison of projected best-in-class AI chip performance from Nvidia and Huawei, illustrating the growing divergence in frontier compute capability. — Source [CFR](https://www.cfr.org/articles/chinas-ai-chip-deficit-why-huawei-cant-catch-nvidia-and-us-export-controls-should-remain)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!dOcs!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbec287b0-e8fe-4025-b474-cfc07a68ac52_839x1341.png)

As frontier AI advances on roughly annual hardware cycles, exclusion from successive generations produces a compounding disadvantage. Algorithmic efficiency can reduce the compute required to reach a given capability, but it cannot substitute for missing **[system-level throughput](https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/blackwell-vs-hopper-gpu-architecture-comparison)** or the aggregate compute enabled by very large clusters running continuously on the newest hardware. Consequently, as hardware asymmetries widen, the scope for software-only leapfrogs narrows rather than expands, solidifying the lead of those who control the physical stack.

Relative contributions of compute scaling and algorithmic progress to AI performance, alongside the rapid growth of installed NVIDIA GPU compute capacity by generation. — Source [Epoch AI](https://epoch.ai/gradient-updates/algorithmic-progress-likely-spurs-more-spending-on-compute-not-less)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!r3gL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F390e471f-7276-4428-9c79-3478b464fc9c_1029x1248.png)

**DeepSeek thus illustrates both the power and the limits of algorithmic efficiency.** Efficiency can compress timelines, but it cannot overcome persistent asymmetries in access to frontier chips, energy, and capital. The initial shock faded because efficiency diffused and the frontier advanced again. Going forward, widening hardware gaps make a repeat of that moment less likely, not more.

### ⭐ Frontier chips as the gating constraint

At the frontier of artificial intelligence, access to advanced chips is shaped less by markets than by geopolitics. U.S. export controls on China, first tightened under the Trump administration and significantly expanded under the Biden administration in October 2022, were not designed simply to block access to Nvidia’s most advanced AI chips. **They were [designed to constrain China’s participation](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://www.bis.doc.gov/index.php/documents/about-bis/newsroom/press-releases/3158-2022-10-07-bis-unveils-new-export-controls-targeting-china-s-ability-to-acquire-computing-chips-and-manufacture-semiconductors/file) in the semiconductor ecosystem that makes frontier AI possible.**

As Chris Miller explains in *[Chip War](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Chip-War/Chris-Miller/9781982172008)*, semiconductor leadership depends on a tightly integrated global supply chain spanning chip design, fabrication, manufacturing equipment, software, and specialized labor. U.S. export controls target this entire system. They impose licensing requirements, often with a presumption of denial, not only on advanced chips but also on manufacturing tools, electronic design automation (EDA) software, technical data, and [even the ability of U.S. persons to support advanced Chinese semiconductor projects](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/ndaa-fy-2026-and-impending-changes-us-outbound-investment-security-program).

As this supply chain is global, the controls extend well beyond U.S. firms. Their effectiveness depends on allied cooperation, secured through a mix of legal leverage, diplomatic pressure, and shared strategic interests:

-   **The Netherlands** has fully adopted the U.S. “systems” approach in its export licensing. This has [effectively halted ASML from shipping](https://www.asml.com/en/news/press-releases/2026/q4-2025-financial-results) its most advanced immersion DUV lithography tools to China. Consequently, once existing orders are fulfilled, ASML forecasts its [revenue share from China](https://www.caixinglobal.com/2026-01-29/asml-expects-china-revenue-drop-following-backlog-fueled-surge-102409285.html) will fall from approximately 33% in 2025 to roughly 20% in 2026.
    

-   **Japan** continues to enforce strict semiconductor export controls through its [Ministry of Economy, Trade, and Industry (METI)](https://www.meti.go.jp/english/press/2025/1111_004.html) on 23 categories of advanced manufacturing equipment. This includes restrictions on critical deposition and etching tools from firms such as Tokyo Electron and Nikon, aimed at preventing their use in sub-14 nm logic chip manufacturing.
    

-   **South Korean** chipmakers Samsung and SK Hynix now must [seek explicit annual approval](https://www.eenewseurope.com/en/samsung-sk-hynix-china-operations-approved-by-us/) to ship equipment to their factories in China. This new [annual license framework](https://www.techzine.eu/news/privacy-compliance/137585/us-grants-samsung-and-sk-hynix-temporary-leeway-in-china/) limits their ability to upgrade those fabs and gives the United States decisive leverage over the production of advanced memory, including High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) essential for AI chips.
    

-   **Taiwan** has effectively blocked Chinese firms from the world’s most advanced fabrication. TSMC no longer manufactures 7 nm or more advanced chips for Chinese customers, following U.S. Department of Commerce directives to ensure that chips made for China remain at least [two generations behind](https://www.astutegroup.com/news/general/us-cracks-down-tsmc-halts-advanced-chip-shipments-to-china-sending-ripples-through-the-semiconductor-industry/) the global cutting edge.

### Technical chokepoints in lithography, fabrication, and design

At the center of this constraint sit extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines produced by [ASML](https://www.asml.com/en/products/euv-lithography-systems). These tools are essential for manufacturing chips at five nanometers and below. As of early 2026, each standard EUV system costs [over $200 million](https://www.tradingview.com/news/marketbeat:2b2592892094b:0-asml-earnings-clear-the-way-for-more-gains-in-2026/), while next-generation [High-NA EUV tools](https://investor.wedbush.com/wedbush/article/tokenring-2026-2-2-asml-and-the-high-na-euv-monopoly-the-path-to-14nm), required for sub-two-nanometer production, now approach [$400 million per unit](https://markets.financialcontent.com/thepilotnews/article/tokenring-2026-1-26-printing-the-2nm-era-asmls-350-million-high-na-euv-machines-hit-the-production-floor).

China has never received commercially viable EUV tools under the current export regime. Replicating them would require breakthroughs across optics, plasma physics, materials science, and software that took leading firms’ decades and tens of billions of dollars to achieve. Even advanced deep ultraviolet (DUV) tools depend on [restricted components, servicing, and software updates](https://www.asml.com/en/news/press-releases/2024/asml-expects-impact-of-updated-export-restrictions-to-fall-within-outlook-for-2025), further limiting China’s ability to maintain its existing machines and operate near the frontier.

Fabrication, the hyper-precise process of printing billions of microscopic transistors, presents another challenging barrier for China. Frontier AI chips are manufactured almost exclusively at TSMC using advanced logic processes that pack billions of transistors onto a single chip while maintaining extremely low defect rates. Recently TSMC has successfully initiated mass production of its [2nm (N2) node](https://beth-kindig.medium.com/the-future-of-ai-stocks-tsmc-commentary-suggests-ai-megatrend-6a5db48ea1ad), utilizing a record-breaking [$56 billion capital expenditure plan](https://markets.financialcontent.com/stocks/article/tokenring-2026-2-2-the-silicon-throne-tsmcs-record-56b-bet-on-the-future-of-artificial-intelligence) to meet the insatiable demand of AI hyperscalers’. Export controls prevent Chinese firms from taping out leading-edge designs at TSMC and restrict access to the equipment upgrades and process knowledge required to sustain high yields.

Despite heavy state investment, Chinese domestic fabs remain several generations behind. While China’s SMIC’s is nearing 5nm capabilities, its yields for advanced AI chips like the **[Ascend 910C](https://techblog.comsoc.org/2025/10/02/huawei-to-double-output-of-ascend-ai-chips-in-2026-openai-orders-hbm-chips-from-sk-hynix-samsung-for-stargate-uae-project/)** are reportedly hovering between **[60% and 70%](https://www.techinasia.com/news/smic-sees-2025-revenue-above-9b-on-strong-chip-demand)**. This is significantly lower than the **80–90% yield rates** typical of TSMC’s mature advanced nodes. Consequently, Chinese firms continue to struggle with yield, reliability, and cost, even when nominal node sizes appear close on paper.

Design software and human capital reinforce these constraints. Electronic design automation (EDA) tools from U.S. firms such as Synopsys and Cadence, which are used to design, simulate, and verify complex chips before fabrication, are [indispensable](https://schnitgercorp.com/2025/05/30/us-government-tells-vendors-to-stop-selling-eda-in-china/) for modern AI hardware. According to recent [2026 legal alerts](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/ndaa-fy-2026-and-impending-changes-us-outbound-investment-security-program), these software chokepoints are increasingly codified alongside broader outbound investment curbs. Furthermore, limits on [U.S. persons](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2022/10/us-government-issues-sweeping-controls-relating-to-semiconductors-advanced-computing-and-china) working on advanced Chinese semiconductor projects restrict access to tacit knowledge, that cannot be replaced by capital investment alone.

### ⭐ System-level advantage and capital lock-in

The effects of these controls are visible in market outcomes. Nvidia’s chief executive has stated that the company’s share of the Chinese AI accelerator market [fell from roughly 95% to effectively zero](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/technology/tech-news/upset-nvidia-ceo-jensen-huang-says-our-market-share-has-fallen-to-zero-from-95-in-/articleshow/124653495.cms) following export restrictions. Before the controls, China accounted for an estimated [around 25% of Nvidia’s data center revenue](https://www.manufacturingdive.com/news/nvidia-q1-2026-earnings-export-controls-china-trump/749261/).

The [Council on Foreign Relations](https://www.cfr.org/articles/chinas-ai-chip-deficit-why-huawei-cant-catch-nvidia-and-us-export-controls-should-remain) analysis explains why this matters for AI specifically. Frontier advantage today is determined less by individual chip specifications than by **system-level capability at scale**. Nvidia’s edge lies in a tightly integrated hardware, software, and networking stack that converts electricity into usable compute more efficiently than any alternative.

Crucially, Nvidia’s main competitors are either American, such as AMD, or based in allied countries like South Korea, where firms such as [Samsung and SK Hynix](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://www.reuters.com/technology/south-korea-sk-hynix-samsung-us-chip-equipment-waiver-2023-10-09/) operate within the same U.S.-aligned ecosystem. Firms and countries that can deploy capital earlier build larger clusters sooner, train larger models first, and justify still larger investments. When access to frontier accelerators diverges, [aggregate compute diverges with it](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Artificial-Intelligence-and-the-Great-Divergence-5.pdf). Capital does not merely fund frontier AI; it compounds and stabilizes leadership once chip and energy advantages exist.

This is why frontier chips function as a gating constraint. **Export controls do not block a single technology that can be reverse engineered; they [constrain an entire, evolving ecosystem](https://www.cfr.org/articles/new-ai-chip-export-policy-china-strategically-incoherent-and-unenforceable).** Each new hardware generation raises the bar again, forcing China to chase a frontier that continues to advance inside a network of allied firms and nations.

### ⭐ The centrality of geopolitics

These constraints reassert geopolitics as a decisive force in frontier AI. Participation at the frontier now depends on control over a small set of inputs that are unevenly distributed and politically mediated: access to successive generations of advanced chips, access to the upstream ecosystems that produce them, [access to large volumes of reliable energy](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/12/ai-energy-nexus-ai-future/), and access to capital markets capable of financing infrastructure at scale. How countries combine these inputs increasingly [determines how they participate in the global AI system.](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/dispatches/eight-ways-ai-will-shape-geopolitics-in-2026/)

This does not imply that innovation elsewhere ceases. Algorithmic breakthroughs, architectural changes, and new training techniques will continue to emerge globally, including in Europe, China, Israel, India, and across parts of the Global South. But innovation heavily interacts with physical and institutional constraints:

-   **Sovereign AI Initiatives:** Nations like the UAE and the UK are launching [sovereign compute reserves](https://geographical.co.uk/geopolitics/the-geopolitical-fragmentation-of-artificial-intelligence) to ensure national security and operational continuity.
    

-   **The Infrastructure Ceiling:** While [open-source models](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/653bc393d10f3500139a6ac5/future-risks-of-frontier-ai-annex-a.pdf) continue to improve, they often lag behind the frontier because they cannot be paired with the massive scale of hardware available to U.S.-aligned firms.
    

-   **Energy as a Chokepoint:** The AI-energy nexus has become so critical that [data center expenditures are forecast to reach $4 trillion by 2030](https://www.macfarlanes.com/insights/102mehd/the-macroeconomic-backdrop-to-private-capital-markets-february-2026/), straining power grids and making energy stability a core metric of national power.
    

### A system of differentiated participation

**A small number of countries relate to the AI stack end to end.** The [United States](https://www.csis.org/analysis/america-first-tech-stack-cannot-be-america-alone) sits at the center of this group, combining leadership in advanced chip design, AI software ecosystems, electronic design automation (EDA) tools, and [capital markets](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/industries/technology/semiconductor-and-beyond.html) with decisive influence over [export controls](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48642). Close allies control complementary layers of the stack: Taiwan anchors leading-edge fabrication through TSMC; the Netherlands controls lithography through ASML; Japan supplies critical manufacturing equipment; and South Korea dominates advanced memory (HBM). Together, these countries control the [full production chain](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/making-the-case-for-a-third-ai-technology-stack/) that defines the frontier and advances with each hardware generation.

**A larger group of countries does not control the full stack but possesses enough enabling inputs to exercise meaningful leverage.** These countries lack semiconductor chokepoints, yet compensate through different combinations of energy, capital, institutional capacity, and [geopolitical alignment](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/dispatches/eight-ways-ai-will-shape-geopolitics-in-2026/). Within this group, influence takes different forms depending on national context.

Some countries, such as Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates, use surplus energy and capital to secure access to [U.S.-aligned chips](https://www.commerce.gov/news/press-releases/2025/11/statement-uae-and-saudi-chip-exports) and attract frontier-scale AI infrastructure, including large data center projects like the 5GW G42 Stargate campus. Canada, Australia, and parts of Northern Europe occupy a similar position, pairing political alignment and strong institutions with energy resources. While these countries do not shape chip design, [they can train competitive national models](https://www.jll.com/en-us/insights/market-outlook/data-center-outlook), host large-scale training and inference workloads, and embed AI deeply into their domestic economies.

Other countries within the same group, including much of the [European Union](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/factpages/ai-continent-action-plan), Japan, Israel, and [India](https://pradumannarayanpal333.medium.com/indias-five-layer-ai-strategy-a-path-to-global-leadership-2abae068a3a7), exercise leverage through different channels. Despite more limited energy or infrastructure scale, they can still build strong national AI models, lead in high-value applications such as [cybersecurity and medical devices](https://www.gtlaw.com/en/insights/2025/1/published-articles/5-trends-to-watch-2025-ai-and-the-israeli-market), and shape global rules through regulation and standards, including frameworks like the [EU AI Act](https://www.artificial-intelligence-act.com/). Their influence flows less from hosting the largest compute clusters and [more from adoption, governance, and integration of AI](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/dispatches/eight-ways-ai-will-shape-geopolitics-in-2026/) across their economies, often relying on external providers for frontier-scale workloads.

**China, as discussed already, occupies a distinct and complex position.** It has scale, talent, capital, and energy, and it will remain a [major AI power](https://www.rand.org/pubs/perspectives/PEA4012-1.html). But it lacks secure access to successive generations of frontier chips and key upstream inputs such as EUV lithography and advanced fabrication services. Despite a recent [shift in U.S. policy](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/01/administration-policies-on-advanced-ai-chips-codified) allowing case-by-case exports of chips like the Nvidia H200, on its own that will not be enough to fundamentally change China’s predicament and lack of leverage in the AI supply chain. In this context, innovation can narrow gaps, but China is unlikely to replace a world-leading international supply chain all within its own borders in the foreseeable future..

**Most other countries relate to the AI stack primarily as adopters**. This includes much of Latin America, Africa, Central Asia, Southeast Asia, and parts of Eastern Europe. These countries may have talent and demand but often lack the [energy surplus](https://insights.techcabal.com/the-building-blocks-africa-needs-for-ai-adoption/), capital depth, or [geopolitical alignment](https://www.csis.org/analysis/open-door-ai-innovation-global-south-amid-geostrategic-competition) required to host frontier infrastructure. Their access to advanced AI is mediated through [foreign platforms](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Microsoft-AI-Diffusion-Report-2025-H2.pdf), cloud providers, and external capital, shaping where [value, learning, and influence](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/ai-risks-from-non-state-actors/) ultimately accumulate. Without the ability to build or host these models, they face a growing [digital divide](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Microsoft-AI-Diffusion-Report-2025-H2.pdf), as the strategic control and capital surpluses of AI companies are likely to remain [highly concentrated](https://www.csis.org/analysis/open-door-ai-innovation-global-south-amid-geostrategic-competition) in the countries that are already high-income.

**The result is not a simple hierarchy but a differentiated system of participation in the AI stack.** Some countries control the full stack, while others dominate critical inputs. Many exert influence by hosting large-scale infrastructure, shaping adoption, or setting rules and standards, and a broad set participates primarily downstream.

At this stage of AI development, power flows less from isolated technical breakthroughs and more from how effectively countries position themselves within the physical and institutional layers of the stack. Durable advantage depends on the ability to convert energy, capital, and geopolitical alignment into sustained access to compute, infrastructure, and markets over time.

# Two Loops: How China’s Open AI Strategy Reinforces Its Industrial Dominance

-   **Dual-Loop Strategy:** China’s approach relies on two reinforcing “loops”: a **digital loop**, where open-source AI models (like Alibaba’s Qwen) drive rapid global adoption and community iteration, and a **physical loop**, where these models are deployed across China’s massive manufacturing base to generate proprietary industrial data.
    
-   **Neutralizing U.S. Export Controls:** By focusing on high-efficiency open-source models that require less computing power, **China is narrowing the performance gap with Western AI.** This strategy allows them to innovate close to the technological frontier despite U.S. restrictions on advanced AI chips.
    
-   **Industrial Data Advantage:** China is prioritizing “Embodied AI”—integrating artificial intelligence into robotics and factories. This generates a specialized “feedback loop” of real-world operational data that proprietary U.S. models cannot easily replicate, potentially giving China a long-term lead in the physical application of AI.
    
-   **Global Ecosystem Dominance:** Chinese open-source models are becoming the global standard for startups and developers (accounting for an estimated 80% of U.S. AI startup usage). This widespread adoption allows Beijing to set technical norms and standards, **challenging the market dominance of “closed-loop” American companies like OpenAI and Google.**

### ⭐ AI Choke Points and Bottlenecks

Don’t be fooled however the Semiconductor boom, Datacenter rollout and so-called “AI revolution” and full of chokepoints and bottlenecks in the 2020s:

#### Further considerations:

-   **Advanced Packaging (CoWoS):** The industry’s “true bottleneck.” Even if silicon wafers are abundant, the specialized process of stacking memory and logic (Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate) is controlled almost entirely by **TSMC**, with lead times for AI GPUs stretching up to **52 weeks**.
    
-   **The HBM “Supercycle” Crunch:** High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM3E/HBM4) is in perpetual shortage. **SK hynix** and **Micron** have reportedly locked up 40% of global DRAM supply for AI hyperscalers, creating a massive price-hike for consumer electronics.
    
-   **Lithography Monopolies:** The reliance on **ASML’s** High-NA EUV machines remains an absolute bottleneck; without these $350M+ tools, training “Frontier” models (GPT-6 equivalents) is physically impossible.
    
-   **ASIC Fragmentation:** Major players (Google, Amazon, Meta) are moving away from Nvidia toward custom silicon (TPUs/Trainium). This is creating a “fragmented stack” where software optimized for one chip cannot easily run on another.
    
-   **The “Pax Silica” Blocs:** New U.S.-led alliances are forcing countries to choose between Western hardware stacks or Chinese open-source ecosystems, creating a “bipolar” AI world.
    
-   **Rare Earth Dominance (Venezuela & Africa):** China has secured new agreements for rare earth minerals (crucial for AI hardware magnets and sensors) in regions like Venezuela, sparking potential U.S. trade retaliation.
    
-   **Sovereign AI Infrastructure:** More nations (Saudi Arabia, UAE, France) are demanding “on-soil” data centers and proprietary models to avoid reliance on Silicon Valley, leading to a “protectionist” data environment.
    
-   **The “Talent Drain” Trap:** While the U.S. remains the top destination for top-tier AI researchers, tightening immigration and security clearances have created a “talent bottleneck” that slows down the deployment of new labs.
    

#### Datacenter Specific

-   **Grid Capacity:** 4–10 year wait times for high-voltage utility interconnections in Tier 1 markets.
    
-   **Power Hardware:** 2.5-year+ lead times for large transformers and high-capacity switchgear.
    
-   **Liquid Cooling:** Monopoly-level shortages of **Quick Disconnect (QDC)** couplings and **Coolant Distribution Units (CDUs)**.
    
-   **HBM Supply:** Near-total allocation of High Bandwidth Memory through 2026, stalling new GPU deployments.
    
-   **Advanced Packaging:** Throughput limits at **TSMC’s CoWoS** facilities delaying finished AI accelerator delivery.
    
-   **Specialized Labor:** Critical shortage of MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing) contractors for 100kW+ rack densities.
    
-   **Zoning & Permitting:** Increasing local moratoriums on “Gigascale” (500MW+) projects due to water and noise concerns.
    
-   **On-Site Generation:** Bottlenecks in the supply of **Small Modular Reactors (SMRs)** and industrial-scale natural gas turbines for “off-grid” centers.
