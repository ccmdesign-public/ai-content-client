# The Pentagon Just Blacklisted a US AI Company for Refusing to Enable Mass Surveillance

On February 26, 2026, the United States Department of Defense did something it usually reserves for North Korea and Iran. It labeled an American company a **supply-chain risk to national security.** The company refused to let the Pentagon use its AI for mass surveillance and fully autonomous weapons. That refusal became its crime. Twenty-four hours later, OpenAI signed the deal Anthropic rejected.

## The Designation That Shouldn’t Exist

The last time the US government used this designation, it was for a Chinese telecom company accused of spying. This time, it’s for a lab in San Francisco that said no.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth made it official on February 27. “Effective immediately, no contractor, supplier, or partner that does business with the United States military may conduct any commercial activity with Anthropic.” The company had six months to wind down its existing work.

President Trump amplified the message hours earlier on Truth Social. “I am directing EVERY Federal Agency in the United States Government to IMMEDIATELY CEASE all use of Anthropic’s technology. We don’t need it, we don’t want it, and will not do business with them again!”

The designation is typically reserved for companies in adversary nations, according to Reuters reporting (Straits Times, 2026). Chinese telecom giants. Russian entities. Not American AI startups with $200 million Pentagon contracts.

What did Anthropic actually do to deserve this?

## The Wrong Question

You might think this is a story about ethics versus the military. That frame is too simple.

This is actually a story about two different definitions of safety. Anthropic defines safety as preventing certain uses entirely. OpenAI defines safety as controlling the deployment architecture while letting the uses proceed. The Pentagon just picked a winner.

One definition cost a company $200 million. The other just won a classified contract. The difference between them is not what you think.

## The 24 Hours That Split the Industry

Last summer, Anthropic was the Pentagon’s favorite AI lab. The Defense Department awarded the company a $200 million ceiling contract (Straits Times/Reuters, 2026). Claude became the first frontier model deployed on classified networks via Amazon. According to BBC reporting (2026), the system was used in the operation to seize Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro.

Then the demands changed.

In January 2026, Pentagon officials told Anthropic they wanted access for **any lawful purpose.** No exceptions. No carve-outs. The company would need to remove its explicit safeguards against mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous lethal weapons.

Negotiations dragged on for weeks. On February 26, the Pentagon issued a final offer with a 5:01 PM deadline the next day.

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei rejected it publicly. “We cannot in good conscience accede to their request,” he told the BBC. The proposed language, he said, was “paired with legalese that would allow those safeguards to be disregarded at will.”

February 27 began with Trump’s Truth Social post. By mid-morning, Hegseth had announced the supply-chain risk designation. The company that had been first to work on classified networks was now blacklisted like a hostile state.

Anthropic vowed to sue.

That evening, OpenAI announced it had signed a deal with the Department of War to deploy its models on classified networks. The timing was precise. According to AFP reporting (Malay Mail, 2026), the agreement came “hours later,” a swift takeover of the contract Anthropic had just lost.

Hundreds of employees from OpenAI and Google DeepMind responded with an open letter. **We Will Not Be Divided,** they wrote. “We hope our leaders will put aside their differences and stand together to continue to refuse the Department of War’s current demands.”

Two companies. Two outcomes. They claimed the same red lines. How?

## The “All Lawful Purposes” Trap

The standard the Pentagon applies is not new. Every defense contractor must accept “all lawful purposes” as a baseline condition. xAI signed it. Google is negotiating it. Amazon operates under it.

But **lawful is a moving target.**

The Pentagon’s Undersecretary Emil Michael framed it as a matter of trust. “At some level, you have to trust your military to do the right thing,” he told the BBC.

Anthropic asked for more than trust. They wanted explicit contractual language excluding mass surveillance and autonomous weapons. They wanted protections that would hold even if trust eroded, even if laws changed, even if future administrations reinterpreted what “lawful” means.

The Pentagon refused. No exceptions. If it’s lawful, we can use it.

This is the trap. A contract that says “we follow current law” is a contract that says “we follow whatever the government decides is lawful next year.” Laws change. Directives change. The people writing the new rules are the same people asking for the data.

The pattern has precedent. In 2015, Apple refused to create a backdoor for the FBI to unlock the San Bernardino shooter’s iPhone. The government found another way. But Apple had consumer leverage. Anthropic had contracts.

When Anthropic held its ground, the Pentagon threatened the Defense Production Act, a Cold War law designed to mobilize private industry for national security emergencies. Legal experts called the threat “extremely flimsy,” according to a former DoD official quoted by the BBC. But the threat itself shifted the game.

Anthropic wasn’t being paranoid. They were asking for language that would hold even if trust eroded. The Pentagon said no.

## The Deal That Divides

OpenAI’s defense of its agreement arrived in a blog post on February 27. The company claimed three non-negotiable red lines: no mass domestic surveillance, no autonomous weapons, no high-stakes automated decisions. It emphasized **cloud-only deployment** as a technical safeguard that prevents models from running on edge devices like drones or weapons platforms. OpenAI personnel would remain in the loop.

The same red lines Anthropic fought for. In writing.

Let that land.

Then consider what Mustafa Tuncer, an international law expert at Turkey’s National Defense University, told me. “Mass surveillance is loosely defined under US domestic law,” he said. Contractual commitments that depend on current legal definitions are uncertain commitments.

![“Current law” is not a lockbox. The contract protects you until the definitions shift. Made By Author.]()

The same applies to autonomous weapons. “The concept of ‘human oversight’ remains broadly defined and subject to change under both US and international law,” Tuncer explained. A person pressing a button on a system that already selected the target is still, technically, human oversight. (The debate among international law scholars about what constitutes “meaningful human control” is fierce and unresolved.)

DoD Directive 3000.09 governs autonomous weapons today. Directives can be rewritten. Laws can be amended. The contract OpenAI signed references current law. **Current law is not a lockbox.**

The employees noticed. The “We Will Not Be Divided” letter was signed by hundreds of people inside OpenAI and Google DeepMind. They are watching their own companies. They are not convinced.

And there is one more twist. Despite the blacklist, BBC reporting suggests Claude is still being used in US and Israeli military operations in the Middle East. The ban may be theater. The safeguards may be theater. The question is what’s real.

## Two Definitions of Safety

You might be thinking: Isn’t this just a philosophical distinction? Don’t both companies end up in the same place?

No. They end up in very different places.

Let me name them explicitly.

**Anthropic’s model:** Safety means preventing certain categories of use entirely. No mass surveillance. No autonomous weapons. Period. The cost: you lose the contract. You lose influence. You get blacklisted like an adversary nation.

**OpenAI’s model:** Safety means controlling the technical architecture while allowing the uses to proceed. Cloud-only deployment. Humans in the loop. The same red lines in writing. The benefit: you keep the contract. You keep the seat at the table.

Both companies claim to protect you. Both cannot be right.

> *Anthropic’s safety model costs you the contract but might protect your rights. OpenAI’s safety model keeps the revenue but relies on definitions that can be rewritten. The Pentagon just picked a winner. History hasn’t.*

The Anthropic approach prevents the harm by preventing the use. But it cedes the ground. The Pentagon moves on to someone else.

The OpenAI approach keeps you in the room. But it relies on definitions that can shift, laws that can change, and “oversight” that can become a rubber stamp. The contract says “current law.” Current law is not static.

The market just picked a winner. OpenAI won. Anthropic lost. But winning and being right are not the same thing.

Li Yan, an analyst at China Modern International Relations Institute, drew a parallel to the Apple-FBI case. “When power can arbitrarily direct technology, when hegemony can easily tear through ethical boundaries, the greatest risk to AI’s future may not be the ‘unpredictable’ technology itself, but the irresponsible applications already before our eyes.”

He also noted the **black box effect.** As AI compute power doubles every four months, the gap between “we designed it safely” and “we know what it’s actually doing” widens. Human oversight becomes performative when the system is too complex to audit.

The question you must now hold: Which definition actually protects civilians when the pressure is on?

## What the Defense Would Say

The Pentagon has its own argument. Let me steel-man it.

**Counter 1:** “The Pentagon operates within the law. They have no interest in mass surveillance. At some level, you have to trust your military to do the right thing.” (Emil Michael, via BBC)

**Rebuttal:**

> Trust is not a safeguard. Laws change. The people writing the laws are the same people asking for the data. The military you trust today may not be the military you get tomorrow.

**Counter 2:** “OpenAI got the same red lines in writing. They actually won what Anthropic was fighting for.” (xAI signed the same terms, per Times Now/Axios)

**Rebuttal:** Winning in writing is not winning in practice. Tuncer’s testimony makes this clear. Definitions are fluid. “Current law” is a moving target. The contract protects you until it doesn’t.

**Counter 3:** “Cloud-only deployment physically prevents autonomous weapons. That’s a technical safeguard, not just words.” (OpenAI blog)

**Rebuttal:** Today. What about next year? What about when the Pentagon asks for an exception, or when “cloud-only” is redefined, or when the drone has a permanent link to the cloud? The employee letter suggests people inside aren’t convinced.

The people building the tech are skeptical. That should concern you.

## Three Things to Watch

Stop asking “is this company ethical?” Start asking **what did they actually sign?** The difference is the difference between marketing and truth.

**Watch 1 — The contract language.** OpenAI published theirs. That is a start. Now ask Google. Ask xAI. Ask every other lab negotiating with the Pentagon. Show us the red lines. Show us the loopholes. Show us where the definitions live.

**Watch 2 — The first exception.** Every contract has them. The Pentagon will eventually ask for something that pushes against the safeguards. The question is not whether the contract allows it. The question is whether the company says no.

**Watch 3 — The employees.** The “We Will Not Be Divided” letter was signed by hundreds of people inside these companies. They are the early warning system. If they start leaving, start talking, start organizing, pay attention.

The next time an AI company announces an “ethical” military deal, you now know what to look for. The fine print. The definitions. The employees. Everything else is marketing.
