# 80% of AI Users Quit in Three Weeks. There’s a Skill Nobody’s Teaching.

# 80% of AI Users Quit in Three Weeks. There’s a Skill Nobody’s Teaching.

[Reuven Gorsht](https://medium.com/@reuvengorsht?source=post_page---byline--e878316fc197---------------------------------------)

17 min read·3 days ago

\--

We’re using AI on two polar spectrums. On one end: people building AI advanced AI systems that run their lives. On the other: 80% of users abandon the technology within a month.

![]()

This week, every second post in my social feeds is about Clawdbot.

People are showing off their multi-agent Claude setups, their custom orchestration systems, their AI swarms that manage tasks while they sleep.

Last week, Claude CoWork that was all the hype. The previous week, it was Ralph Wiggum.

Somewhere in between, there’s a barrage of “the tool that will change everything.” or “This AI tool changed my life” videos and blogs being posted every minute.

The internet is littered with them. The “AI Tool of the week”. Workflow of the month. Another YouTube thumbnail promising 10X productivity if you just learn this one thing.

Even Andrej Karpathy, OpenAI co-founder, architect of modern deep learning, the guy who coined “vibe coding,” recently tweeted: **“I’ve never felt this much behind as a programmer.”**

![]()

It’s exhausting. There’s so much noise. And it’s not working.

Microsoft just released a study tracking 300,000 Copilot users. The findings are striking. Initial excitement peaks in the first three weeks, then craters. Hard.

> The data shows an 80/20 split. Roughly 20% become active users. The other 80%? Go dormant.

We’re operating AI on two polar spectrums. On one end: people building AI advanced AI systems that run their lives. On the other: 80% of users abandon the technology within a month.

There’s a small cohort operating in a different reality. Building agents, orchestrating swarms, consulting AI before every decision. And there’s a massive group who typed “*help me with this report*” into Copilot, got a generic answer, and concluded the whole thing was overhyped garbage.

**That’s not an adoption curve. That’s a massive chasm.**

There are billions pouring into the technology, the models, the chips, the data centers. All to face a leaky funnel, or benefit 1% of early adopters who change tool stacks more frequently than their socks?

**Here’s what most companies get wrong: they treat** [**AI adoption**](https://reuvengorsht.substack.com/p/tony-robbins-says-we-have-3-years) **like software adoption.** Roll out licenses. Run a training session or two. Send a few tip emails. Wait for the hockey stick.

But AI isn’t like software. That playbook doesn’t work anymore, and the early data we have on AI adoption and rates of project failures serves as an indicator that something is amiss.

When you buy software, say, a CRM, the features you need and those the solution delivers are well defined. You configure your business processes into the software, your users learn where the buttons are, they follow the workflow, they’re eventually proficient. The tool does what it says on the box.

**AI is not defined software. It has unlimited capabilities.**

The same tool that rewrites emails can also run your entire content pipeline, negotiate with vendors, coach you through difficult conversations, or even re-architect your software system in less than 24 hours.

> **The ceiling isn’t defined by the product. It’s defined by your ability to drive it.**

And that’s the problem. Both the technology and it’s infinite use cases don’t hold still.

There is no “user manual” or “best practices” for AI. The use cases are infinite. And the skill required to unlock value isn’t about “learning the tool.” In fact, it’s an entire skill set altogether.

It’s because of this gap, that the chasm between “just getting started” and “years ahead” isn’t closing. It’s widening.

The advanced users and the beginners are both accounted for. But the middle? The place where AI actually transforms how you work?

**Almost empty.**

That’s the gap. Not early adopters vs. laggards. Not techies vs. normies.

**The missing middle.**

## The Three-Week Crater

Here’s what happens 80% of the time.

Someone gets access to Copilot or ChatGPT.

They’re curious. Maybe even excited.

They type “***help me with this report***” or “***rewrite this email***”, get a generic or confidently wrong answer, and conclude that doing the work manually is faster.

They’re not wrong about their experience. They’re wrong about what’s possible.

This is what we can call the “three-week crater.”

The initial excitement peaks, then drops off a cliff. Most users never recover. They check the box, move on, occasionally wonder what all the fuss was about.

> **This isn’t a software failure. It’s a literacy failure.**

You may read the previous paragraph and say “well, maybe my people are overwhelmed and scared of AI”. There’s truth to that.

On other side of the spectrum: where the most advanced users who are playing with next-level AI capabilities, can’t even keep up. We’re talking about people who are the bleeding edge of the technology and have been exposed to it for years.

At the risk of blaming the technology again, the truth is, technology is moving faster than any human can adopt it. Beginners and experts are both struggling. Just for different reasons. However, again, **this is not a technology problem.**

The answer isn’t “try harder.” The answer is: **we’re thinking about it wrong.**

AI is not just a tool. You don’t learn it the way you learn Excel. There’s no feature set to memorize, no certification to complete. The skills that matter aren’t technical. They’re cognitive. **And almost no one is teaching them.**

## The Missing Middle

Most organizations think about AI adoption in two modes. And they’re both wrong.

**The Basics**

“Here’s how to open ChatGPT. Here’s where the prompt box is. Here are some example prompts.”

This is what most corporate training looks like. ***It treats AI like software to learn, not a capability to develop.***

An hour of tips and tricks, then back to your desk to figure out the rest. You already know what happens next.

**The Frontier**

APIs, fine-tuning, agent architectures, orchestration frameworks. Multi-agent swarms. MCP, LSP, hooks, skills, subagents.

This is what the power users and advanced technologists are doing. It’s powerful, but it requires technical fluency most knowledge workers don’t have and don’t need.

**The Missing Middle**

This is where AI transforms from novelty to infrastructure. It’s not about prompting tricks or technical architecture. It’s about judgment.

Where does AI fit in my workflow? When can I trust the output? How do I manage this thing like I’d manage a capable but inexperienced team member?

Here’s the insight that reframes everything:

> **AI is not a tool skill. It’s a management skill**

(hat tip to Nate B Jones here for crystallizing this).

You wouldn’t throw a new hire into a client meeting on day one and say “handle it.” You’d prep them, set expectations, debrief after. In other words, you’d manage them.

The 20% who survive the adoption crater? They figured this out intuitively. They stopped treating AI as a search engine and started treating it as a direct report.

The 80% who cratered? They were never taught or knew this was an option. They were left in the lobby while others got into the elevator.

## The Six Missing Middle Skills

Here’s some good news in helping your organization cross the AI adoption chasm. We have to start by understanding and reframing the core skills for AI adoption.

Instead of leaving things at AI 101, we must consciously work towards building and reenforcing new judgment competencies. There are Six competencies that no one teaches.

Let me show you the difference through two people: Mike and Sarah. Same role, same tools, same tasks: prepare a competitive analysis for an upcoming client pitch.

![]()

## 1\. Setting the Stage

**Mike’s approach:** Pastes the competitor’s website URL into ChatGPT. “Analyze this company.”

**Sarah’s approach:** Provides three paragraphs of context. Who the client is, what they care about, what angle matters for this pitch. Includes two examples of competitive analyses she’s done before that hit the mark.

***The difference:*** Mike gets a generic overview he could have found on Google. Sarah gets a focused analysis that sounds like it came from someone who really understands the account.

## 2\. Knowing What to Trust

**Mike’s approach:** The output of the analysis looks professional, so he copies it into his deck. Big mistake!

**Sarah’s approach:** She reads it twice. First pass: Does this matter for a high-stakes client pitch, or is it just filler?
Second pass: Do these claims hold together, or is there a hallucination buried in an otherwise solid paragraph?

***The difference:*** Mike presents a “fact” about the competitor’s market share that turns out to be fabricated. Sarah caught a similar error and verified before it reached the client.

## 3\. Breaking Down the Work

**Mike’s approach:** “Gives company URL, prompts: write me a full competitive analysis.”

**Sarah’s approach:** “First, list this competitor’s three biggest weaknesses based on their product reviews. Then I’ll tell you which ones to expand on.” She knows which parts AI should handle and which parts need her judgment.

***The difference:*** Mike gets an overwhelming 3,000-word document that’s 80% irrelevant. Sarah builds the analysis in pieces, steering as she goes, and gets a quality, usable report, focused on differentiators.

## 4\. Treating the First Draft as a Starting Point

**Mike’s approach:** The first output is either “good enough” or “this is useless.”

**Sarah’s approach:** The first output is the 70% start. She runs a logic pass. Then a tone pass. Then a “would I actually say this in front of the client?” pass. Three rounds, five minutes each.

***The difference:*** Mike’s slides feel AI-generated, they’re bloated and full of inaccuracies. Sarah’s feel like her.

## 5\. Building It Into the Process

**Mike’s approach:** “I should really use AI for this… but I’m already behind. I’ll figure it out next time.”

**Sarah’s approach:** Prep for all customer calls include a competitive analysis that is done with the help of AI. Not as an extra step. As step one. Every time. Sarah not only saves an hour per client, but her conversations resonate and convert into large contracts.

***The difference:*** Mike sees AI as a side project. Sarah sees it as how part of the work gets done.

## 6\. Knowing When to Stop

**Mike’s approach:** “AI is amazing at everything now.” He uses it to draft a new pricing strategy that he’ll present to senior management.

**Sarah’s approach:** She knows AI is great at synthesis and pattern recognition. She knows it’s unreliable for anything requiring current data or sensitive client history. She stops before she crosses the line and uses AI to format her strategy report and make it more polished.

***The difference:*** Mike’s deck includes a confident recommendation based on outdated competitor pricing. He lost credibility with senior management who spotted the mistake right away. Sarah’s report is accurate, polished and presented well.

**These aren’t prompting tricks. They’re management skills applied to a new kind of collaborator.**

**Mike is using AI as a tool. Sarah is “managing” AI like a new co-worker.**

Mike isn’t stupid. He just never learned there was another way.

## Two Modes: Centaur and Cyborg

Research from BCG and Harvard identifies [two distinct patterns](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/centaurs-and-cyborgs-on-the-jagged) for working with AI at the Missing Middle level.

The skill isn’t picking one. It’s knowing when to switch.

![]()

## Centaur Mode

Humans and AI have clear, separate roles. You do X, AI does Y, handoffs are explicit.

For example, Harvey.ai can confidently handle a first draft for a legal contract. It brings in the relevant clauses, structures the document, and even completes the formatting. Once complete, the lawyer reviews the output, ensures everything is correct, makes some adjustments, and sends it to the client for review.

**Best for:** High-stakes work requiring accountability. Legal review, medical decisions, financial analysis. Anything where you need a clear audit trail of who decided what.

**The Missing Middle skill:** Knowing when to maintain total human control over the final output. Most centaur cases require the six management skills and the [relevant work experience](https://reuvengorsht.substack.com/p/a-fool-with-a-tool-is-still-a-fool). For example, if the lawyer is very junior, they will not be able to spot mistakes and make adjustments.

## Cyborg Mode

Humans and AI weave together continuously. You’re thinking and prompting and editing and prompting again, all in real-time flow.

**Best for:** Creative and iterative work. Drafting, brainstorming, building, designing. Anything where refinement is the goal.

**The Missing Middle skill:** Integrating AI into your thought process without losing your own judgment. At this point, AI becomes your thought partner — creating with you, helping you brainstorm and strategize.

Most people default to one mode without realizing they’re choosing. They’re either always hands-off (Centaur by accident) or always entangled (Cyborg by accident).

The Missing Middle move is conscious switching:

> “**This task needs Centaur mode because the stakes are high. This one needs Cyborg because I’m iterating toward something.**”

## The Catch 22 Time Bomb

**The Time Bomb: How We’re Breaking the Talent Pipeline**

There’s a slower crisis building underneath us.

If we’re saying we need expertise to effectively “manage” AI, think about how expertise actually develops in organizations.

Juniors don’t start with judgment. They build it.

By doing the unglamorous work: research, first drafts, data gathering, the grunt work that teaches you how a domain actually works.

A first-year analyst doesn’t understand the business because someone explained it. They understand it because they spent six months buried in the details, pattern-matching, making mistakes, getting corrected.

**This work is now being delegated to AI.**

On the surface, this looks like efficiency. Why have a junior spend four hours on research when AI can do it in ten minutes? Let them focus on higher-value work.

**But here’s the problem: that “low-value” work is where judgment comes from.**

The senior people who are great at AI collaboration? They’re great because they have decades of domain knowledge. They know what good looks like. They can smell when something’s off. They earned that intuition by doing the work that juniors are now skipping.

## **The Cyborg Apprenticeship**

Research from BCG and Harvard describes two ways of working with AI. Centaur mode: human and AI take turns, clear handoffs, separate responsibilities. Cyborg mode: human and AI weave together continuously, thinking and prompting in real-time flow.

Most organizations default to Centaur mode for juniors. AI does the research, junior reviews it. Clean separation. Efficient.

But Cyborg mode is where judgment develops. Junior does the research WITH AI. They see what it gets right, what it misses, where it hallucinates. They develop the smell test. They learn the domain by wrestling with it, not by reviewing a summary.

> **Without a Missing Middle structure, we’re producing a generation of professionals who can prompt AI but can’t evaluate what comes back. Who can generate outputs but can’t recognize errors. Who can move fast but don’t know where the edges are.**

This won’t show up in quarterly metrics. It won’t be obvious next year.

It’ll show up in five years when you need senior judgment and no one has it. When you need someone to catch the AI’s mistake, and no one in the room has the domain expertise to see it.

**The Opportunity**

The good news: this isn’t inevitable.

The Missing Middle isn’t just a gap to diagnose.

It’s a gap to fill. The organizations that figure out how to build judgment alongside AI will have a massive advantage.

The question is: what does that actually look like?

## The Chasm That Keeps Moving

Geoffrey Moore’s “Crossing the Chasm” has been the playbook for technology adoption for thirty years.

Early adopters first. Then a gap where most technologies die. Then the mainstream crosses over. Then the laggards follow. Eventually everyone ends up in roughly the same place.

The iPhone in 2007 was clunky and expensive. By 2012, your parents had one. The gap closed.

**But that model assumes the technology stands still while adoption catches up.**

What happens when the technology evolves faster than the adoption cycle?

By the time the [mainstream](https://medium.com/generative-ai/every-technology-required-your-choice-ai-doesnt-818ac3f8d56d) reaches where the frontier was, the frontier has moved somewhere else entirely.

> **The chasm isn’t a gap you cross once. It’s a treadmill. You’re running just to stay behind.**

**This is why the Missing Middle matters so much.**

The frontier will always be ahead. That’s fine. That’s how frontiers work. Someone has to explore the edges.

But the Missing Middle skills are stable. Setting the stage. Knowing what to trust. Breaking down the work. Treating first drafts as starting points. Building AI into the process. Knowing when to stop.

These translate across tools. They work whether you’re using ChatGPT or Claude or whatever comes next. They’re not about catching the frontier. They’re about building judgment that compounds.

> **The path isn’t catching up to where the frontier is. It’s mastering the middle so you can keep climbing no matter how fast the rungs appear.**

## Building the Judgment Layer

The organizations that win in the next five years won’t be the ones with the most AI licenses, vanity metrics, or adopted projects. They’ll be the ones that build the judgment layer across their organizational culture.

Think of it as AI muscle memory. Not a one-time training. Not a rollout. A permanent part of your organization’s DNA.

The instinct to know when AI helps, when it hurts, and how to get value from it consistently.

The evidence is already in. Most AI projects fail. A 2024 RAND study found that 80% of enterprise AI initiatives never make it to production. Gartner reported that through 2025, at least 30% of generative AI projects would be abandoned after proof of concept.

The pattern is consistent: organizations invest in tools, skip the capability building, and watch adoption crater.

Here’s the playbook for doing it differently:

**Start With the Right People**

Most organizations make the same mistake: they hand AI adoption to their most technical employees. Wrong choice.

**The skills that matter at the Missing Middle aren’t technical. They’re managerial.**

Giving clear instructions. Reviewing output critically. Iterating toward quality. Breaking complex work into chunks. Knowing when to trust and when to verify.

Your best AI champions aren’t your best engineers. They’re your best managers.

Find them. Give them time. Let them lead.

**Stop Thinking of AI as a Tool.**

This is the mindset shift that separates the 20% from the 80%.

AI isn’t software you learn.

It’s a collaborator you manage.

**The skills that unlock value aren’t technical. They’re the same skills that make someone good at leading a team: clarity, judgment, iteration, knowing when to delegate and when to stay hands-on.**

Those who have these skills will do well with AI almost immediately. Those who don’t need to develop them. That’s the real training gap. Not prompting. Management.

The six Missing Middle skills map directly to management competencies:

1.  **Setting the stage.** Provide background, constraints, and examples. The same way you’d brief a new team member before handing them a project.
2.  **Knowing what to trust.** Detect confident-but-wrong errors. Build the smell test based on stakes. High-stakes work gets verified. Low-stakes work gets spot-checked.
3.  **Breaking down the work.** Decompose complex workflows into chunks. No one hands a new hire a massive project and says “handle it.” Same principle.
4.  **Treating first drafts as starting points.** Move from 70% to 95% through structured passes. The first output is never the final output.
5.  **Building it into the process.** Move from “I’ll try AI later” to “this is how we do this task now.” Embed it into standard operating procedures.
6.  **Knowing when to stop.** Identify where AI capability drops off for your specific domain. The frontier is jagged. Map it before you cross it.
7.  **Create Space for Discovery**

You don’t know your best use cases yet. Neither do your employees.

Form lightweight AI Labs. Small teams of non-technical power users who experiment with workflows. Their job isn’t to build infrastructure. It’s to prove value. Fast iterations. Low stakes. Real work.

Run systematic discovery across departments. The Trek Bicycle approach: interview every team about how AI might improve their specific work. Don’t rely on top-down directives. The best use cases are hiding in the middle of the org chart.

**Make Learning Visible**

The jagged frontier is different for every domain. The only way to map it is through shared experience.

Create a mechanism for employees to report failures. Not to punish, but to learn. When AI fails at a task, document it. Share it. Help the whole organization understand where the boundaries are so others don’t discover them the hard way.

Make success visible too. Run low-stakes competitions. “What workflow did you improve this week?” People adopt tools when they see peers winning with them. Social proof beats mandates.

**Protect the Pipeline**

Your senior people are good at AI because they spent years building domain expertise.

They know what good looks like. They can smell when something’s off.

> Your juniors are building that expertise right now. Or they’re not.

AI now does the grunt work that used to teach judgment: research, first drafts, data gathering. If you let AI replace that learning entirely, you’ll face a judgment deficit in five years. Senior roles with no one qualified to fill them. Mistakes no one in the room can catch.

Rebuild the apprentice model. Juniors work with AI, not just receive AI output. They see what AI gets right and wrong. They build the smell test through experience, not abstraction.

**Measure What Matters**

Stop tracking license utilization. That metric tells you nothing about capability.

Track judgment development:

-   How many employees can identify when output needs verification?
-   How many know which tasks fit which work pattern?
-   How many have integrated AI into a repeatable workflow?
-   Where are the documented failure cases, and what did the organization learn?
-   The goal isn’t AI activity. It’s AI fluency. Measure accordingly.

**The Opportunity**

Most organizations are stuck in the Basics, waiting for the technology to mature. The frontier is moving faster than they can follow. The Basics will never catch up.

But the Missing Middle is stable ground.

Setting the stage works whether you’re using ChatGPT, Claude, or whatever comes next. Knowing what to trust transfers. Breaking down the work transfers. These are management competencies, not technical ones.

The organizations that build this layer now will compound their advantage. The magic isn’t in the machine. It’s in the judgment layer you build around it.

## The Close

I wrote a piece about the AI Ladder, [a learning framework for AI](https://medium.com/generative-ai/the-ai-learning-framework-that-took-me-from-stuck-to-fluent-498313b39dfd), a few days ago because I was overwhelmed. Ten levels, three zones, one rung at a time. A framework to make the climb feel possible.

But watching the discourse this week, Clawdbot everywhere, Karpathy admitting he feels behind, 80% of Copilot licenses gathering dust, I realize the ladder was only part of the story.

**The issue isn’t that people are at different levels.**

**It’s that we’re teaching the wrong levels entirely.**

There’s a cohort building systems I barely understand, years ahead of everyone else. There’s a cohort still stuck at “rewrite this email,” wondering what the fuss is about.

And in between? Almost nothing.

The Missing Middle is where AI transforms from novelty to infrastructure. From tool to collaborator. From hype to habit.

It’s not about prompting. **It’s about judgment.**

It’s not about tools. **It’s about management.**

It’s not about catching the frontier. **It’s about mastering the middle.**

If you’re in the 80% who cratered, you weren’t doing it wrong. You were never taught the Missing Middle skills.

Now you know they exist.

Time to learn them.
